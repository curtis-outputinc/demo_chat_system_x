# Immigration pathways and common situations

For every situation below, the assistant explains in general terms what the
pathway typically involves and what a lawyer reviews. It never tells a
visitor whether they qualify, predict outcomes, or walks them through a
specific application.

## Permanent residence (Canada)

Common pathways include Express Entry, Provincial Nominee Programs, the
Canadian Experience Class, the Federal Skilled Worker stream, and the
Atlantic Immigration Program. Each has its own eligibility framework that
considers factors like work experience, language ability, education, age,
and connections to a specific province. Express Entry uses a points-based
system called the Comprehensive Ranking System, or CRS, which assigns a
score based on those factors and others such as a job offer or a provincial
nomination. Higher scores increase the chance of receiving an invitation to
apply.

Possible ways to improve a CRS score may include stronger language test
results, additional skilled work experience, more education, a provincial
nomination, or improving a spouse or partner's factors on a joint profile.
The right strategy depends on the specific profile, and a lawyer can review
the situation.

In some cases a person already in Canada can apply for permanent residence
from inside the country. Whether an inside-Canada process is available
depends on the person's current status and the pathway they are applying
under.

## Permanent residence (United States)

The firm helps with US immigration as well. Common areas include
employment-based and family-based green cards, adjustment of status,
consular processing, and related applications. Pathways and eligibility
factors differ from Canada and depend on the visitor's situation; a lawyer
can review what applies.

## Family sponsorship

Canadian citizens and permanent residents may be able to sponsor a spouse,
common-law partner, conjugal partner, or dependent child if they meet the
requirements. The sponsor must meet income and status requirements and
agree to financially support the person being sponsored.

Inland sponsorship is generally used for spouses or partners already living
in Canada with valid status. Outland sponsorship is generally processed
through the immigration authority and is often used when the person being
sponsored is outside Canada. Each option has practical differences.

Canada also has parent and grandparent sponsorship options, though intake
is controlled by the government and availability can be limited. The super
visa allows parents and grandparents of Canadian citizens and permanent
residents to visit for extended periods, with proof of private medical
insurance and an invitation letter from a child or grandchild in Canada.

US family-based immigration covers spouses, parents, children, and certain
relatives of US citizens and lawful permanent residents. Categories,
processing routes, and waiting times differ depending on the relationship
and the applicant's situation.

Changes in a relationship can have significant implications for a
sponsorship application in progress. The earlier a lawyer reviews the
situation, the more options may be available.

## Work permits

In Canada, some work permits require a Labour Market Impact Assessment, or
LMIA, which is a document showing the employer looked for qualified
Canadian workers first. Other work permits are LMIA-exempt, including
permits under CUSMA/USMCA, intra-company transfers, and certain
international agreements. Post-Graduation Work Permits, or PGWPs, allow
eligible graduates of designated Canadian learning institutions to work in
Canada after completing their studies; length generally depends on the
length of the study program. Bridging open work permits can help applicants
continue working while a permanent residence application is in process.

US work-related categories include H-1B, L-1, O-1, TN under USMCA, and
others, each with its own requirements and process. A lawyer can review
which route may apply.

If a work permit is expiring soon, acting early matters. Depending on the
situation, an extension or a different status may be possible, and there
are rules about continuing to work while an application is being processed.

## Study permits

International students may be able to study in Canada by obtaining a study
permit. Eligibility depends on acceptance from a designated learning
institution, proof of financial support, and other factors. Study permits
can be refused for reasons such as concerns about financial support, the
study plan, ties to the home country, prior immigration history, or whether
the officer believes the applicant will leave Canada when required. A
lawyer can review the refusal letter or the application before submission.

The United States has its own student visa categories with their own
requirements; a lawyer can explain what applies.

Study-to-work pathways such as the PGWP and certain US options can be part
of a longer-term plan toward permanent residence.

## Visitor visas and travel authorizations

These cover temporary visits to Canada or the United States. Eligibility
depends on factors including the visitor's ties to their home country, the
purpose of the visit, and prior immigration history. Refusals are common
and a lawyer can review the reasons before reapplying.

## Refugee claims and humanitarian applications

People in Canada who fear returning to their home country because of
persecution, a threat to life, or a risk of torture may be able to make a
refugee claim before the Immigration and Refugee Board. Eligibility and
process depend on the individual's situation and immigration history.

A humanitarian and compassionate application is a way for people who do not
qualify under standard categories to request permanent residence based on
factors such as hardship of leaving Canada, establishment in Canada, the
best interests of any children involved, or other compelling circumstances.

In the United States, asylum and related protections involve their own
framework and deadlines.

These matters are highly fact-sensitive and often time-sensitive. A lawyer
should review the situation as early as possible.

## Refusals, appeals, and judicial reviews

A refusal is not necessarily the end of the options. Depending on the type
of application and the reason for the refusal, available steps may include
requesting reconsideration, filing an appeal, applying for a judicial
review at the Federal Court of Canada, or submitting a stronger new
application. Each has its own deadlines, and acting quickly is important.

A procedural fairness letter means the immigration authority has identified
a concern with an application and is giving the applicant a chance to
respond before a final decision. This is an important and time-sensitive
opportunity.

## Citizenship

To apply for Canadian citizenship, a person generally needs to have been a
permanent resident of Canada for a certain number of days within a specific
period, meet language requirements, file taxes if required, pass a
knowledge test, and take the Oath of Citizenship. The calculation of days
has specific rules; some days count fully and others partially depending on
status at the time. An error in calculating days can delay an application.

US naturalization has its own residency, physical presence, language, and
civics requirements. A lawyer can review the timeline and ensure the
application is complete.

## Inadmissibility and waivers

A person may be inadmissible to Canada or the United States because of a
criminal record, a medical condition that meets certain criteria, or a
finding of misrepresentation, among other reasons. Available paths in some
cases include deemed rehabilitation, criminal rehabilitation, a Temporary
Resident Permit in Canada, or waivers in the US. A lawyer reviews the
specific record and advises on options.

## Other situations

Removal orders, deportation hearings, detention, procedural fairness
letters with deadlines, and refugee hearing dates are treated as urgent.
