# What happens: process and timelines

## The free consultation

A consultation usually means reviewing the basic facts of the visitor's
situation, explaining the options that may apply, answering questions, and
deciding together whether the firm can help. There is no obligation to hire
the firm afterward.

## After someone reaches out

When a visitor submits their contact details through the chat or books a
call, a member of the firm's intake team follows up to schedule the free
initial consultation. The team typically reaches out within one business
day. If the situation is urgent, that note is flagged when the lead is
passed to the team so the right calls happen first.

The information collected through the chat is limited: first name, last
name, best contact phone number, email address, preferred contact method,
and a brief general description of the matter. The chat does not collect
sensitive immigration details, document numbers, or case specifics; those
are shared directly with the lawyer in the confidential consultation.

The visitor never has to repeat their situation to the intake coordinator.
The note that goes to the team includes enough general context that the
follow-up call begins from where the chat ended.

## What the consultation looks like

The consultation is confidential. The visitor explains the general
situation, the lawyer asks the questions needed to understand what may
apply, and the lawyer explains the options. Where useful, the lawyer
outlines the steps that would follow, the documents the firm would need to
review, and the rough scope of work.

## How a matter generally moves

If the firm takes a matter on, the lawyer handles the legal work:
identifying the right pathway, preparing and submitting the application,
gathering supporting documentation, responding to government correspondence,
and representing the client in any hearings or appeals. The client provides
information and attends appointments; the firm manages the legal process.

An application typically moves through stages such as initial review of the
facts and documents, preparing the application package, submission,
acknowledgment from the authority, possible requests for additional
information or interviews, and a final decision. Where a refusal occurs, the
next stage may be a response, an appeal, or a judicial review depending on
the matter and the reasons.

## Plain-language terms the assistant can explain

A procedural fairness letter is a notice from the immigration authority
identifying a concern with an application and giving the applicant a chance
to respond before a final decision. A judicial review is a Federal Court
process that examines whether an immigration decision was made correctly
and fairly; it is not a new hearing of the case. An appeal is a separate
process available for certain decisions in front of a tribunal. A removal
order is a formal direction that a person must leave the country. The CRS
score is the points score used in Canadian Express Entry. The LMIA is a
document some Canadian employers need before hiring a foreign worker.

When any of these are mentioned, the assistant gives a plain explanation
and offers to connect the visitor with the firm to discuss what applies to
them.

## How long it takes

Processing times vary widely depending on the type of application, the
country, current government workload, and individual circumstances. The
assistant does not give a specific time estimate for any visitor's matter.
The firm can give a better sense of timing after reviewing the details.

## What happens after a successful application

Each pathway has its own conclusion: an approval letter, a confirmation of
permanent residence, a permit, a citizenship ceremony, or a successful
hearing outcome. The firm explains the steps that follow and any
obligations that come with the new status.
