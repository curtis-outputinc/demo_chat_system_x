# Construction Loans and Progress Draw Mortgages: Frequently Asked Questions

Plain-language Q&A reference for the kinds of questions borrowers ask
about construction financing and progress draw mortgages. Synthesized
from two AI-generated sources (Gemini and ChatGPT), deduplicated
semantically, plus 50 additional borrower-facing questions added.

## How the agent should use this file

These Q&As give the agent background context for construction
conversations. Typical ranges noted (20-30% equity for owner-occupied,
30-40% for investment builds, 650-680 minimum credit, 5-15%
contingency reserve, $200-$400 site inspection fees, 5-7 business
days for draw release, 9-12 month standard build timeline, $400-$600
appraisal, 10-14% private rates, 3-5% private setup fees) are general
market context, not quotes. Real numbers vary by lender, project,
builder, and date.

The Ontario Construction Act 10% statutory holdback and Tarion
warranty requirements are jurisdiction-specific to Ontario. Other
provinces have different rules.

## Category 1: Fundamentals

1. What is a construction loan?
A short-term mortgage used to finance the building of a custom home,
a major tear-down/rebuild, or an investment project. Instead of
advancing a lump sum upfront, the lender releases funds in stages
(draws) as construction milestones are met.

2. What is a progress draw mortgage?
The same as a construction loan in practical terms. The "progress
draw" name emphasizes the staged disbursement: funds are released as
the build progresses through pre-defined milestones, each verified by
an inspection.

3. How is a construction loan different from a regular mortgage?
A regular mortgage funds a completed property all at once based on
current market value. A construction mortgage funds an unfinished
project incrementally over 6-18 months, requires inspections at each
stage, and is evaluated against the property's future
"as-completed" appraised value.

4. What are the two main types of construction mortgages?
Contractor-Built: you hire a licensed general contractor to manage
the whole project. Self-Built (owner-builder): you act as your own
general contractor and hire individual sub-trades directly. Bank
financing is much easier for contractor-built than for self-built.

5. How do interest payments work during construction?
You pay interest only on the amount advanced to date, not on the
full approved loan size. Payments are interest-only during the build
period, which keeps carrying costs lower until completion.

6. What is a draw mortgage?
Another name for a construction loan. Funds release in draws based
on completed construction milestones rather than all at once.

7. What is a completion mortgage?
A mortgage that funds when the home is fully built or near
completion. Different from a draw mortgage because no funds advance
during construction; the lender funds at the end based on the
completed property value.

8. What's the difference between a draw mortgage and a completion mortgage?
Draw mortgage advances money during the build at each milestone.
Completion mortgage advances funds once the property is complete or
ready for possession. Draw mortgages cover both land and
construction costs; completion mortgages assume the borrower funded
the build through other means.

9. What is a "convertible" or "end" mortgage?
A seamless structure where your short-term interest-only construction
loan automatically rolls into a traditional amortizing fixed or
variable mortgage once 100% completion is verified.

10. What is construction-to-permanent financing?
Same as a convertible mortgage. Starts as construction financing,
later converts to a regular amortizing mortgage after the home is
complete. Reduces the need to arrange a separate take-out mortgage
later.

11. What is a take-out mortgage?
The permanent traditional mortgage that pays out the short-term
construction loan after the build is complete. Many construction
lenders (especially private and alternative) require take-out
mortgage pre-approval before advancing the first draw.

12. Why does a take-out mortgage matter?
Because construction loans are short-term, the borrower needs an
exit. Take-out pre-approval proves there's a permanent mortgage
ready to replace the construction loan at completion. Without it,
the borrower could be stuck with maturing construction debt and
nowhere to go.

## Category 2: Land and Property

13. Can I get a construction loan if I don't own the land yet?
Yes. Lenders can structure a "Land Advance" as your first draw to
help acquire the vacant lot, typically lending 50-65% of the land's
current market value, with the remaining construction funds released
as building begins.

14. Can I use the equity in vacant land I already own as my down payment?
Yes. If you own the land clear of debt, its appraised market value
serves as your equity contribution. This frequently allows you to
borrow 100% of the physical building costs.

15. Do I need to own the land first before applying?
Not necessarily. Many borrowers buy the land and arrange
construction financing as one combined transaction. Some lenders
prefer borrowers already own the land; others structure both as one
deal.

16. Can I build a home before selling my current home?
Possibly. The lender reviews whether you can carry your current
mortgage plus the construction loan interest plus your living costs
during the build. Bridge financing options may apply.

17. What types of properties qualify for construction financing?
Most residential properties: detached homes (most common), semi-
detached, townhouses, duplexes, triplexes, fourplexes,
prefabricated/modular homes (through CMHC Prefab Plus or similar
programs), and certain mixed-use properties.

18. Can I get construction financing for a rural property?
Possibly. Rural construction adds complexity: location, road access,
water and septic systems, utility availability, zoning, and resale
market all factor in. Expect more scrutiny and possibly lower LTV.

19. Can I get construction financing for a duplex or multi-unit property?
Yes. The lender reviews zoning, permits, plans, budget, projected
rental income (where applicable), completed value, borrower
strength, and property type.

20. Can I build a secondary suite with construction financing?
Yes. CMHC's Refinance to Add Secondary Suites program supports this
specifically. Other options include refinance, HELOC, or specialized
construction-style draws. Eligibility depends on the property,
existing mortgage, equity, and lender.

21. Can I get construction financing for a modular or prefab home?
Yes. CMHC's Prefab Plus program is a specialized 4-stage high-ratio
draw system optimized for factory-built and modular homes. Allows
funding for land preparation and module delivery deposits without
needing alternative bridge financing.

22. Can construction financing be insured?
Yes. CMHC's Improvement program supports insured financing for
major improvements or building a new home from the ground up,
subject to eligibility. Sagen and Canada Guaranty offer similar
parallel products.

## Category 3: The Progress Draw System and Milestones

23. What is a progress draw schedule?
A predetermined checklist negotiated before construction starts that
establishes exactly which milestones must be completed and inspected
before the lender releases the next package of funds.

24. How many draws are typical in a standard Ontario build?
Traditional institutional banks typically restrict projects to 4 to
5 distinct draws to minimize administrative overhead. Alternative
and private lenders can offer customized, flexible draw counts.

25. What are the standard 4 stages of a traditional construction draw?
- Draw 1 (Foundation, ~15-20% complete): Excavation done, footings
  and foundation walls poured, backfilling complete.
- Draw 2 (Lock-up, ~50% complete): Structural framing complete, roof
  shingled, windows and doors installed so the house is locked from
  weather.
- Draw 3 (Rough-ins/Drywall, ~75% complete): Electrical, plumbing,
  HVAC roughed in; insulation installed; drywall hung, taped, and
  sanded.
- Draw 4 (Completion, 100% complete): Flooring, trim, painting,
  fixtures, and cabinetry finished; home ready for municipal
  occupancy.

26. How long does it take for the bank to release cash after a milestone is hit?
Typically 5 to 7 business days. The inspector visits the site,
compiles the progress report, submits it to the bank, and clears
title checks before money moves.

27. Why does the lender release money in stages instead of all upfront?
To minimize risk. The lender wants to verify that value is being
created (work is actually being done) before advancing more money.
This protects both the lender and the borrower from over-advancing
funds that won't be put to building.

28. Who decides when each draw is released?
The lender, based on the approved draw schedule, inspection results,
and construction progress. The borrower, builder, appraiser,
inspector, and lender all play roles.

29. Can I request an advance of funds before starting a stage?
Institutional lenders operate strictly on "prove the work is done
first." No advance cash for materials. Certain private lenders may
offer pre-advances if substantial equity exists.

30. Can the builder be paid directly from the lender?
Sometimes. Depending on the lender and structure, funds may be
advanced to the borrower, the lawyer (most common), or directly to
the builder. The lawyer typically disburses to the builder after
verifying the draw conditions.

31. Are inspections required during construction?
Yes, almost always. The lender requires inspections before releasing
each draw to verify progress matches what's being claimed.

32. Who pays for the site inspection fees?
The borrower. Inspection fees typically range $200-$400 per site
visit. Banks often deduct them directly from the loan advance
proceeds.

33. What is an as-completed appraisal?
A valuation conducted by a licensed appraiser who analyzes your
vacant land plus your architectural plans to project the fair
market value of the home once construction is 100% complete.
Lenders use this value to set the maximum loan amount.

34. What is the difference between "as-completed" and "as-improved" value?
"As-completed" usually refers to a fully new build; "as-improved"
refers to the value after an improvement or renovation. Both are
forward-looking valuations the lender uses to size the loan.
CMHC's Improvement product specifically references "as-improved"
value.

35. What happens if the appraisal comes in lower than expected?
The lender scales the loan amount down to fit the lower as-completed
value. The borrower may need a larger down payment, lower budget,
revised plans, or a different lender.

## Category 4: Costs, Fees, and Contingency

36. What does a construction loan cost?
Beyond the loan itself: interest during the build (interest-only on
amount advanced), legal fees, appraisal fees ($400-$600), site
inspection fees ($200-$400 each, multiple per project), title
insurance, builder's risk insurance, lender administration fees,
and possibly a lender setup fee.

37. Why are construction loan rates often higher than regular mortgage rates?
Construction financing is riskier for the lender than a completed
property because the asset is being created (and could be partially
or not completed). The premium compensates for that risk and the
extra administration of the draw program.

38. Do I need a contingency fund?
Yes, almost always. Construction projects routinely face cost
overruns, delays, permit issues, material price changes, or
unexpected site conditions. Lenders mandate a 5-15% contingency
reserve added to your qualifying debt ratios or proven liquid
savings to absorb spikes.

39. What is a "cost overrun contingency"?
A buffer the lender requires built into your qualification: typically
5-15% of project cost shown as either independent liquid savings or
added to debt ratios. Lenders treat construction over-budget as the
norm, not the exception.

40. What happens if my construction project goes over budget?
Cost overruns are serious. The borrower may need extra funds,
contingency reserves, a revised budget, or lender approval for
changes. If the budget runs significantly over and no extra equity
is available, the project can stall mid-build.

41. What are "hard costs"?
Physical construction elements: lumber, concrete, plumbing, drywall,
labour, materials. Hard costs are what the lender funds through
draws.

42. What are "soft costs"?
Non-physical design and legal costs: architect fees, structural
engineering, municipal permits, surveys, builder insurance. Soft
costs are usually paid by the borrower out of pocket using initial
equity, not funded through draws.

43. Will banks fund soft costs through draws?
Institutional banks expect soft costs to come from your initial
equity. Construction draws are earmarked for verified hard-cost
progress only.

44. What documents does the lender need to approve a construction loan?
Approved architectural blueprints, comprehensive line-by-line
construction budget, signed builder contract, building permit copy,
independent appraisal report based on the plans, land title, income
documents, credit information, builder's risk insurance proof.

45. Do I need a fixed-price builder contract?
Strongly preferred. Lenders prefer Fixed-Price or Guaranteed Maximum
Price (GMP) contracts. Cost-plus contracts (where the final price
fluctuates) are difficult to clear through standard bank
underwriting.

46. Can I make changes to the layout or materials mid-construction?
Be cautious. Significant changes require "Change Orders." If a
change increases the cost, the bank will NOT increase your loan
amount. You must fund the difference entirely in cash.

47. Can I include closing costs in the construction loan?
Generally no. Legal fees, land transfer tax, and most third-party
fees must be paid separately at closing. The site inspection fees
are usually deducted from each draw.

48. How much down payment or equity do I need for a construction loan?
For owner-occupied custom builds: 20-30% equity (cash plus land
value combined). Investment or rental builds usually require 30-40%
equity. Insured high-ratio construction (via CMHC Improvement) can
allow lower if you qualify, subject to insurer rules.

## Category 5: Builder Requirements (Ontario)

49. What is Tarion?
The regulatory body managing the Ontario New Home Warranties Plan
Act. Tarion provides the statutory warranty that comes with new
homes in Ontario.

50. Is Tarion warranty mandatory for an Ontario construction loan?
For contractor-built residential loans: yes. The builder and the
home must be fully registered with Tarion. Conventional banks
enforce this without exception.

51. What is HCRA?
The Home Construction Regulatory Authority licenses new home
builders in Ontario. Lenders verify HCRA licensing to confirm your
builder is in good legal standing before approving funding.

52. Can I build my own home (owner-builder) under a self-build track?
Yes. Owner-builders who physically act as general contractors can
secure a Tarion owner-builder exemption. However, standard banks
restrict these loans heavily and often route to specialized credit
unions or private lenders.

53. What are the bank constraints for an owner-builder self-build loan?
Lenders typically require: no individual sub-contract represents
more than 50% of the total construction budget, the site is within a
reasonable distance of an urban market centre, and the owner-builder
has extensive trade experience or a project manager engaged.

54. What is a "Notice of Project"?
A formal filing required with Ontario's Ministry of Labour when a
construction project's total value exceeds standard thresholds. The
general contractor files it before breaking ground to ensure
provincial safety compliance.

55. Does the builder need to be HCRA-licensed and Tarion-registered?
For contractor-built residential projects: yes, both. HCRA confirms
the builder is licensed; Tarion confirms the new home itself is
warranty-registered.

56. What if my builder loses their HCRA licence during the project?
A serious problem. The lender may freeze further draws until the
status is resolved. Your broker and lawyer would coordinate
options: complete the project with a different licensed builder,
restructure the loan, or worst case, file claims through Tarion.

57. What happens if my builder goes bankrupt mid-construction?
The lender stops all funding advances. Your broker works with your
lawyer to restructure the file, assess the remaining equity, and
source an approved alternative builder to salvage the project under
the Tarion warranty framework.

58. Do I need to use the lender's approved appraiser?
Usually yes. The lender's appraisal panel ensures the appraisal
meets their standards for the as-completed valuation. The borrower
typically can't substitute their own appraiser.

## Category 6: Ontario Construction Act and Statutory Holdback

59. What is the mandatory statutory holdback in Ontario?
Under the Ontario Construction Act, your real estate lawyer is
legally required to withhold exactly 10% of each construction draw
advance in a trust account.

60. Why does the 10% holdback exist?
As legal security. It ensures money remains available to pay
sub-trades or suppliers if the general contractor defaults on
payments, preventing construction liens from being registered
against the property title.

61. When is the accumulated 10% holdback released?
Typically 60 days after "substantial completion" of the project is
officially declared AND a clear title search proves no sub-trades
have filed construction liens.

62. How does the 10% holdback affect my cash flow during the build?
If a draw milestone is verified at $100,000, the lender advances
$90,000 to your builder. You or the contractor must have cash
reserves to carry the 10% gap until the holdback is released. This
catches some borrowers and builders off guard.

63. What happens if a sub-contractor registers a construction lien?
The lender immediately freezes all future draw advances.
Construction cannot resume and funds will not release until your
lawyer clears, pays out, or bonds the lien off the land registry.

64. Who is responsible for the 10% holdback?
The lawyer holds it in trust on behalf of the project. The funds
ultimately belong to the borrower (or are released to subcontractors
if liens are filed and proven). The lawyer's role is statutory under
the Construction Act.

65. Can I waive the 10% holdback?
No. The Construction Act requires it on covered projects. It's a
consumer and trade protection mechanism, not optional.

66. Does the holdback apply to all construction projects?
The Construction Act applies broadly to construction work in
Ontario. Specific thresholds and definitions apply; your lawyer
confirms what applies to your project.

67. What is "substantial completion"?
A defined milestone under the Construction Act roughly meaning the
project is ready or could be used for its intended purpose. Triggers
the 60-day clock for holdback release.

68. What is a construction lien?
A legal claim a sub-trade, supplier, or worker can register against
the property's title if they haven't been paid for their
contribution. Liens block financing, sales, and refinancing until
discharged.

## Category 7: Tax (HST) on Construction

69. Do I pay HST on my custom home construction costs?
Yes. The 13% HST applies to all hard costs, materials, and
contractor invoices throughout the building process in Ontario.

70. What is the Ontario First-Time Home Buyer HST rebate on new builds?
For eligible first-time purchasers of new or custom-built homes
valued up to $1 million, Ontario removes or rebates the provincial
portion of the HST, providing up to $80,000 in direct tax relief
(per the 2025 Fall Statement and expanded 2026 Budget). See the
Ontario HST corpus file for details.

71. Can the builder's HST rebate be credited during construction?
Generally no for custom builds on land you already own. You pay HST
on invoices progressively, then file a rebate claim with CRA once
the home reaches substantial completion.

72. Can I get the FTHB HST relief on a custom build?
Yes if you qualify as a first-time buyer and the build falls within
the program windows. The 2026 Budget expanded relief to all eligible
buyers on certain timelines.

73. Will my HST claim affect my mortgage approval?
The HST rebate doesn't directly affect mortgage approval, but the
out-of-pocket HST during the build affects your cash flow and may
factor into the contingency budget the lender requires.

74. Is HST charged on the appraisal fee?
Yes, like most professional services in Ontario, HST applies to the
appraisal fee.

75. Is HST charged on the lender or broker fees?
Often yes. Lender administrative fees and broker fees in Ontario
typically carry HST. Confirm with your lawyer at closing.

## Category 8: Insurance During Construction

76. Do I need home insurance active while my house is just a foundation?
Yes. Lenders mandate a specialized Course of Construction (Builder's
Risk) insurance policy to protect against site theft, fire,
vandalism, and liability from day one of excavation.

77. What does Course of Construction insurance cover?
Site theft of materials, fire, vandalism, weather damage during
construction, and liability for injuries on site. Standard
homeowner's policies don't cover unoccupied construction sites.

78. When can I switch to a regular homeowner's policy?
At substantial completion when the home is occupied or
move-in-ready. Some insurers offer a combined policy that converts
from Course of Construction to standard homeowner's at completion.

79. Who pays for the Course of Construction insurance?
The borrower. It's a required closing cost and continues throughout
the build period.

80. Will my insurance company want builder details?
Yes. The insurer typically reviews the builder, the project, the
plans, and the site to underwrite the policy. Insurance can be
denied or restricted on projects with unusual risks.

## Category 9: Alternative and Private Construction Options

81. When should I choose a private construction loan over a bank?
Choose private when: you need to close on a project within days, you
lack verifiable T4 income, you want to build without a Tarion
warranty (owner-builder), you require a flexible cost-plus
structure, or your credit isn't strong enough for bank approval.

82. What do private construction loan rates and fees look like?
Higher rates (typically 10-14% interest-only) and upfront lender/
broker setup fees totaling 3-5% of the loan size. Reflects the
higher risk and faster execution private lenders provide.

83. Do private construction lenders still require site inspections?
Yes, to ensure building progress matches advanced cash. They are
flexible with timing and don't require rigid phase-by-phase
matching but they verify work is happening.

84. Can a private construction loan fund 100% of building costs?
Only if you have substantial underlying equity in your vacant land.
Private lenders calculate exposure on a strict Loan-to-Cost (LTC)
or Loan-to-Value (LTV) ceiling, typically capping advanced exposure
around 70-75% of project completed worth.

85. What is a Syndicated Construction Loan?
A larger-scale development financing structure where multiple
private or institutional investors collectively pool capital to fund
a multi-unit townhome, subdivision, or large project.

86. Can B-lenders fund construction?
Some can, especially for smaller construction projects or
renovation-construction hybrids. The lender reviews project size,
equity, borrower strength, property type, builder, and exit plan.

87. Can credit unions fund construction?
Some Ontario credit unions specialize in construction financing,
especially for owner-builders or rural builds. Ontario credit unions
are provincially regulated and not bound by OSFI's stress test, which
can help marginal files.

88. Can a HELOC be used for construction?
For smaller projects, yes. For full new builds, usually not; the
HELOC's revolving structure and 65% LTV cap don't fit the
construction-draw funding model.

89. Can I refinance my existing home to fund construction elsewhere?
Yes. A refinance on your existing home can extract equity to fund a
build on another property. Combined with a construction loan on the
new property, this can fund the build without needing land
financing.

90. Can construction be funded with a reverse mortgage?
Generally no. Reverse mortgages are for older homeowners with
completed homes, not for construction. The funds could be used after
completion but aren't structured for in-build draws.

## Category 10: Process and Timeline

91. How long does the entire construction loan approval process take?
Typically 4-8 weeks for approval and to start the first draw. The
draw cycle then continues for the duration of the build (typically
6-18 months).

92. What is the typical construction loan term length?
9-12 months is standard for the construction phase. Some loans
extend to 18 months for larger projects. After completion, the loan
converts to a permanent mortgage or is paid off by a take-out
mortgage.

93. What happens if my construction project is delayed past the loan term?
Most loans have strict expiration timelines. If you exceed it, you
must apply for an extension, which triggers administrative fees and
possible interest rate adjustments. Delays from weather, material
shortages, or builder issues are common, so plan the timeline
generously.

94. How is the build timeline established at the start?
The builder provides a schedule based on the project scope, weather,
and trade availability. The lender uses that schedule to set draw
milestones and the loan maturity date.

95. Can I extend my construction loan?
Yes, usually with an extension fee (often 1% of the loan amount)
and possibly a rate adjustment. The lender wants to see the reason
for the delay and the revised completion plan.

## Category 11: Troubleshooting

96. Can I refinance or pull out equity mid-construction?
No. A construction loan agreement is locked into the registered
budget. No equity extractions, refinances, or secondary adjustments
until final inspection hits 100% completion.

97. Can I buy appliances and furniture on credit during the build?
Avoid this. Running up credit card balances mid-build flags your
credit file. If your debt service ratios spike, the bank can cancel
your end mortgage conversion, leaving you trapped with maturing
construction debt.

98. What happens if my builder goes bankrupt mid-project?
The lender freezes draws. Your broker and lawyer work to assess
remaining equity, source an alternative licensed builder, and
restructure the file. The Tarion warranty framework may help recover
some losses on the original builder's incomplete work.

99. What if my appraisal comes in below my construction budget?
The lender scales financing to fit the as-completed appraisal. You'd
need a larger down payment, smaller scope, or a different lender to
proceed. Some borrowers reduce finishes or scope to bring the build
into appraisal range.

100. What happens if a lien is filed during construction?
All draws freeze immediately. The lien must be cleared (paid out or
bonded off) before construction can resume and funds can release.
The lawyer leads the discharge process; the lender doesn't release
until proven clear.

101. Can I switch construction lenders mid-project?
Very difficult. Construction loans are written for specific projects
with specific draw schedules. Switching mid-project typically isn't
practical. If the original lender becomes problematic, the borrower
may need to refinance the entire project, which is expensive and
disruptive.

## Category 12: Additional borrower questions

102. Can I live in the home during construction?
No, the home isn't safe or legal to occupy until substantial
completion and municipal occupancy approval. Some projects allow
partial occupancy of completed phases, but this is unusual.

103. Do I need to live in the home after construction?
For owner-occupied loans: yes, typically within a reasonable
timeframe of completion (often 6 months). Investment construction
loans don't require occupancy.

104. Can I rent out the home after construction without telling the lender?
On owner-occupied loans, this could breach the loan terms. The
lender approved the loan based on owner occupancy. Renting requires
notifying the lender and possibly converting to an investment
mortgage at different terms.

105. What is "occupancy approval" from the municipality?
The municipal sign-off that the home meets code and is safe to
inhabit. Triggers final draw release and the mortgage conversion to
permanent financing.

106. Can the lender require specific subcontractors?
Generally no, but the lender approves the general contractor. The
sub-trades are the GC's responsibility.

107. What if my building permit expires during the build?
Building permits have time limits. If yours expires, you must renew
it (with municipal approval and possibly additional fees). The
lender may pause draws until the permit is current.

108. Can I make a lump-sum payment on the construction loan early?
Most construction loans allow some prepayment but check the
contract. Construction loans usually have less restrictive
prepayment than long-term closed mortgages because the structure
expects payout at completion anyway.

109. What information do I need to provide for a construction loan review?
Land ownership status, land value or purchase agreement, building
plans, permit status, construction budget (detailed line-by-line),
builder details and contract type, builder Tarion/HCRA registration,
timeline, down payment available, your income and credit, and your
expected completed value.

110. Can a mortgage broker help with construction financing?
Yes. A broker can compare bank, alt, and private construction loan
options, structure the draw schedule with the lender, and coordinate
with the appraiser, lawyer, and insurance broker. Construction
financing benefits significantly from broker experience.

## What this corpus file is not

- It is not the Ontario Construction Act statutory text; consult a
  lawyer for specific Construction Act questions.
- It is not Tarion warranty terms or HCRA licensing rules in detail;
  Tarion and HCRA publish their own consumer guides.
- It is not specific CMHC Improvement or Prefab Plus product terms;
  see the CMHC corpus files for those programs.
- It is not legal, tax, or financial advice for any specific
  construction project.
- The typical ranges noted (draw release timeline, inspection fees,
  contingency reserves, private rate ranges, term lengths) are
  Ontario market context as of the authoring date, not current
  quotes.
