# Reverse Mortgages: Frequently Asked Questions

Plain-language Q&A reference for the kinds of questions borrowers ask
about reverse mortgages in Canada. Synthesized from two AI-generated
sources (ChatGPT and Gemini), deduplicated semantically, plus 50
additional borrower-facing questions added. The framework explanation
of reverse mortgages and the captured Equitable Bank product detail
live in `jurisdictions/CA/reverse-mortgages-canada.md`. This file is
the borrower-facing Q&A complement.

## How the agent should use this file

These Q&As give the agent background context for reverse mortgage
conversations. Typical ranges noted (closing costs $2,000-$3,500,
appraisal $400-$600, admin fees $995-$1,795, 3-4 week processing,
180-day estate grace period, prepayment penalty scaling 5/4/3 months
interest by year on Equitable Flex) are general market context, not
quotes. Real numbers vary by lender, product, file, and date.

Two main Canadian reverse mortgage providers: HomeEquity Bank
(CHIP product family) and Equitable Bank (Reverse Mortgage Flex and
Flex PLUS). Both are federally regulated. OSFI treats reverse
mortgages as HELOCs for B-20 risk-management purposes (see
`jurisdictions/CA/osfi-heloc-clp-clarification.md`).

## Category 1: Reverse Mortgage Fundamentals

1. What is a reverse mortgage?
A reverse mortgage is a specialized loan for Canadian homeowners 55
and older that lets the borrower convert part of their home equity
into tax-free cash without making any monthly mortgage payments. The
loan is repaid when the home is sold, the borrower moves out
permanently, or the last surviving borrower passes away.

2. Who is a reverse mortgage for?
Older homeowners (55+) who have home equity, want to access cash
without selling, and either don't want to make monthly payments or
can't qualify for income-based products like a HELOC or refinance.
Often used by retirees on fixed income.

3. Does the lender take ownership of my home?
No. You retain full ownership and stay on title. The lender simply
registers a financial charge against the property, just like a
traditional mortgage. You control the home throughout the term.

4. Do I have to sell my home to get a reverse mortgage?
No. A reverse mortgage is designed specifically to let you access
equity while continuing to live in the home.

5. Is a reverse mortgage the same as a HELOC?
No. A HELOC is a revolving line of credit requiring monthly interest
payments and income/credit qualification. A reverse mortgage requires
no monthly payments and qualifies on age, equity, and property
rather than income.

6. Is a reverse mortgage the same as a refinance?
No. A refinance is a new mortgage with monthly principal and
interest payments and full income-based qualification. A reverse
mortgage has no monthly payments and qualifies on age and equity.

## Category 2: Eligibility (Age, Property, Residency)

7. How old do I need to be?
At least 55. If there is more than one homeowner on title, every
person on title must be 55 or older, and the maximum borrowing
amount is calculated based on the AGE OF THE YOUNGEST owner.

8. Do both spouses need to be 55 or older?
Yes, if both spouses are on title. If only one spouse is on title,
only that spouse needs to be 55+, but lenders may have additional
disclosure requirements for the non-title spouse.

9. Can I get a reverse mortgage in Ontario?
Yes. Reverse mortgages are available across Canada through HomeEquity
Bank and Equitable Bank. Ontario homeowners can qualify subject to
age, property, value, and location criteria.

10. Does the home need to be my primary residence?
Yes. Reverse mortgages require the home to be your primary,
owner-occupied residence. Equitable Bank requires borrowers to live
in the residence at least 6 months of the year.

11. What types of properties qualify?
Standard eligibility: detached homes, semi-detached homes,
townhouses, and registered condominiums. Property must be on freehold
land owned by the borrower.

12. Can I get a reverse mortgage on a cottage or rental?
No. Standard reverse mortgages require the property to be your
primary owner-occupied residence. Cottages, secondary homes, and
pure investment properties do not qualify.

13. Can I get a reverse mortgage on a condo?
Yes. Registered condominiums qualify subject to the building's
condition, financial health, and the unit's marketability.

14. Can I get a reverse mortgage on a rural property?
Possibly. Rural properties face stricter scrutiny because of resale
liquidity. Maximum LTV caps are typically lower than urban properties
of equivalent value.

15. Can I get a reverse mortgage on leased land or a mobile home park?
No. Lenders require ownership of both the physical home AND the
underlying freehold land. Land-lease communities, co-operative
housing, and properties on leased land are generally ineligible.

16. Is there a minimum home value?
Yes. Most products require the property to have a minimum appraised
market value of $250,000. Some premium products (e.g., Equitable
Bank Flex PLUS) require $300,000 or more.

17. Does my location inside Ontario affect borrowing capacity?
Yes. Major urban centres (Toronto, Ottawa, Mississauga, London)
receive the maximum LTV caps. Rural and remote northern areas face
lower caps due to slower resale markets.

18. Do I need high credit to qualify for a reverse mortgage?
No. Because no monthly payments are required, lenders do not run
traditional debt-service ratio calculations. Qualification is
asset-based: age, home value, and location are the main factors.

19. Do I need strong income to qualify?
No. Income is not the main qualifying factor. Reverse mortgages are
often used by people with home equity but limited qualifying income
(retirees on pension and Old Age Security, for example).

20. Can I get approved if I am currently in a consumer proposal?
Possibly. The reverse mortgage proceeds can be used to pay off and
discharge an active consumer proposal at closing, freeing up monthly
cash flow.

## Category 3: How Much You Can Borrow

21. How much can I borrow with a reverse mortgage?
Canada.ca says reverse mortgage borrowers may access up to 55% of
the current home value. Equitable Bank's standard Flex product goes
up to 59% with Flex PLUS for older borrowers (typically 70+) on
qualifying urban properties. The specific cap depends on age,
property value, location, and lender.

22. Can I borrow the full value of my home?
No. Reverse mortgages only allow access to a portion of the home's
equity. The cap exists to ensure enough equity remains to cover the
growing loan balance over time.

23. Why does the borrowing cap vary so much between a 55-year-old and an 80-year-old?
Because interest accumulates without monthly payments, lenders grant
lower amounts to younger applicants (typically 15-25% at age 55) and
higher amounts to older applicants (up to 55-59% at age 75+). Older
borrowers have a shorter expected loan duration, which limits how
much the balance can grow before the loan becomes due.

24. What other factors affect how much I can get?
Property type (detached gets more than condo gets more than
non-standard), location (urban vs rural), property condition, current
appraised value, and any existing mortgage or HELOC that must be
paid out from the advance.

25. Can I get a reverse mortgage if I still owe money on my mortgage?
Yes. But the reverse mortgage must take first position on title, so
the existing mortgage gets paid out from the reverse mortgage
proceeds at closing. If your approved reverse mortgage amount isn't
enough to cover the existing mortgage payout, the deal doesn't work.

26. Can I get a reverse mortgage if I have a HELOC?
Yes. Same rule as above: the HELOC must be paid out and discharged
at closing as part of the reverse mortgage funding.

## Category 4: Receiving the Funds

27. How do I receive the money?
Three options: lump-sum advance at closing, scheduled monthly or
quarterly advances (income-style cash flow), or a combination. The
choice depends on the product and the borrower's needs.

28. What's the minimum initial advance?
Most standard reverse mortgages require a minimum initial advance of
$25,000 at closing. Subsequent optional advances typically have a
$5,000 minimum.

29. What is HomeEquity Bank's CHIP Mortgage vs Income Advantage?
CHIP Mortgage is the standard product, providing a lump-sum cash
advance. Income Advantage is structured for regular cash flow: a
smaller initial advance plus automated recurring monthly deposits to
the borrower's bank account.

30. What is the CHIP Open product?
A specialized short-term reverse mortgage that allows repayment at
any time with no prepayment penalties. Carries a higher rate than
standard CHIP. Designed for borrowers planning to sell within 1-2
years (bridge use).

31. What is Equitable Bank's Reverse Mortgage Flex PLUS?
A product designed for older borrowers (typically 70+) that allows
access to enhanced leverage, up to 59% LTV on qualifying urban
properties. Higher LTV reflects the lender's actuarial view that
older borrowers have a shorter expected loan duration.

## Category 5: Interest, Rates, and Compounding

32. Are reverse mortgage interest rates higher than traditional mortgage rates?
Yes, almost always. The premium reflects the lender's risk: they
receive no monthly cash flow for potentially decades, and they bear
the long-term risk of compounding under the no-negative-equity
guarantee.

33. Can I choose a fixed or variable rate?
Yes. Lenders offer 1-year, 3-year, and 5-year fixed terms as well
as adjustable variable rates linked to the lender's reverse mortgage
prime rate.

34. Does the loan balance grow over time?
Yes. Because interest is added to the balance monthly without
payments offsetting it, the balance compounds and grows over time.

35. How does compounding affect my home equity over 10 years?
The loan balance grows steadily. If home values appreciate at a
healthy rate, your remaining equity can still increase. If prices
stall or fall, the remaining equity share gradually shrinks. The
faster home prices grow, the more cushion remains.

36. Can I pay off the interest monthly to stop compounding?
Yes. Most modern reverse mortgages include prepayment privileges
allowing the borrower to pay 10% or more of the principal or
outstanding interest annually without penalty. This slows or stops
the compounding for borrowers with available cash flow.

37. Do reverse mortgage rates ever drop?
Yes, when broader market rates fall. Variable-rate borrowers see
the rate change with the lender's prime. Fixed-rate borrowers stay
at their locked rate until the term ends, then qualify at current
rates.

38. Can I lock in my reverse mortgage rate?
Yes, by choosing a fixed-rate term. Most lenders offer 1, 3, or 5
year fixed terms. Variable rates are also available.

39. What is the term length on a reverse mortgage?
1, 3, or 5 years is typical for fixed rates. The reverse mortgage
itself has no maturity date in the traditional sense; only the rate
term changes. The loan continues until a repayment trigger.

40. Do I have to renew my reverse mortgage like a regular mortgage?
The rate term renews, but the loan itself doesn't have a maturity
date the way a regular mortgage does. At rate-term renewal, you
choose a new rate option. The loan continues until a repayment
trigger.

## Category 6: Process, Documents, ILA

41. What are typical closing costs for a reverse mortgage?
Approximately $2,000 to $3,500 in total. This covers the appraisal
(typically $400-$600), lender administration fees ($995-$1,795
depending on product and lender), and independent legal advice.

42. Do I have to pay setup costs out-of-pocket?
No. Except for the initial appraisal fee, all setup costs and legal
fees are deducted directly from the mortgage advance at closing.
Borrowers don't need to bring cash to the closing table.

43. What is Independent Legal Advice (ILA)?
A mandatory consultation with a real estate lawyer who does NOT
represent the lender. ILA is required in Ontario specifically to
protect older borrowers, ensuring they fully understand the
compounding loan structure and how it affects their estate before
signing.

44. Why is ILA mandatory?
Because reverse mortgages are complex, irreversible, and target an
older demographic. ILA is a consumer protection measure ensuring the
borrower receives unbiased explanation of the loan terms, estate
impact, and alternatives.

45. How long does the reverse mortgage process take?
Typically 3 to 4 weeks from application to funding. Time covers the
appraisal, title searches, mandatory ILA, and execution of funding
documents.

46. What documents do I need to apply?
Government ID, proof of age, current mortgage statement (if any),
property tax bill, home insurance confirmation, basic income
documents (for the lender's records, not for qualification), and
sometimes spousal status documentation.

47. Do I need to update my home insurance?
Yes. The lender must be named as loss payee on your home insurance
policy. Failure to maintain insurance is a default condition under
the mortgage.

## Category 7: Tax Treatment and Government Benefits

48. Is the money from a reverse mortgage taxable?
No. The proceeds are 100% tax-free. They are classified as a loan
advance, not income, so they are completely exempt from personal
income tax.

49. Will a reverse mortgage reduce my OAS or GIS?
No. Canada.ca confirms reverse mortgage proceeds are loan advances,
not income, and do not affect Old Age Security, the Guaranteed
Income Supplement, or other income-tested government benefits.

50. Will a reverse mortgage affect my CPP?
No. CPP is contribution-based, not income-tested. Reverse mortgage
proceeds have no impact on CPP entitlement.

51. Can I use reverse mortgage funds to invest and deduct the interest?
Potentially yes. If you use the funds to purchase income-producing
non-registered investments, the interest accruing on that portion
of the reverse mortgage may be tax-deductible. A tax professional
should confirm what applies to your situation.

52. Should I talk to my accountant before getting a reverse mortgage?
Yes. While the proceeds are tax-free, the use of funds and impact on
estate planning have tax implications a qualified accountant should
review.

## Category 8: Ownership, Heirs, and the Estate

53. What is the "No Negative Equity Guarantee"?
A legally binding clause provided by major Canadian reverse mortgage
lenders. It guarantees that the total amount repaid will never
exceed the fair market value of the home at the time it is sold.
Your estate cannot be held personally liable for a shortfall if the
home sells for less than the loan balance.

54. Will my children inherit debt from a reverse mortgage?
No. The loan is tied to the property, not to your children
personally. The debt is settled from the sale proceeds of the home.
The no-negative-equity guarantee protects heirs from any shortfall.

55. Will my children inherit less because of the reverse mortgage?
Possibly. The compounding loan balance reduces the remaining equity
in the home, which reduces what passes to the estate. The size of
the reduction depends on how long the loan has been in place and
how home values changed during that time.

56. What happens to leftover equity after the reverse mortgage is paid off?
All remaining equity belongs to you or your estate. After the loan
balance is cleared from the home sale, 100% of the remaining cash
goes to the estate (or you, if the home is sold during your
lifetime).

57. How long does my estate have to pay off the reverse mortgage after I pass away?
Typically 180 days (about 6 months) in Ontario. This grace period
allows the estate to complete probate, arrange financing, or sell
the property to clear the balance. Specific timing is set in the
mortgage contract.

58. What happens if the home doesn't sell within the grace period?
The estate may request an extension, especially if the home is
listed and actively marketed. The lender typically works with the
estate as long as there's good faith effort to sell.

59. Can my heirs refinance to pay off the reverse mortgage and keep the home?
Yes, if they can qualify for a traditional mortgage to refinance the
balance. This is a common path when one heir wants to keep the
family home.

60. Can my heirs take over my reverse mortgage?
Generally no. The reverse mortgage was qualified on the original
borrowers' ages and property. Heirs cannot assume it. They either
pay it off, refinance it, or sell the home.

## Category 9: When the Loan Becomes Due

61. When does a reverse mortgage need to be repaid?
The loan becomes due when one of these triggers: the last surviving
homeowner on title passes away, the home is sold, the borrower moves
out permanently (e.g., to long-term care), or the borrower defaults
on baseline homeowner obligations (property tax, insurance,
maintenance).

62. What happens if I move into long-term care?
If you are the only owner on title, the loan becomes due when you
permanently leave the home. If you have a spouse on title who
continues living in the home, the loan stays active until the last
surviving spouse on title leaves or passes away.

63. How long can I be temporarily away before the lender considers it permanent?
Most contracts allow up to 6 consecutive months for medical reasons.
Absences longer than 6-12 months without return may be classified as
permanent, triggering the loan due date. The contract specifies the
exact terms.

64. Can a reverse mortgage lender ever force me out of my home?
No, not as long as you maintain the property, keep home insurance
active, and pay property taxes. The lender cannot demand repayment
or force a sale while you remain in compliance with the basic
homeowner obligations.

## Category 10: Defaults, Risks, and Prepayment

65. What happens if I default on property taxes or home insurance?
This is a technical default under the reverse mortgage. The lender
has the legal right to pay the arrears on your behalf to protect
their security and can call the loan due if the default isn't
remedied.

66. Are there prepayment penalties if I pay off the reverse mortgage early?
Yes. Reverse mortgages are structured for the long term, so paying
out early triggers a prepayment charge. Penalties scale down the
longer you hold the loan.

67. What does the standard prepayment penalty look like?
Equitable Bank Flex (as an example): 5 months interest in year one,
4 months in year two, 3 months in years 3 through 10. From year 11
onward, often zero prepayment fees. Other lenders have similar
declining-by-year structures.

68. What are the main risks of a reverse mortgage?
Compounding balance reducing estate equity over time, higher
interest cost than traditional mortgages, prepayment penalties if
the loan is paid off in early years, restrictions on selling or
moving, and potential reduction in inheritance.

69. What if my home values drop significantly after I take out the reverse mortgage?
The no-negative-equity guarantee protects you. Even if a severe
market correction drops the home value below the loan balance, the
lender absorbs the shortfall. Neither you nor your estate is liable
beyond the sale price.

## Category 11: Use Cases

70. Can I use a reverse mortgage to pay off my existing mortgage?
Yes. This is one of the most common uses. The reverse mortgage
proceeds pay out the existing mortgage at closing, eliminating the
monthly payment burden.

71. Can I use a reverse mortgage to pay off debt?
Yes. Credit card debt, lines of credit, car loans, CRA debt, and
other obligations can be consolidated through reverse mortgage
proceeds.

72. Can I use a reverse mortgage for home renovations?
Yes. Renovations, accessibility upgrades, and aging-in-place
modifications are common uses.

73. Can I use a reverse mortgage for in-home healthcare or medical modifications?
Yes. Funding ramps, stairlifts, accessibility renovations, or
private nursing care to age in place comfortably is a common and
valuable use of reverse mortgage proceeds.

74. Can I use a reverse mortgage to help my children buy a home?
Yes. The "living inheritance" strategy lets parents extract tax-free
equity to gift a down payment for their child's home purchase. The
parent watches the child enjoy the home rather than waiting for
inheritance later.

75. Can I use a reverse mortgage for retirement income?
Yes. Scheduled advances can supplement retirement income, cover
ongoing expenses, or create additional cash flow on top of CPP, OAS,
GIS, and pension income.

76. Can I use a reverse mortgage to buy a new home while downsizing?
Yes. Sell the current home, buy a smaller property, and use a new
reverse mortgage on the new property to cover any funding shortfall.
This avoids carrying monthly payment stress in the new home.

77. Can I use reverse mortgage funds for a vacation or travel?
Yes. The funds are yours to use for any legal purpose. Many
borrowers use proceeds for travel, lifestyle, or experiences they
want to enjoy in retirement.

78. Can I use reverse mortgage funds to start or fund a business?
Yes, though most planners would caution against using long-term
compounding debt for higher-risk business ventures. A financial
advisor should review the specifics.

## Category 12: Comparisons

79. How does a reverse mortgage compare to a HELOC?
A HELOC is cheaper (lower rate) but requires income and credit
qualification AND monthly interest payments. A reverse mortgage is
more expensive but requires no monthly payments and no income
qualification. HELOC is usually better if you can qualify and can
afford the payments.

80. Is a reverse mortgage better than refinancing?
Depends. A refinance gives a lower rate but requires income to
qualify and adds a monthly payment. A reverse mortgage requires no
income qualification and has no monthly payment, but it's more
expensive. The right choice depends on income, payment capacity, and
estate considerations.

81. Can I use a reverse mortgage if I was declined for a refinance?
Yes, often. If the refinance was declined because of insufficient
income, age, or limited employment, a reverse mortgage may still
work because qualification is asset-based, not income-based.

82. Should I talk to my family before getting a reverse mortgage?
Strongly recommended. A reverse mortgage affects estate planning and
inheritance. Discussing it with family allows everyone to understand
the trade-offs and prevents surprises later.

83. Should I talk to my financial planner first?
Yes. A planner can help model the long-term impact on estate value,
compare alternatives, and ensure the reverse mortgage fits the
broader retirement strategy.

84. Are reverse mortgages insured by CMHC?
No. CMHC insures traditional residential mortgages. Reverse
mortgages are uninsured products offered by federally regulated
lenders (HomeEquity Bank, Equitable Bank). The no-negative-equity
guarantee is provided by the lender, not by government insurance.

## Category 13: Additional borrower questions

85. Are there only two reverse mortgage providers in Canada?
The two main providers are HomeEquity Bank (CHIP product family) and
Equitable Bank (Reverse Mortgage Flex and Flex PLUS). A few smaller
or specialty lenders exist, but those two dominate the Canadian
market.

86. Can I qualify if I'm 50 and turning 55 soon?
No. The 55+ minimum is a hard rule at the time of application. You
need to be 55 on the application date, not at some future date.

87. Can I qualify if only one spouse is 55+?
Yes, but only the 55+ spouse can be on title for the reverse
mortgage. The non-55 spouse may need to sign acknowledgements but
cannot be on title for the reverse mortgage product.

88. Will the lender require my non-title spouse to sign documents?
Often yes. Most provinces require a non-title spouse to sign
acknowledgements protecting their interest, even if they're not on
the mortgage itself. ILA may apply to the non-title spouse as well.

89. Can I cancel a reverse mortgage application before closing?
Yes. Before signing the final commitment and before the funds are
advanced, you can withdraw. You may forfeit the appraisal fee
already paid.

90. Is there a cooling-off period after I sign?
Some provinces require a short cooling-off period after signing
reverse mortgage documents. Confirm with your lawyer during ILA.

91. Can I change my mind after the money is advanced?
Once funds are advanced, the mortgage is fully in effect. You can
pay it off (subject to prepayment penalty during the first 10
years), but you cannot reverse the transaction.

92. What if I don't want all the money the lender approves?
You don't have to take the maximum. Lenders set a maximum approved
amount, but the borrower decides how much to draw. The minimum
initial advance applies ($25,000 typical).

93. Can I increase my reverse mortgage later if I need more money?
Yes, through additional advances. As long as you stay within the
lender's maximum approved amount and meet the minimum advance ($5,000
typical for subsequent advances), you can request more later.

94. Can I refinance my reverse mortgage to get more equity?
Possibly. If your home value has increased significantly or your
age has crossed a higher LTV tier, you may qualify for a refinance
or top-up. The lender re-evaluates the file.

95. Can I switch from a reverse mortgage to a traditional mortgage?
Yes, if you qualify for a traditional mortgage based on income and
credit. Some borrowers do this when their financial picture changes
(inheritance, business success, return to work). The reverse
mortgage is paid out from the new traditional mortgage proceeds.

96. Can I switch reverse mortgage lenders?
Possibly, treated as a new application. The new lender re-evaluates
the file. Existing penalty may apply if breaking the current term
early.

97. Are reverse mortgage rates negotiable?
Slightly. Most lenders offer modest rate concessions through brokers
or for strong files. The reverse mortgage market has fewer
competitive lenders than traditional mortgages, so the negotiation
window is narrower.

98. How is the appraisal for a reverse mortgage different from a regular mortgage?
The appraisal must be done by an approved third-party appraiser the
lender accepts. Some lenders accept the borrower's choice of
qualified appraiser; others require their panel. The appraisal
focuses on current market value and marketability.

99. What if my home needs major repairs before getting a reverse mortgage?
The lender may require the repairs to be completed before funding,
or may hold back funds to be released after repairs are verified.
Some products allow rolling repair costs into the advance.

100. What if my heirs disagree about selling the home?
The heirs must work out the disagreement among themselves, typically
with the executor mediating or seeking court guidance. The lender's
180-day grace period continues regardless. If no resolution by the
deadline, the lender may sell to recover the balance.

101. Will the lender try to sell the home themselves?
Only after the grace period expires without estate action. The
lender prefers the estate to sell because estate-managed sales
typically achieve fair market value. Forced lender sales are a last
resort.

102. Can my estate keep the home indefinitely?
Only if they pay out the reverse mortgage in full from other estate
assets or by refinancing. The home cannot be held indefinitely with
the balance still owing.

103. What documents will my estate need to settle the reverse mortgage?
Death certificate, probate documents, the original mortgage
contract, current discharge statement from the lender, sale
documents (if selling), or refinance commitment (if refinancing).
The estate's lawyer coordinates with the lender.

104. Can a reverse mortgage be used to delay selling the home?
Yes. If a senior wants to stay in their home but cash flow is tight,
a reverse mortgage provides funds without forcing a sale. The home
sale happens later (often via the estate) instead of now.

105. Does a reverse mortgage affect my will?
Not directly. The will still controls how the estate is distributed.
The reverse mortgage just reduces the estate's net value because
the home equity is partially used. Updating the will after a major
reverse mortgage is often recommended to set realistic expectations.

106. Can I receive reverse mortgage funds in another country?
No. The funds are deposited to your Canadian bank account.
International transfers happen after that, subject to your bank's
rules and currency exchange.

107. Can I use reverse mortgage funds for stock market investments?
Yes, the funds can be used for any legal purpose. Using leveraged
home equity for volatile market investments carries real risk; most
planners caution against it but won't block the use.

108. Can I have both a reverse mortgage and a regular HELOC?
Generally no. The reverse mortgage must hold first position on title.
An existing HELOC would need to be paid out and discharged at the
reverse mortgage closing.

109. Is a reverse mortgage different from a home equity loan?
A "home equity loan" usually means a second mortgage or HELOC. A
reverse mortgage is structurally different because it requires no
monthly payments and qualifies on age + equity, not income + credit.

110. What if I want to break the reverse mortgage in year 4?
You can, with a prepayment penalty of approximately 3 months
interest at year 4 on Equitable Flex products. Other products have
similar declining penalty structures. Confirm specifics with the
broker before signing.

111. What information do I need to provide for a reverse mortgage review?
Ages of all homeowners on title, property address and location,
property type, estimated property value, existing mortgage balance
(if any), HELOC balance (if any), desired loan amount, occupancy
status, and intended use of funds.
