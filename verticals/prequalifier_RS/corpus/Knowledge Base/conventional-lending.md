# Conventional (A-lender) mortgages

This file describes conventional A-lender mortgages at a general,
educational level so the assistant can explain them in plain language.
Specific rates, qualifying amounts, payment estimates, lender names, and
approval guidance never go here. Those are off-limits in chat regardless
of what the corpus says.

## What an A-lender is

A-lenders are mainstream prime lenders: the major banks, credit unions, and
monoline lenders that price off prime credit, verifiable income, and
mainstream property profiles. They publish posted rates, run published
qualification rules, and underwrite to the tightest documentation
standards.

## Borrower profile that typically fits

- Credit score in the prime range.
- Income that can be verified through standard documents (T4 / W2,
  notices of assessment, employment letter, recent pay stubs).
- Stable employment history.
- Property that fits standard urban or suburban profiles (detached, semi,
  town, low-rise condo, high-rise condo with healthy reserve, owner-occupied
  or standard rental).
- Down payment from a verifiable source.

## Common A-lender products

- Fixed-rate mortgages: the rate is locked for a set term. Predictable
  payment, breakage penalty if the borrower leaves the term early.
- Variable-rate mortgages: the rate moves with the lender's prime. Payment
  may or may not float depending on the product.
- Adjustable-rate mortgages: payment changes with prime.
- Insured high-ratio mortgages: down payment below the conventional
  threshold; default insurance is required.
- Conventional low-ratio mortgages: down payment at or above the threshold;
  insurance optional or not required.
- Refinances, switches, renewals.
- HELOCs and combined mortgage-line products.

## What an A-lender typically reviews

Income, credit, debt servicing ratios, down payment source, property type
and location, appraisal where required, and the borrower's overall file
strength. Each lender has its own overlays on top of the published rules,
which is why two A-lenders can take a different view of the same file.

## When an A-lender is NOT the right fit

- Recent or current credit events (collections, judgements, recent
  bankruptcy or proposal).
- Income that cannot be verified through standard documents.
- Self-employed borrowers without two years of notices of assessment, or
  with significant write-downs.
- Properties in markets the A-lender does not lend in.
- Non-standard property (mixed-use, unique rural, leasehold on certain
  reserves, large acreage).
- Files that need to close in days rather than weeks.

When a file does not fit an A-lender, the natural next step is an
alternative lender or, depending on the situation, a private mortgage. See
`alternative-lending.md` and `private-lending.md`.

## What the assistant should and should not say

The assistant can explain what an A-lender is, what products they typically
offer, what they typically look for, and when a file likely fits or does
not. The assistant should not name specific A-lenders as a borrower's best
fit, quote rates, or predict whether a borrower will be approved.
