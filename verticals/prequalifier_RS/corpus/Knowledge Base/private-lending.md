# Private mortgages

This file describes private mortgages at a general, educational level so
the assistant can explain them in plain language. Specific rates,
qualifying amounts, payment estimates, lender names, and approval guidance
never go here.

## What private lending is

Private mortgages are equity-based loans funded by private individuals,
syndicates, or private mortgage corporations. The lending decision is
driven mostly by the property's equity position and marketability, the
exit strategy, and the file's story. Credit and income matter much less
than they do at A or alt. Speed and flexibility are the trade-off the
borrower pays for with higher rates and fees.

## When a private mortgage is the right fit

- Bridge: the borrower is closing on a new property before the existing
  one sells, and needs short-term capital.
- Rescue: the borrower is facing a power-of-sale, missed payments, a CRA
  or IRS arrears situation, or a foreclosure proceeding and needs to
  refinance out before things escalate.
- Speed: an A or alt timeline will not fit the closing date.
- Story files: prior bankruptcy or proposal, very thin credit, undisclosed
  income, complex business structures, a file the alt lenders will not
  take at this LTV today.
- Equity takeout for business capital, tax arrears, debt consolidation, or
  property improvement, where the borrower has strong equity but a weak
  borrower side.
- Second or third positions behind an existing first.

## Borrower profile that typically fits

- Real equity in the property (the loan-to-value ceiling on a private
  first is typically conservative, and on a second or third it is lower
  still).
- A clear short-term plan: refinance back to alt or A in a year or two,
  sell the property, settle from a known liquidity event, or otherwise
  retire the private mortgage.
- A willingness to pay private-tier rates and fees because the alternative
  is worse (lost property, missed close, lost deal).

## Common product shapes

- Short terms: 6 months to 2 years is typical. 1-year terms are common.
- Interest-only or interest-accrued payment structures.
- Lender fees and broker fees are standard and disclosed up front.
- Higher rates than A or alt. The borrower trades cost for speed and
  flexibility.
- Open or short closed terms so the borrower can refinance out as soon as
  the exit triggers.

## What a private lender typically reviews

- The property: type, location, marketability, condition, recent
  comparables. An appraisal is almost always required.
- Equity position and loan-to-value (combined LTV when there are existing
  charges).
- Exit strategy and how realistic it is.
- The file's overall story: why private, why now, what changes in twelve
  months that solves it.
- Identity and authority to mortgage (lawyer or notary verification).

Credit and income are reviewed but rarely drive the decision.

## When a private mortgage is NOT the right fit

- Equity is too thin to support the lender's LTV ceiling for this position
  (first vs second vs third).
- No realistic exit strategy. Private mortgages are designed to be
  short-term; without an exit, the borrower ends up renewing at the same
  cost or losing the property.
- The file actually fits alt and the borrower would be much better off
  there. Going private when alt fits costs the borrower money for no
  reason.
- Property the private lender will not lend on (extremely remote, mixed-use
  beyond their appetite, environmental issues, title problems).

## Urgency framing

Private files often arrive against a hard clock: a power-of-sale notice, a
closing in days, a rate-hold expiring, or a private maturing. The
assistant should treat urgency as a signal to move quickly to a broker
call, not as a reason to skip the rest of the prequalification.

## What the assistant should and should not say

The assistant can explain what private lending is, why a borrower might
need it, what trade-offs are normal in this bucket (speed, flexibility,
higher rates and fees, short terms, exit-strategy emphasis), and that a
licensed broker confirms specifics. The assistant should not name specific
private lenders as a borrower's best fit, quote rates or fees, predict
approval, or imply that a private mortgage saves a property from a
specific outcome.
