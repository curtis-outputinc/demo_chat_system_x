# Private Mortgages: Frequently Asked Questions

Plain-language Q&A reference for the kinds of questions borrowers
actually ask about private mortgages. Synthesized from two AI-generated
sources (Gemini and ChatGPT), deduplicated semantically, with 50
additional borrower-facing questions added. The framework explanation
of private lending (when it fits, who it's for at a structural level)
lives in `private-lending.md`. The regulatory background (Criminal Code
s.347 interest ceiling, Ontario power of sale, B-20 treatment of
private mortgages as HELOCs) lives in the jurisdictional corpus files.

## How the agent should use this file

These Q&As give the agent background context for private-mortgage
conversations. The behaviors.md rule still applies: the agent should
NOT quote specific private-side rates, fees, or lender names to a
borrower in chat. When a borrower asks about specifics, the agent
should acknowledge the question, give general framework, and route
specifics to a licensed broker on the follow-up call.

Typical ranges noted below (8-12% private 1st rates, 3-5% setup fees,
$2,500-$4,000 legal, etc.) are general Ontario market context as of the
authoring date, not quotes. Real numbers vary by lender, file,
location, and date.

## Category 1: Private Mortgage Fundamentals

1. What is a private mortgage?
A private mortgage is a short-term, asset-based loan funded by a
private individual, a Mortgage Investment Corporation (MIC), or a
syndicate of investors, rather than by a traditional institutional
bank. It is usually a temporary financing solution, not a long-term
mortgage plan.

2. Who typically uses a private mortgage?
Borrowers facing serious credit issues, urgent timelines, income
challenges, tax debt, mortgage arrears, power-of-sale risk, unusual
property types, or a bank that recently backed out of a deal. Private
mortgages are often short-term bridges, not permanent financing.

3. Is private lending legal and regulated in Ontario?
Yes. Private lending is fully legal in Ontario. Mortgage brokering
activity is regulated, and brokers arranging private mortgages must be
licensed by the Financial Services Regulatory Authority of Ontario
(FSRA). Private mortgages themselves fall under the Ontario Mortgages
Act.

4. Is a private mortgage the same as a second mortgage?
Not necessarily. A private mortgage can be registered in first
position or second position. A first mortgage is the primary charge on
the property. A second mortgage is registered behind an existing first
mortgage.

5. What is a private second mortgage?
A private second mortgage is a loan secured behind an existing first
mortgage on the same property. It is often used to access equity
without breaking or paying penalties on a low-rate first mortgage from
a bank.

6. What is a Mortgage Investment Corporation (MIC)?
A MIC is a regulated corporate structure that pools capital from
multiple individual investors and lends it out as a portfolio of
diversified private mortgages. MICs are generally a more institutional
form of private lending and often offer slightly lower rates than
single-investor private deals.

7. What is a syndicated mortgage?
A syndicated mortgage is a single mortgage funded by multiple private
investors together, each holding a share of the same loan. Syndicates
are often used to fund larger transactions that no single investor
would take on alone.

8. How is a private mortgage different from a B-lender mortgage?
B-lenders are alternative institutional lenders (trust companies, some
banks, certain credit unions) that operate under more flexible criteria
than A-lenders but still underwrite based on income and credit. Private
mortgages are funded by individuals or MICs and are almost entirely
equity-based, with less weight on income and credit.

9. How is a private mortgage different from a credit union mortgage?
Credit unions are member-owned institutional lenders that often follow
similar underwriting standards to banks, though some are provincially
regulated and can offer more flexibility on income or credit. Private
lenders are individuals or MICs and operate outside the institutional
framework entirely.

10. What is a "no qualifying" mortgage?
"No qualifying" usually refers to a private mortgage that does not
require traditional income verification or credit-score qualification.
The lender focuses on the property's equity and the borrower's exit
plan rather than running standard bank-style debt-service ratios.

11. How is a private mortgage different from a HELOC?
A HELOC (home equity line of credit) is a revolving credit line
secured by your home, usually from a bank or credit union, that you
draw on as needed. A private mortgage is a one-time loan with a fixed
term and a defined payment schedule. HELOCs are usually cheaper if the
borrower qualifies.

## Category 2: Why People Use Private Mortgages

12. Why do people choose private mortgages if they are expensive?
Speed, flexibility, and accessibility. Private lenders can fund quickly,
they do not require traditional income verification, and they look past
weak credit, all of which matters when a borrower has run out of time
or has been declined by traditional and alternative lenders.

13. Is private lending always a last resort?
Not always. Sometimes it is the right tool for a short-term problem,
an urgent timeline, a bridge between a sale and a purchase, or a
temporary credit or income issue that will resolve within a year or
two. What matters is whether the cost, risk, and exit plan make sense.

14. Can I use a private mortgage to buy time to fix my credit?
Yes, that is a common use case. The borrower uses private capital for
a year to clear consumer proposals, pay down debts, or rebuild credit
history, then refinances to an A-lender or B-lender once the credit
profile improves.

15. Can I use a private mortgage to stop a power of sale?
Possibly, if there is enough equity and enough time. A new private
mortgage can fund quickly to pay out the foreclosing lender and save
the remaining equity. This kind of file is urgent and should reach a
broker immediately.

16. Can I get a private mortgage if I am behind on mortgage payments?
Possibly. Mortgage arrears are a common reason to explore private
lending. The lender will want to know the arrears amount, the legal
stage with the current lender, the property value, the current
mortgage balance, and the urgency.

17. Can I get a private mortgage if I owe CRA?
Possibly. Tax debt complicates the file, especially if CRA has
registered a lien against the property, but private lenders regularly
work with files involving outstanding CRA debt when the equity
supports it.

18. Can I use a private mortgage to pay off tax debt?
Yes, this is one of the most common uses of private equity loans.
Institutional banks rarely lend to clear large CRA balances; private
lenders will, provided there is sufficient equity.

19. Can I use a private mortgage to consolidate debt?
Yes, if there is enough equity. Consolidating high-interest unsecured
debt into a single equity-backed loan can lower monthly payment
pressure. The trade-off is that previously unsecured debt is now
secured against the home.

20. Can I use a private mortgage to pay off credit cards?
Yes. Rolling 20%+ credit card debt into a 10% interest-only private
mortgage can drastically reduce monthly out-of-pocket cost. The risk
is that missed payments now put the property at risk, not just credit.

21. Can I use a private mortgage for renovations?
Yes. Private funds may be used for renovations if there is enough
equity and a clear plan. The lender may want to know the renovation
budget, the current property value, and the estimated value after the
work is complete.

22. Can I use a private mortgage for new construction?
Yes. Private lenders fund construction more readily than banks
because they can disburse capital quickly via progress draws. The
lender may require plans, permits, builder information, land value,
appraisals, and a draw schedule.

23. Can a private mortgage be used for a fast property flip?
Yes. Real estate investors frequently use private capital to purchase
and renovate properties for resale because banks rarely offer
short-term flip-style financing.

24. Can I get a private loan if my property is currently listed for sale?
Yes. This is a bridge loan. The private lender funds short-term
capital and the loan is paid off automatically when the property sale
closes.

25. Can an estate use a private mortgage to pay off taxes before selling?
Yes. Executors often use private capital to clear estate-related debts
and to fund property updates that maximize the final sale price.

26. Why would someone use a private second mortgage instead of refinancing?
To avoid breaking a low-rate first mortgage and triggering a breakage
penalty. A private second mortgage layers new debt on top of the
existing first without touching it, which can be cheaper overall when
the first mortgage rate is well below current market rates.

## Category 3: Qualification, Credit, and Income

27. Does a private lender check my credit score?
The lender will pull your credit report to look for active
bankruptcies, consumer proposals, judgements, and any liens registered
against the property. A low credit score will not automatically
disqualify you, but it affects rate, fees, and which lenders will
participate.

28. Can I get a private mortgage with very bad credit?
Yes. Private lenders regularly fund files that A-lenders and B-lenders
decline due to credit. Equity, property location, property condition,
and the exit plan become the key qualifying factors.

29. Do I need to show job letters or pay stubs for a private mortgage?
Usually no. Private lending is equity-driven. Your property's value
and location matter far more than your employment status or pay
documentation.

30. Do private lenders care about income at all?
They care, but less than banks do. The lender still wants to understand
how the monthly interest payments will be made. If income is weak, the
lender will lean harder on equity cushion and the exit strategy.

31. Can I get a private mortgage if I am currently unemployed?
Yes, if there is a meaningful equity cushion (usually LTV well under
70%) and a bulletproof exit strategy explaining how the loan will be
repaid.

32. Can I get a private mortgage if I am self-employed?
Yes. Private lenders are typically more flexible than banks with
self-employed borrowers because the file is driven by property equity,
not declared income or notice of assessment averages.

33. Can I get a private mortgage without tax documents?
Possibly. Tax documents are not usually the main qualifying factor,
but the lender still wants to understand repayment ability and may
ask for bank statements or proof of cash flow instead.

34. Can I get a private mortgage if I was declined by the bank?
Yes. Bank declines are one of the most common reasons borrowers
explore private lending. Why the bank declined the file (credit,
income, debt ratios, property type, appraisal, documentation) shapes
how a private broker structures the next step.

35. Does a private lender require a co-signer?
Not usually. Because private lending is equity-driven, the
co-signer's income and credit are not central to qualification. A
co-signer may be required in specific situations the broker can
explain.

36. Can I get a private mortgage if my co-owner refuses to sign?
No. Every individual on the property's legal title must sign the
mortgage commitment and the legal documents for the loan to fund.

37. Can a spouse who is not on title help me qualify?
Generally no, because qualification is property-equity driven and the
spouse is not on title. The broker can advise on whether adding the
spouse to title is reasonable in your situation, though that is a
legal decision with implications beyond the mortgage.

## Category 4: Loan-to-Value (LTV) and Property Rules

38. What is the maximum LTV for a private mortgage?
There is no single legal maximum, but typical caps are 75-80% LTV in
major urban centres and 50-60% LTV in rural areas. Private second
mortgages combine with the first mortgage balance and are usually
capped at similar levels measured on a combined basis.

39. Can I get a private mortgage at 90% LTV?
Very difficult. Most private lenders are uncomfortable above 80% LTV,
and many cap lower depending on credit, property type, and location.
The risk profile at 90%+ usually pushes the file outside what private
capital will fund.

40. What loan-to-value do private lenders allow on a second mortgage?
There is no universal rule. Lenders set their own combined LTV limits
based on first mortgage balance, property value, borrower risk, and
property location. Higher combined LTV is harder to place and more
expensive.

41. What types of properties do private lenders avoid?
Highly remote raw land, environmentally contaminated sites, properties
with severe structural damage, properties with title problems, and
homes that would be difficult to sell quickly if the lender ever had
to enforce.

42. Can I get a private mortgage on a property in rural Ontario?
Yes, but expect lower LTV ceilings (typically 50-60%) and higher
interest rates because rural properties take longer to sell and the
resale market is less liquid.

43. Can I get a private mortgage on a condo?
Yes. Private lenders regularly fund condo files. Building age, condo
fees, special assessments, square footage, and resale market all
factor into the lender's view.

44. Can I get a private mortgage on vacant land?
Sometimes. Vacant land is harder to finance because it produces no
income and can be slower to sell. Expect lower LTV and higher cost
than a comparable file on a built property.

45. Can I get a private mortgage on a rental property?
Yes. Private lenders fund rental properties, though they may price
the loan differently than an owner-occupied file. They will review
the property value, the current rent, the existing mortgage, and the
overall equity position.

46. Do private lenders prefer owner-occupied homes or rentals?
Many private lenders prefer owner-occupied properties because they
are generally better maintained, which protects the lender's
collateral position. Both are routinely funded.

47. Can I get a private mortgage on a property in another province?
Possibly. Many private lenders are provincially scoped (especially
in Ontario, BC, and Alberta), so an out-of-province property may
require a lender that operates in that province. The broker can
advise on availability.

48. Can a private lender refuse to fund based on the appraisal?
Yes. If an independent appraisal reveals structural damage, safety
hazards, contamination, or a sale price significantly above
defensible market value, the lender can withdraw the commitment.

49. Why are private mortgage appraisals scrutinized so carefully?
The property is the lender's only safety net if a default occurs.
The lender must be confident the valuation is realistic and that the
property could be sold within a reasonable time at that value if
enforcement ever became necessary.

50. What happens if a property appraisal comes in lower than expected?
The lender recalculates the maximum LTV against the lower value, which
typically reduces the total cash that can be advanced. The borrower may
need to bring more equity, accept a smaller loan, or revise the deal.

## Category 5: Rates, Fees, and Total Cost

51. What are typical Ontario private mortgage interest rates?
Private first mortgage rates generally range between 8% and 12%, and
private second mortgage rates run higher, often between 10% and 15%.
The exact rate depends on equity position, file risk, property
location, and lender appetite at the time of funding.

52. Are private mortgage rates higher than bank rates?
Yes, almost always. Private rates reflect the higher risk the lender
is taking and the speed and flexibility the borrower is paying for.

53. Are private mortgage rates negotiable?
Sometimes. Stronger files (low LTV, urban property, clean title) have
more leverage to negotiate rate and fees than weaker files. A broker
shopping multiple lenders is the best way to compare.

54. What are the typical setup fees on a private mortgage?
Setup fees typically total 3-5% of the loan amount and include lender
onboarding fees, broker compensation, and legal processing. The exact
breakdown is disclosed before signing.

55. How is the lender fee paid?
The lender fee is usually deducted directly from the mortgage advance
at closing, so the borrower receives the loan amount minus the fee.
There is no separate cash payment at closing for the lender fee.

56. How is the broker compensated on a private mortgage?
The broker may be paid through a broker fee that is deducted from the
loan advance, through a fee from the lender, or sometimes through a
combination. Broker compensation must be disclosed before the borrower
signs.

57. Why do I have to pay for two separate lawyers on a private deal?
Under Ontario law, the borrower must have independent legal
representation that is separate from the lender's lawyer. This ensures
both parties have advice that is not conflicted by representing the
other side.

58. How much should I budget for legal fees on a private mortgage?
Budget approximately $2,500 to $4,000 to cover both your personal legal
fees and the lender's closing legal fees combined. Complex files may
run higher.

59. Is there an absolute legal limit on private mortgage interest rates?
Yes. Section 347 of the Criminal Code of Canada caps the effective
annual cost of borrowing (interest plus all fees) at 35%. Charging
above that threshold is a criminal offence.

60. Can a private lender charge late payment fees?
Yes. Late payment administrative fees and default interest rate
adjustments apply if payments miss their scheduled due dates. The fee
amounts are set out in the mortgage commitment.

61. Is title insurance required on a private mortgage?
Yes, almost always. Title insurance protects the lender against title
defects, zoning issues, and fraudulent property claims. The borrower
typically pays for it as part of closing costs.

62. Is the interest on a private mortgage tax-deductible?
Sometimes. Mortgage interest is generally tax-deductible when the
borrowed funds are used to earn investment income or for a business
purpose, not when used for personal expenses. A tax professional
should confirm what applies to your situation.

63. Are private mortgage payments reported to my credit bureau?
Sometimes. Not every private lender reports to the credit bureaus.
MICs and larger private operations are more likely to report than
individual investors. The lender or broker can confirm.

64. Will having a private mortgage hurt my credit?
A private mortgage by itself does not hurt your credit. Missed
payments hurt your credit, regardless of who the lender is. If the
lender reports to the bureau and payments are on time, the file may
actually help by adding paid-as-agreed history.

65. Can a private mortgage help me rebuild credit?
Yes, indirectly. If the private mortgage frees up cash flow by
consolidating high-interest debt, and the borrower then pays down
remaining balances and avoids new late payments, credit can rebuild
during the term, often enough to qualify for an A or B lender at
renewal.

## Category 6: Term, Payments, and Structure

66. What is the standard term length for a private mortgage?
Most private mortgage terms are 12 months. Six-month and 24-month
terms are also possible depending on the borrower's transition needs
and the lender's policy.

67. How are private mortgage payments calculated?
Most private mortgages are structured as interest-only, meaning the
monthly payment covers only the interest cost and does not reduce the
principal balance.

68. What does an "interest-only" payment plan look like in practice?
Each month the borrower pays just the interest on the outstanding
balance. Monthly payments are lower than a principal-and-interest loan
of the same size, but the full principal is still owed at the end of
the term.

69. Can I choose to pay principal on a private mortgage?
Sometimes. It is not the default structure, but principal pay-down
privileges can be negotiated into the commitment letter when cash flow
permits. The broker can ask the lender during structuring.

70. Can I lock in a fixed rate for the full term of my private mortgage?
Usually yes. Most private mortgages are fixed rate for the full term
(typically 12 months). Variable-rate private mortgages exist but are
less common.

71. Can private mortgage payments be made weekly or biweekly?
Usually no. Most private mortgages require monthly interest payments.
Some lenders may accommodate other frequencies; the broker can ask.

72. Does my private mortgage payment include property taxes?
Usually no. Private mortgages typically do not collect property taxes
through the lender. The borrower remains responsible for paying
property taxes directly to the municipality.

73. What is a balloon payment on a private mortgage?
The balloon payment is the lump sum owed at the end of the term to
clear the outstanding balance. With an interest-only structure, the
balloon is the full original principal, paid through refinance, sale,
or other exit at term end.

74. Can I pay off a private mortgage early?
Most private mortgages are closed for the first 3 to 6 months, meaning
early payout triggers a prepayment penalty or interest-to-end-of-closed-
period charge. After the closed period, many become open or partly
open. The commitment letter spells out the rules.

75. Are there prepayment penalties on a private mortgage?
Yes, typically during the closed portion of the term. The penalty is
usually some number of months' interest. After the closed period, the
mortgage may be open with no penalty, or have lighter notice
requirements.

## Category 7: Exit Strategy

76. What is an exit strategy?
An exit strategy is the borrower's concrete plan to repay or replace
the private mortgage within the term. Common exits: sell the property,
refinance to a bank or B-lender, complete construction and move to
take-out financing, receive an expected lump sum (settlement, sale of
another asset), or rebuild credit and qualify for traditional
refinance.

77. Why does the private lender care about my exit strategy?
Because private mortgages are short-term and expensive. Without a
viable exit, the borrower can get stuck renewing repeatedly, paying
fees each time, and slowly eroding their equity. Lenders and reputable
brokers will not arrange a private mortgage without a plausible exit.

78. Can I get a private mortgage without a clear exit strategy?
Reputable brokers and lenders will refuse a file without a viable exit
plan. Arranging a private mortgage that traps the borrower in
high-cost debt indefinitely is considered predatory.

79. What happens if my exit strategy fails at the end of the 12 months?
You either request a term extension from the same lender (typically
with a renewal fee around 1% of the loan), seek another private
lender to take out the loan, or face default. The broker should
revisit your file 60-90 days before maturity to plan the exit.

80. Can I refinance out of a private mortgage into a bank mortgage later?
Yes, when you qualify with an A-lender or B-lender. The goal during
the private mortgage term is often to do exactly that: improve credit,
build verifiable income history, reduce other debts, increase equity,
and then move to cheaper traditional financing.

81. Can I sell my home to pay off the private mortgage?
Yes. Selling the property is a common and clean exit strategy. The
mortgage is paid out from sale proceeds at closing.

82. Can I renew my private mortgage at the end of the term?
Possibly, but renewal is not guaranteed. The lender may grant an
extension, change the rate or fees, or refuse. This is why a real exit
plan matters before entering the loan.

83. What is the risk of rolling into multiple private renewals?
The high rates and fees can slowly erode home equity if the borrower
stays in private financing year after year without graduating to a
cheaper lender. Multiple renewals are a warning sign that the exit
strategy is not working.

## Category 8: Default and Risks

84. What happens if I default on a private mortgage?
The lender may charge default fees, demand payment in full, or begin
legal enforcement against the property. Because the loan is secured
by your home, missed payments can put the property at risk through
power of sale or foreclosure.

85. Can a private lender take my house?
If a borrower defaults, the lender has legal remedies that can include
power of sale or foreclosure. The specific process depends on the
mortgage documents and Ontario law. A borrower facing default should
seek legal advice and contact the broker immediately.

86. What is power of sale in Ontario?
Power of sale is the legal remedy that allows a lender to seize and
sell the property to recover what is owed after a borrower defaults,
following statutory notice timelines set by the Ontario Mortgages Act.

87. How long after I miss a payment can a private lender start legal action?
Under the Ontario Mortgages Act, a lender can formally issue a Notice
of Sale once a payment has been in default for 15 days. The Notice
of Sale starts a further statutory notice period before the sale can
actually proceed.

88. What happens if I default on a private second mortgage?
The private second mortgage holder can initiate power of sale or
foreclosure proceedings even if the first mortgage is fully current.
The first mortgage is paid out from sale proceeds first; the private
second is paid out second.

89. Can a private lender call my loan early?
Generally no, unless the loan agreement specifically provides for a
demand right or the borrower has triggered a default condition (missed
payments, failure to maintain insurance, failure to pay property
taxes, sale of the property without notice, etc.).

90. What if I lose my job during a private mortgage term?
Because qualification was equity-driven, job loss does not
automatically default the loan as long as you can still make the
monthly interest payment. If you cannot make payments, contact the
broker immediately to discuss restructuring or an earlier exit.

91. What if my home value drops during the term?
A decline in home value does not by itself default the loan, but it
may affect the broker's ability to find a take-out lender at maturity.
The lender may also recalculate LTV at renewal time.

92. What if the private lender goes bankrupt during my term?
The mortgage itself survives the lender's bankruptcy. The loan would
typically be administered by a trustee or assigned to another party,
who would continue to collect payments under the existing terms. The
borrower's obligations do not change.

93. What happens to my private mortgage if I die during the term?
The mortgage becomes a debt of the estate. The executor either pays
out the loan from estate funds, sells the property to repay it, or
refinances. Title insurance and any life insurance the borrower
arranged separately may affect the outcome.

94. What are the main risks of taking a private mortgage?
Higher rates and fees than traditional lending, shorter terms with
renewal uncertainty, the property being at risk if payments are
missed, equity erosion over time if the borrower cannot exit, and the
prepayment penalty during the closed portion of the term.

## Category 9: Process, Documents, and Closing

95. How fast can a private mortgage close?
With a current appraisal and clean title, a nimble private lender can
close in as little as 3 to 5 business days. Standard timelines are 1
to 3 weeks. Urgent files should be flagged to the broker immediately.

96. What documents are typically needed for a private mortgage?
Common documents: government ID, mortgage statement, recent property
tax bill, home insurance proof, an appraisal (or willingness to order
one), payout statements for any existing debts being cleared, basic
income information, and details on the purpose of funds. Files
involving arrears or legal issues may need more.

97. Do I always need an appraisal for a private mortgage?
Almost always, yes. The property's value drives the loan, so the
lender wants an independent appraisal confirming what the property is
worth.

98. Do I always need a lawyer for a private mortgage?
Yes. Mortgage transactions secured against real estate require legal
work to register the charge against the property, transfer funds, and
discharge any debts being paid out at closing.

99. Can the broker arrange a private mortgage directly?
A licensed mortgage broker or agent sources private mortgage options
through their brokerage, subject to suitability rules and required
disclosures. The borrower receives clear information about costs,
risks, and terms before signing.

100. Can I get pre-approval for a private mortgage?
Pre-approval is less formal in private lending than in bank lending.
A broker can give a strong indication of what is available once they
have the basic file (property value, mortgage balances, equity, credit
overview), but a firm commitment usually requires the full document
package.

101. What is "subject to financing" in a private mortgage offer?
"Subject to financing" is a condition in a purchase agreement that
gives the buyer time to arrange financing before the deal becomes
firm. The buyer can use private financing to satisfy a financing
condition the same way they would use any other lender.

102. How long does it take to discharge a private mortgage after I pay it off?
Discharge timing varies. The lender's lawyer prepares and registers
the discharge, typically within 30-60 days of payout. The borrower's
lawyer can confirm the registered discharge to ensure clear title.

103. What is "second position" in mortgage terminology?
"Second position" or "second-ranking" means the mortgage is registered
behind another, earlier-registered mortgage. If the property is ever
sold under enforcement, the first-position mortgage is paid out first
and the second is paid out from what remains.

104. Can a private mortgage be transferred or assigned to a new lender?
Yes, in many cases. The original lender can assign the mortgage to
another investor or institution. The borrower's terms typically do not
change; the new holder collects payments under the existing agreement.

105. Can the private lender modify my loan terms after closing?
Generally no, not unilaterally. Both parties must agree to any
modification of the loan terms. A typical reason for modification
mid-term is an extension request close to maturity.

## Category 10: Specialized Scenarios and Comparisons

106. What is a blanket mortgage in private lending?
A blanket mortgage is a single loan secured against multiple
properties owned by the borrower. It can let the borrower access a
larger total loan amount by pooling collateral across several
properties.

107. Can I wrap multiple debts into a single private mortgage?
Yes. Private mortgages are frequently used to consolidate
high-interest unsecured debt, tax debt, judgements, and other
obligations into a single equity-backed loan with one monthly payment.

108. Can a private mortgage cover both purchase and renovations?
Yes. A private lender can structure a single mortgage that funds the
purchase price plus a renovation budget, often released via progress
draws as work is completed.

109. Can I use a private mortgage instead of a HELOC?
Sometimes. If you cannot qualify for a HELOC because of income,
credit, or bank rules, a private second mortgage may be considered.
However, when a HELOC is available, it is usually cheaper.

110. If I already have a HELOC, can I get a private second mortgage too?
Possibly, but the lender must account for the full HELOC limit (not
just the balance drawn) when measuring combined LTV, because the
borrower could draw more later. This typically reduces the amount a
private lender will advance.

111. Can my parents or a family member be the private lender?
Yes, family members can act as private lenders. The transaction
should still be properly documented with a registered mortgage and
involve legal advice on both sides to protect the family
relationship and ensure enforceability.

112. Are private mortgages more like loans from friends and family?
They can be, when the lender is an individual investor. MICs and
syndicated mortgages are more institutional. Either way, the legal
documents make the loan enforceable through standard mortgage
remedies.

113. How is a private mortgage different from a "hard money loan"?
"Hard money loan" is the American term for the same concept: a
short-term, asset-based loan funded by a private lender. The Canadian
term is "private mortgage." The structure and purpose are similar.

114. Can a private lender require a specific insurance company?
The lender can require the borrower to maintain home insurance with
the lender named as loss payee, but the borrower can usually choose
the insurer. Confirm specifics in the commitment letter.

115. Do I have to keep the property insured during the private mortgage?
Yes, always. Maintaining home insurance with the lender named as loss
payee is a standard mortgage requirement. Failure to maintain
insurance is typically a default condition.

116. Who pays the property taxes during a private mortgage?
The borrower. Private lenders typically do not collect property taxes
through the mortgage payment, so the borrower remains responsible for
paying the municipality directly and providing proof if requested.

117. Can a private lender require I sell the property?
Not unless the loan is in default and the lender is exercising
enforcement rights. During a current-and-performing loan, the lender
cannot force a sale. The borrower can choose to sell, which would
pay out the loan.

118. What happens if I sell my property while the private mortgage is active?
The mortgage is paid out from sale proceeds at closing, including any
prepayment penalty that applies. The lawyer arranges the payout and
the lender issues a discharge.

119. Can I take out a second private mortgage on top of an existing one?
Yes, in theory. The new third-position lender (or wherever it ranks)
would need to be comfortable with the combined LTV and the existing
charges. Stacking is harder and more expensive than a single position.

120. Can I have multiple private mortgages on the same property?
Yes, ranked by registration order (first, second, third, etc.). Each
subsequent position is riskier and usually more expensive because it
recovers from sale proceeds only after the prior-ranking charges are
paid out.

121. What is the most important step when taking a private mortgage?
Working with a licensed broker to build a clear, realistic exit
strategy before signing. The exit plan determines whether the private
mortgage protects your equity or slowly erodes it.

122. Are interest rates on private mortgages expressed the same way as bank rates?
Yes, as an annual percentage. However, the effective cost when fees
are included can be substantially higher than the headline rate. The
broker should disclose the all-in effective cost.

123. Does taking a private mortgage prevent me from getting future credit?
No, not by itself. Future credit decisions depend on credit history,
income, and debt-service ratios at the time you apply. A private
mortgage paid as agreed does not block future borrowing.

124. Will the private mortgage broker shop multiple lenders for me?
Yes, that is a key reason to use a licensed broker rather than going
direct to an individual lender. The broker compares multiple private
options to find the best rate, fees, and terms for your situation.
