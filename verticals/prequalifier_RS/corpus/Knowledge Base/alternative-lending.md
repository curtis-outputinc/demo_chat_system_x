# Alternative (B-lender / MIC) mortgages

This file describes alternative mortgages at a general, educational level
so the assistant can explain them in plain language. Specific rates,
qualifying amounts, payment estimates, lender names, and approval guidance
never go here.

## What alternative lending is

Alternative lending is the lending tier between A-lenders and private
mortgages. It is where borrowers go when an A-lender's standardized rules
do not fit but the file still has real strength: stable property, decent
equity or down payment, and a story a B-lender or MIC can understand.

Two common kinds of alternative lender:

- B-lenders: trust companies and specialty banks that lend on more
  flexible income and credit criteria than A-lenders, with slightly higher
  rates and lender or broker fees.
- MICs (Mortgage Investment Corporations): pooled-capital lenders that
  underwrite on the strength of the property and the file's overall story.
  Generally faster than B-lenders, with higher rates and fees again.

## Borrower profile that typically fits

- Self-employed borrowers without two years of clean notices of
  assessment, or with significant tax write-downs.
- Commission, contract, or recently changed income.
- Bruised credit: recent late payments, collections, a discharged
  bankruptcy or proposal, or a low score from limited credit history.
- New-to-Canada or new-to-US borrowers with limited credit history.
- Borrowers using foreign or non-standard income sources.
- Files with strong equity in the property but a weaker borrower side.
- Borrowers who need a shorter term to bridge back to A in a year or two.

## Common product shapes

- 1- and 2-year terms more common than 3- and 5-year on the alt side.
- Slightly higher rates than A-lenders. Lender fees and broker fees are
  common and disclosed up front.
- Sometimes interest-only or partial-pay structures.
- Refinances and equity takeouts are a frequent use case.
- Second mortgages behind an A-lender first.

## What an alternative lender typically reviews

Equity position and loan-to-value, property strength and marketability, the
overall story of why the file does not fit A right now, exit strategy
(usually a return to A at renewal), and credit and income at a more
flexible standard. Documentation requirements are lighter than A but still
real.

## When an alternative lender is NOT the right fit

- Equity position too thin for the bucket's loan-to-value limits.
- Property type or market the alt lenders avoid.
- Borrower needs to fund in days, faster than alt underwriting cycles.
- Open collections or recent events the alt lender will not look past at
  current LTV.

When a file does not fit alt, the natural next step is a private mortgage.

## Exit strategy framing

A healthy alt-side file usually has an exit plan: clean up credit, build
two years of NOA, pay down debts, and move back to an A-lender at renewal.
The assistant can explain this in general terms. It should not promise a
specific timeline or that any specific lender will take the file later.

## What the assistant should and should not say

The assistant can explain what alternative lending is, when a file
typically fits, what trade-offs are normal in this bucket (rate, fees,
shorter term, exit plan), and that a licensed broker confirms specifics.
The assistant should not name specific B-lenders or MICs as a borrower's
best fit, quote rates or fees, or predict approval.
