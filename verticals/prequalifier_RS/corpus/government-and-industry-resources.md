# Government and industry reference content

This file aggregates reference material from official sources that
the bot can draw on for accurate answers about federal homebuyer
programs, the CMHC Purchase mortgage insurance program, and the
REALTOR designation. Each section names the source URL so we can
re-verify if numbers change.

## CMHC Purchase program (cmhc-schl.gc.ca)

Source: cmhc-schl.gc.ca/professionals/project-funding-and-mortgage-financing/mortgage-loan-insurance/mortgage-loan-insurance-homeownership-programs/purchase

### What CMHC Purchase is

CMHC Purchase is the federal mortgage default insurance program
that enables Canadian homebuyers to purchase a home with a minimum
down payment of 5%. The program protects the lender if the
borrower defaults, which is how borrowers with less than 20% down
qualify for a mortgage at all.

### Who qualifies

Applicants must be Canadian citizens, permanent residents of
Canada, or non-permanent residents legally authorized to work in
Canada. Non-permanent residents need a work permit, and the
purchase cannot violate the Prohibition on the Purchase of
Residential Property by Non-Canadians Act.

### Property eligibility

Properties must be located in Canada and suitable for full-time
year-round occupancy. The home must have year-round vehicular
access (including homes on islands served by bridge or ferry).
Properties may be 1 to 4 units, owner-occupied by the borrower or
a legally related household member on a rent-free basis.

### Down payment minimums (tiered by purchase price)

- First $500,000 of lending value: minimum 5% down.
- Remainder above $500,000: minimum 10% down.

Worked example: a $760,000 home needs $51,000 down. That is 5% on
the first $500,000 ($25,000) plus 10% on the remaining $260,000
($26,000). The conventional 20% down on the same home would be
$152,000.

### Maximum LTV (loan-to-value)

- 1 to 2 units, owner-occupied: up to 95% LTV.
- 3 to 4 units, owner-occupied: up to 90% LTV.

### Maximum purchase price

The maximum lending value or as-improved property value for
homeowner loans under this program is $1,500,000.

### Amortization

Standard maximum amortization is 25 years. Some first-time buyers
may qualify for a 30-year amortization through CMHC Home Start
(separate program overlay). 30-year amortization on insured
mortgages is also available for first-time buyers purchasing
newly built homes, per the 2024 federal change.

### Down payment sources

- Traditional: savings, proceeds from selling another property,
  non-repayable gifts from relatives.
- Non-traditional (only for 1 to 2-unit properties at 90.01-95%
  LTV with strong credit and Canadian permanent residency or
  citizenship): unsecured personal loans or lines of credit, arm's
  length, not tied to the property purchase.

### Mortgage insurance premium schedule

Premiums are charged as a percentage of the total insured loan
amount and may be added to the mortgage or paid up front.

- LTV up to 65%: 0.60%
- LTV 65.01% to 75%: 1.70%
- LTV 75.01% to 80%: 2.40%
- LTV 80.01% to 85%: 2.80%
- LTV 85.01% to 90%: 3.10%
- LTV 90.01% to 95% (traditional down payment): 4.00%
- LTV 90.01% to 95% (non-traditional down payment): 4.50%

### Debt service ratios

- Gross Debt Service (GDS): maximum 39%.
- Total Debt Service (TDS): maximum 44%.

GDS and TDS calculations must use a qualifying interest rate equal
to the greater of (contract rate + 2%) or 5.25%. That second
number is the mortgage qualifying rate (MQR) used as a stress test
floor.

### Credit requirements

At least one borrower or guarantor must have a minimum credit
score of 600. CMHC may consider alternative methods of
demonstrating creditworthiness for borrowers without a Canadian
credit history.

### Advance options

- Single advance: available when improvement costs are 10% or
  less of the as-improved value.
- Progress advances: used for new construction or improvements
  greater than 10% of as-improved value. CMHC offers Full Service
  (validates up to 4 consecutive advances at no cost) or Basic
  Service (lender validates without pre-approval).

### Rental income

The program has specific guidance on how rental income is treated
in the debt service ratio calculation, depending on owner-occupancy
and the MLI application type. Refer the borrower to a broker for
specifics; do not generalize.

### CMHC homeowner underwriting contact

For lenders and brokers: CMHC Homeowner Underwriting Centre,
1-888-463-6454 (1-888-Go-emili). Borrowers do not call this number
directly; they go through their broker or lender.

### Important caveat

This program detail is subject to change. The premium schedule,
LTV limits, maximum purchase price, and stress test floor can be
revised by CMHC, OSFI, or the federal Department of Finance. The
bot should treat the figures above as accurate at the time of
writing but should encourage the visitor to confirm specifics with
a broker for the date they intend to close.

## Government of Canada programs for homebuyers (cmhc-schl.gc.ca)

Source: cmhc-schl.gc.ca/consumers/home-buying/government-of-canada-programs-to-support-homebuyers

This CMHC page is a central index of the federal financial
incentives available to homebuyers. As CMHC puts it, "The
Government of Canada offers a number of financial incentives to
help you throughout the homebuying journey."

### Home Buyers' Amount (federal tax credit)

A federal non-refundable tax credit for first-time homebuyers that
reduces the federal income tax owed in the year of purchase. The
buyer claims it on their tax return. Details and eligibility are
administered by the Canada Revenue Agency.

### Home Buyers' Plan (HBP)

The HBP lets first-time homebuyers withdraw funds from their RRSP
to put toward purchasing or constructing a qualifying home, for
themselves or for a specified disabled person. The withdrawal is
not taxed if the rules are followed, but it must be repaid to the
RRSP within a 15-year period.

### First Home Savings Account (FHSA)

A registered plan designed for first-time homebuyers to save
toward their first home. Contributions are generally tax-deductible
(like an RRSP) and qualifying withdrawals are tax-free (like a
TFSA). It combines the best of both. The FHSA has annual and
lifetime contribution limits and specific eligibility rules; the
bot should defer to the CRA's official page for exact figures and
to a broker for how it fits the buyer's plan.

### GST/HST New Housing Rebate

Eligible buyers may claim a rebate on GST or HST paid when
purchasing newly constructed homes, substantially renovating an
existing property, or converting a non-residential building into
a residential home. Specific thresholds, partial-rebate price
ranges, and eligibility (including the federal first-time buyer
GST/HST rebate where applicable) are administered by the CRA.

## What a REALTOR is (CREA)

Source: crea.ca/who-we-are/the-realtor-difference

### What CREA is

CREA is the Canadian Real Estate Association. Only members of
CREA are entitled to use the REALTOR trademark. The trademark is
not a generic word for a real estate agent in Canada; it
identifies CREA members specifically.

### REALTOR versus real estate agent

Not every licensed or registered broker or salesperson is a
REALTOR. To use the REALTOR designation, an agent must be a CREA
member and adhere to CREA's standards and the REALTOR Code of
Ethics.

### What CREA requires of REALTORS

CREA members commit to following the REALTOR Code of Ethics, the
accepted standard of conduct for all real estate practitioners
who are REALTORS. CREA members are also expected to maintain
current knowledge of market conditions, comparable prices, and
neighbourhood trends; to participate in ongoing professional
education through courses and workshops; to cooperate through
MLS Systems, which enable cooperative property sales; and to
comply with anti-money laundering rules (FINTRAC obligations).

### Why this matters for a mortgage chat conversation

A mortgage agent and a REALTOR play different roles in a home
purchase. The REALTOR represents the buyer or seller in the
transaction itself (finding the home, negotiating the offer,
managing the closing on the real estate side). The mortgage
agent arranges the financing. They work alongside each other,
not in place of each other. If a visitor asks whether they need a
REALTOR, the right answer is yes (for the purchase side) and the
mortgage agent is happy to coordinate with the REALTOR they
choose.
