# What happens: process and timelines

## The free consultation

A consultation usually means reviewing the basic facts, explaining the options,
answering questions, and deciding whether the firm can help. There is no
obligation to hire the firm afterward.

## After someone reaches out

When a visitor submits their details or books a call, the firm follows up based
on its availability to talk through the situation. Submitting details puts the
visitor in the queue for the team to review.

## How a case generally moves

If the firm takes a case, the lawyer handles the legal work: investigating what
happened, gathering evidence, dealing with the insurance companies, and
negotiating on the client's behalf. The client provides information and attends
appointments, but the firm manages the legal process.

A claim often moves through stages like medical treatment and recovery, an
investigation, a demand to the insurer or other party, negotiation, and, if a
fair resolution is not reached, a lawsuit. Even after a lawsuit is filed, many
cases settle. Some go through mediation, where both sides meet with a neutral
person to try to agree. Few cases reach a trial.

Plain-language terms the assistant can explain if asked: a demand letter is a
formal request that lays out the facts and the compensation sought. Discovery is
the stage where both sides exchange information. A deposition is a formal
question-and-answer session under oath. A settlement is an agreed resolution
without a trial. A verdict is a decision made by a judge or jury.

## How long it takes

Timelines vary widely depending on the injuries, the recovery, the evidence, the
insurance issues, and whether the matter settles or goes further. The firm can
give a better estimate after reviewing the details. The assistant does not give
a specific time estimate.

## After a settlement

When a settlement is reached, documents are signed and the matter is generally
final, so it is important to understand what is being signed. Fees and case
expenses are handled out of the recovery, and in some situations outstanding
medical bills or insurer reimbursements may apply. The firm explains all of this
before anything is signed.
