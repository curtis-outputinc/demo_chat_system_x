# Offers, negotiation, and closing

**Q: How do I submit an official offer on a house?**
The agent prepares a formal purchase agreement detailing price, deposit, closing date, included items, conditions, and any special terms, then submits it to the seller.

**Q: What should be included in an offer?**
Price, deposit amount, closing date, items included or excluded, financing and inspection conditions, document review conditions if applicable, and any special clauses. The agent walks you through each piece.

**Q: How long does a seller have to respond to my offer?**
The offer includes an expiration deadline. The seller can accept, reject, counter, or let it expire. Typical windows are short.

**Q: Can a seller accept another offer while negotiating my counter-offer?**
Yes. A counter-offer technically restarts the negotiation, leaving the seller free to take a better offer if one arrives. The agent walks through timing carefully.

**Q: What is an escalation clause?**
A clause that automatically raises your offer above any competing verified offer by a set increment, up to a maximum ceiling. Whether to use one depends on the situation and local norms.

**Q: What is a contingency or offer condition?**
A condition that must be satisfied for the deal to firm up. Common examples include financing, inspection, document review, and insurance.

**Q: What is a firm offer?**
A firm offer has no remaining conditions. Once accepted, both sides are usually committed to closing.

**Q: Should I make a conditional offer?**
That depends on your comfort, the market, and the property. Conditions protect you but can reduce competitiveness. The agent walks through trade-offs.

**Q: What is a bidding war?**
A situation where multiple buyers compete for the same home. Bidding wars can drive up price or strengthen terms; staying inside your maximum is the key discipline.

**Q: How do I compete in a multiple-offer situation?**
You can compete on price, deposit size, closing flexibility, fewer conditions, or terms that match the seller's needs. The agent helps craft a strategy that fits your situation.

**Q: Should I offer over asking price?**
Not always. Asking price is a strategy, not a value. The agent reviews comparable sales and local demand before recommending an offer price.

**Q: What is a deposit?**
The deposit is money you provide after an offer is accepted to show commitment. It is usually applied to the purchase price at closing. Amounts, timing, and refund rules depend on the contract and the jurisdiction.

**Q: Is my deposit refundable?**
That depends on the contract terms and whether conditions are still in place. The agent and lawyer should walk through specifics before you submit.

**Q: What is a counter-offer?**
A response to an offer that modifies one or more terms (price, closing date, conditions, items included) and sends the negotiation back to the other side.

**Q: What if my financing condition expires before my loan is approved?**
If the deadline passes without an extension, you may be at risk of breaching the contract and losing the deposit if your loan is denied later. The agent tracks these dates carefully.

**Q: What are seller concessions?**
Financial credits the seller agrees to provide, often to cover some closing costs or to buy down the buyer's rate. They are negotiated as part of the offer.

**Q: What is a home inspection negotiation?**
If an inspection turns up issues, the buyer may request repairs, a price reduction, or a closing credit. Local norms shape what is typical.

**Q: What is a legal deed?**
A signed document that officially transfers ownership of a property from seller to buyer.

**Q: What is the difference between a deed and a title?**
Title is the legal right of ownership. The deed is the document used to transfer that right.

**Q: What is a quitclaim deed?**
A type of deed that transfers whatever interest the seller has in a property without any warranties. Often used between family members or in divorces.

**Q: What happens on closing day?**
Final documents are signed, funds are transferred, the title is updated, and possession is handed over per the contract. Exact steps vary by jurisdiction.

**Q: What is a Closing Disclosure?**
A document the lender provides before closing that lists your final loan terms, payments, and closing costs. Specific names and timing vary by country.

**Q: What is a short sale?**
A sale in which the home is sold for less than the remaining mortgage balance, with the lender accepting the loss. Short sales require lender approval and take longer.

**Q: What is a foreclosure?**
A legal process in which a lender takes back a property after the borrower stops making mortgage payments, typically followed by a sale to recover losses.

**Q: What is a backup offer?**
A secondary offer that steps into first position if the original contract falls through. The buyer is usually not bound until that happens.

**Q: What is a cloud on title?**
An unresolved claim or registration on the title that creates uncertainty about ownership. It can delay closing until resolved.

**Q: What is a wire fraud warning at closing?**
Scammers may send fake wire instructions to redirect funds. Always verify wire instructions by phone using a trusted number before sending money.

**Q: What is dual agency?**
A situation where one agent or brokerage represents both buyer and seller. Rules vary by jurisdiction and require written consent. The agent explains the local rules and risks.

**Q: What does "possession date" mean?**
The day and time you are entitled to take physical possession. Often closing day, but not always.

**Q: What is a home warranty?**
An annual service contract that covers repair or replacement of major systems and appliances. Whether one makes sense depends on the home, your risk tolerance, and the coverage terms.

**Q: What is an appraisal contingency?**
A condition that protects the buyer if the property appraises below the agreed price. The buyer can renegotiate or walk away within the terms.

**Q: What is a right of first refusal?**
A contractual right that lets a specific party (a tenant, an HOA, a family member) match an offer and buy a property before it can be sold to someone else.

**Q: What is a lowball offer?**
An offer well below market value. It can offend sellers and stall negotiation; sometimes it is a starting point, depending on the situation.

**Q: Can I buy a home with cash instead of a mortgage?**
Yes. Cash offers can be more attractive to sellers because they remove financing risk and may close faster. Cash buyers still need due diligence and title review.

**Q: What happens if a seller backs out of a signed contract?**
If a seller breaks a firm contract without legal grounds, the buyer may have legal options to recover costs or pursue specific performance. Consult the agent and lawyer immediately.

**Q: What can delay closing?**
Lender or financing issues, missing documents, title problems, late funds, inspection or repair disagreements, or insurance issues. Most delays are solvable with quick communication.

**Q: What utilities should I set up before moving in?**
Electricity, gas, water, internet, possibly cable, security, and waste services depending on the property.

**Q: What should I do before moving into a new home?**
Set up utilities, arrange insurance, book movers, update your address, review closing documents, plan cleaning, and confirm key pickup.

**Q: What happens if something breaks after closing?**
It depends on the contract, the disclosure, any warranties, and the nature of the issue. For significant problems, speak with the agent and lawyer.

**Q: What items are usually included in a home sale?**
Permanently attached items (lighting fixtures, built-ins) usually stay. Appliances and movable items are negotiable. The contract lists what is in and out.

**Q: What is title insurance?**
Insurance that helps protect against certain title-related risks discovered after closing. Coverage varies by policy and jurisdiction.

**Q: When do I get the keys?**
Usually on the agreed possession date, after closing is complete and funds have transferred. Specifics depend on the contract.

**Q: What does title mean?**
Title refers to legal ownership of the property. A title search confirms current ownership and surfaces liens or claims.

**Q: Do I need a real estate lawyer or settlement professional?**
In many places, yes. They review documents, handle title transfer, register the property, and manage closing funds.

**Q: How is closing handled in the U.S. vs. Canada?**
In the U.S., closings often involve attorneys, title companies, escrow companies, or settlement agents depending on the state. In Canada, including Ontario, real estate lawyers usually handle title transfer, mortgage registration, and closing.
