# Buying: getting started

**Q: How do I start the home buying process?**
The first step is to understand your budget. Most buyers start with a pre-approval conversation with a mortgage professional and then set search criteria with the agent. From there it's looking at listings, shortlisting, viewings, offer, due diligence, and closing.

**Q: Do I need a pre-approval before looking at homes?**
It is strongly recommended. A pre-approval helps you understand your price range and shows sellers you are a serious buyer.

**Q: What is the difference between pre-qualification and pre-approval?**
Pre-qualification is usually a quick estimate based on self-reported information. Pre-approval is a more formal review of your credit and documents and gives a stronger signal to sellers. Final approval can still depend on the property and underwriting.

**Q: Can I buy a home without a real estate agent?**
You can, but an agent helps with pricing, negotiation, paperwork, scheduling, and avoiding mistakes. Buyer representation usually adds protection on the buyer's side of the deal.

**Q: How much money do I need to buy a home?**
You will need a down payment, closing costs, and a buffer for inspections, moving, and early expenses. The exact amount depends on the home price, loan type, and location. The agent can walk through a realistic budget during a consultation.

**Q: What are closing costs?**
Closing costs are the various professional and administrative fees paid at the end of a transaction. They commonly include legal or title fees, transfer taxes, lender fees, and a few prepaid items. The exact mix and amount depend on the location and the deal; the lender and lawyer or title professional give a written estimate.

**Q: What is a down payment?**
A down payment is the amount of money you pay upfront toward the purchase price. The required amount depends on the loan type, location, and property type.

**Q: Can I use gifted money for a down payment?**
Often yes, but lenders usually require documentation confirming the source of the money and whether it is a gift or a loan. A signed gift letter is common.

**Q: What does a buyer's agent do?**
A buyer's agent helps you find homes, schedule showings, evaluate properties, compare them against the market, structure an offer, negotiate, and walk you through closing.

**Q: How do I know what price range I should shop in?**
Your price range should reflect your pre-approval, your monthly comfort level, your down payment, your debts, your lifestyle, and your longer term goals. Stretching to the top of a pre-approval is not always wise.

**Q: Should I rent or buy?**
Renting offers flexibility and zero maintenance. Buying builds equity and can be a better long-term financial decision if you plan to stay several years and the numbers work. There is no one right answer.

**Q: Should I buy now or wait?**
That depends on your finances, your timeline, current rates, inventory, and your local market. The right time to buy is usually when both the numbers and your life situation line up.

**Q: How do interest rates affect my buying power?**
Higher rates raise monthly payments and reduce how much you can afford. Lower rates improve affordability but can also increase competition.

**Q: Can I buy a home with bad credit?**
It may be possible, but your loan options, rates, and down payment requirements can be affected. A mortgage professional can review your situation and lay out paths.

**Q: Can I buy a home if I am self-employed?**
Yes. Self-employed buyers usually need to provide extra documentation such as tax returns, business income records, bank statements, and proof of income stability.

**Q: Can I buy a home if I have student loan debt?**
Yes. Lenders look at your overall debt-to-income ratio, not just the headline number. Manageable student loan payments do not automatically disqualify you.

**Q: Can I buy a home before selling my current home?**
Yes, but it depends on your finances, lender approval, and comfort holding both. Some buyers use bridge financing or make their offer conditional on the sale of their current home.

**Q: Can I buy a home remotely?**
Yes. Video walkthroughs, electronic signing, virtual tours, and remote closings are common. Remote buyers usually rely more heavily on inspections and trusted local professionals.

**Q: What should I look for when touring a home?**
Look past the staging. Note layout flow, natural light, storage, room sizes, condition of roof and windows, plumbing and electrical signs, basement moisture, smells, and neighbourhood noise.

**Q: How many homes should I look at before making an offer?**
There is no magic number. Some buyers find the right home on the first showing; others tour many. Focus on your must-haves and trade-offs, not the count.

**Q: Should I visit a home more than once before making an offer?**
If time allows, yes. A second visit at a different time of day can confirm things you noticed and catch things you missed.

**Q: How do I know if a home is priced fairly?**
The agent compares recent comparable sales, current competition, condition, lot, location, and buyer demand. Asking price is a strategy, not a guarantee of value.

**Q: Can you tell me what a home is worth?**
General market value depends on details that need a real review. The agent can run a comparative market analysis on a specific property.

**Q: What is a comparative market analysis?**
A CMA estimates a property's value by comparing it to similar homes that recently sold, are listed now, or did not sell. Sold prices matter more than asking prices.

**Q: What is a fixer-upper?**
A fixer-upper is a home that needs meaningful repairs or updates. It is often priced lower so the buyer can build equity through the work, but the renovation budget and risk should be planned carefully.

**Q: What is a buyer's market?**
A buyer's market means more homes are for sale than there are interested buyers. Buyers usually have more room to negotiate on price and terms.

**Q: What is a seller's market?**
A seller's market means there are more buyers than available homes. Prices and competition tend to rise, and bidding situations are more common.

**Q: How is the real estate market right now?**
That depends on location, property type, price band, inventory, and rates. The agent can give a current local market update during a consultation.

**Q: What are first-time buyer programs?**
Programs vary by city, province, state, and eligibility. The agent and a mortgage professional can help check what applies in your area.

**Q: What type of home is best for a first-time buyer?**
That depends on budget, lifestyle, maintenance comfort, commute, future plans, and what you want long-term. Condos, townhomes, semi-detached, and smaller detached homes are all common starting points.

**Q: Should I buy a condo or a house?**
A condo usually means lower maintenance and shared amenities. A house usually means more space, privacy, and responsibility. The right fit depends on your budget and lifestyle.

**Q: Can I buy a home and rent it out?**
Yes, but check local rental rules, financing requirements, insurance, taxes, and whether the property is suitable for tenants.

**Q: What is the difference between an inspection and an appraisal?**
An inspection looks at the physical condition of the home for defects and risks. An appraisal estimates the property's value for the lender. They serve different purposes.

**Q: Do I need a home inspection?**
A home inspection is usually recommended. It can reveal issues with the roof, foundation, plumbing, electrical, HVAC, insulation, drainage, and safety systems before you fully commit.

**Q: What is a home appraisal?**
An appraisal is a professional estimate of a property's market value, usually ordered by the lender to confirm the loan amount is supported by the property.

**Q: What is earnest money or a deposit?**
The deposit is money the buyer provides to show commitment after an offer is accepted. The amount, timing, and refund rules depend on the contract and local norms. Deposit specifics get walked through during the offer.

**Q: Is my deposit refundable?**
That depends on the contract terms and whether any conditions are still in place. Specifics should be confirmed with the agent and the lawyer before submitting an offer.

**Q: What happens if my financing falls through?**
If you have a financing condition and act within the allowed timeline, you may be able to exit the deal. Without that protection, there can be legal or financial consequences.

**Q: What is a contingency or condition in a real estate contract?**
A condition is something that must be met for the sale to go through, such as financing approval, satisfactory inspection, document review, or insurance.

**Q: Can I buy a home "as-is"?**
Yes. "As-is" means the seller will not make repairs or give credits for known defects. You can still inspect and decide whether to proceed at the agreed price.

**Q: What is a final walkthrough?**
It is a short visit to the property close to closing to confirm the seller has vacated, any agreed repairs are done, and the home is in the expected condition.

**Q: What is property tax and how is it paid?**
Property tax is a local tax based on the home's value. It is often included in the monthly mortgage payment via an escrow or tax account, depending on the lender.

**Q: How do school districts affect home prices?**
Homes in well-rated school catchment areas often hold value better and command higher prices. School boundaries can shift, so they should be verified directly with the official board.

**Q: What is the MLS?**
The MLS is a shared listings database real estate professionals use to publish and share property information so listings reach the widest pool of buyers.

**Q: What are property disclosures?**
Disclosures are documents in which the seller states known issues with the property. Disclosure rules vary by jurisdiction and should be confirmed with the agent and lawyer.

**Q: What is a title search?**
A title search reviews public records to confirm the seller's legal ownership and to surface any liens, claims, or registered interests on the property.

**Q: What is title insurance?**
Title insurance helps protect against certain title-related risks discovered after closing. Coverage and price vary by policy and jurisdiction.

**Q: Should I make a conditional or unconditional offer?**
That depends on your comfort, the market, your financing, and the property. Conditions protect you but can make the offer less competitive. The agent walks through the trade-offs before you write.

**Q: Can I change my mind after my offer is accepted?**
Sometimes, depending on whether conditions are still active. After conditions are removed and the deal is firm, backing out can have serious legal and financial consequences.

**Q: Can a buyer back out after making an offer?**
It depends on the contract and whether conditions are still in place. The agent and lawyer should be involved before any walk-away decision.

**Q: What is a clear-to-close status?**
"Clear-to-close" means the lender has reviewed and approved the loan, conditions are satisfied, and you are cleared to sign final paperwork.

**Q: What is a Loan-to-Value (LTV) ratio?**
LTV compares the loan amount to the appraised value of the home. Lenders use it to measure risk before approving a loan.

**Q: Should I pay off debt before buying a home?**
Reducing high-interest debt can improve your affordability and your application. The right move depends on your savings, your timeline, and your overall budget. A mortgage professional can run the numbers.

**Q: What happens after my offer is accepted?**
The buyer satisfies any conditions (financing, inspection, document review, insurance), the deposit is delivered per the agreement, and the file moves toward closing with the lender, lawyer, and agent coordinating.
