**Use this file only when the visitor specifically mentions Toronto, the GTA,
or Ontario.** The brokerage serves the United States and Canada broadly (see

## Local data and bodies

- TRREB (Toronto Regional Real Estate Board) is the primary source of local market data for the GTA.
- The Multiple Listing Service used by TRREB members is what powers most of the listing data agents in this area work with.

## Taxes that matter to buyers in the GTA

- **Land Transfer Tax**: Ontario charges a provincial Land Transfer Tax on most property purchases. Toronto charges an additional municipal Land Transfer Tax on top of the provincial one. Buyers in Toronto should plan for both when estimating closing costs.
- **First-time homebuyer rebates**: Both the provincial and municipal Land Transfer Taxes have first-time buyer rebate programs. Eligibility rules apply; confirm with the lawyer or settlement professional.
- **Non-Resident Speculation Tax (NRST)**: A surcharge on certain property purchases in Ontario by non-residents. Rules and rates change; confirm currently applicable rules with a tax professional.
- **HST and new construction**: New-build homes in Ontario may involve HST and rebate programs that established resale homes do not. The lawyer and builder paperwork handle the details.

## Closing costs in the GTA

Closing costs in Ontario typically include legal fees, title insurance, Land Transfer Tax (provincial and, in Toronto, municipal), adjustments for property taxes and utilities, and lender or mortgage-related fees. The lawyer provides a written estimate before closing.

## Condo Act and Status Certificate

Condo ownership in Ontario is governed by the Condominium Act. The status certificate is the document a buyer (and the buyer's lawyer) reviews to assess a building's financial health, rules, lawsuits, insurance, special assessments, and the unit's standing. Reviewing a status certificate is a standard part of any condo purchase in Ontario.

## The bidding war reality

In hot GTA conditions, multiple-offer situations are common on well-priced listings. Preparing usually means a clean pre-approval or financing confirmation, a deposit ready, possibly a pre-inspection before submitting, and a clearly defined walk-away price.

## Lawyers, title, and registration in Ontario

In Ontario, real estate lawyers handle title transfer, mortgage registration, closing funds, and the legal documents at closing. Buyers and sellers should retain a lawyer once an offer is firm (or earlier if the transaction is complex). The agent handles people and negotiation; the lawyer handles paperwork, money, and registration.

## Common GTA-specific buyer questions

- "Do I have to pay both land transfer taxes if I buy in Toronto?" — Yes, both the provincial and municipal Land Transfer Tax apply on most Toronto purchases. First-time buyers may qualify for partial rebates.
- "Is there a foreign buyer tax?" — There is the Non-Resident Speculation Tax in Ontario, plus possible federal restrictions on non-resident purchases. Rules change; confirm with a tax professional and lawyer.
- "How important is the status certificate?" — Very important for any condo purchase in Ontario. The buyer's lawyer reviews it as a standard step.
- "Should I waive my financing condition in a multiple-offer situation?" — That is a real risk. Lender pre-approval is not final approval; the property still needs to appraise and qualify. The agent and lender should walk through the risk before any waiver decision.

## Emergency or escalation flags

If a visitor mentions eviction, legal action, foreclosure, power of sale, or active disputes, the assistant suggests speaking with the appropriate licensed professional (real estate lawyer, paralegal, financial advisor) and offers to connect them with the agent quickly.
