# Condos, HOA, renting, and leasing

## Condos and HOA

**Q: What is an HOA or condo corporation?**
A homeowners association (in the U.S.) or condo corporation (in Canada) manages shared property in a building or planned community. Owners pay monthly or annual fees and follow the community rules.

**Q: How do condo fees work?**
Condo fees cover building maintenance, shared amenities, insurance, reserve fund contributions, and sometimes utilities depending on the building. What is included varies a lot.

**Q: Are condo fees bad?**
Not necessarily. Higher fees can cover meaningful services and a healthy building. Low fees can mean a well-run building or an underfunded one. What matters is the value behind the fee.

**Q: What should I check before buying a condo?**
Review the building's financial health, reserve fund, recent repairs, rules, insurance, lawsuits, planned fee increases, and any special assessments. The status certificate (Ontario) or resale certificate (many U.S. states) is the document for this.

**Q: What is a special assessment?**
An extra payment the building charges owners when reserve funds are not enough for major repairs, legal costs, or unexpected expenses.

**Q: What is a status certificate?**
In Ontario, the status certificate is a package of documents covering the condo corporation's finances, rules, insurance, lawsuits, and the unit's standing. It is one of the most important documents to review before buying a condo.

**Q: Can I have pets in my condo?**
That depends on the building's rules and any local laws. Some buildings allow pets with restrictions on size or number; some are pet-free. Always confirm rules in writing.

**Q: Can I rent out my condo?**
That depends on the building's rules and local law. Some condos allow long-term rentals but ban short-term rentals; some restrict any rental activity. Confirm before buying.

**Q: What about Airbnb or short-term rentals?**
Short-term rentals are heavily regulated in many cities and may be prohibited by building rules. Confirm both municipal rules and the building's bylaws before assuming any short-term rental income.

**Q: How do I know if a condo building is well-managed?**
Look at recent financial statements, the size and health of the reserve fund, special assessment history, lawsuits, insurance claims, and meeting minutes if available. The agent and a qualified reviewer can read the signs.

**Q: Are newer condos better than older ones?**
Newer condos often have modern finishes, warranties, and amenities, but layouts can be smaller and some have growing pains. Older condos often have larger layouts but may need building maintenance. Each comes with trade-offs.

**Q: What is pre-construction?**
Buying a condo or home that has not been built yet, directly from the developer, often years before move-in. Pre-construction has its own risks (delays, occupancy fees, closing adjustments, assignment rules) and benefits.

## Renting and leasing

**Q: Can you help me find a rental property?**
Yes. The agent can help collect budget, location, move-in date, bedrooms, pets, parking, and other preferences.

**Q: What documents do I need to rent a property?**
Commonly: ID, proof of income, employment letter, credit report, rental application, references, and sometimes bank statements. Specific requirements vary by location and landlord.

**Q: Do I need good credit to rent?**
Strong credit helps, but landlords also consider income, references, employment, savings, and rental history. Some accept a guarantor where rules allow.

**Q: Can I rent with no credit history?**
It may be possible. Proof of income, references, a guarantor, or extra documents can help.

**Q: What is a guarantor?**
Someone who agrees to take responsibility for rent and lease obligations if the tenant cannot pay.

**Q: How do landlords choose tenants?**
Landlords usually review income, credit, employment stability, rental history, references, move-in date, and overall application strength, within local fair housing rules.

**Q: How much rent can I charge for my property?**
Rental value depends on location, property type, size, condition, parking, amenities, utilities included, and current local rental demand.

**Q: What should I look for in a rental property?**
Location, commute, safety, condition, lease terms, included utilities, parking, laundry, noise, maintenance responsiveness, and overall lifestyle fit.

**Q: What is included in the rent?**
It varies. Some rentals include utilities, parking, internet, or laundry. Confirm what is in and what is extra in writing before signing.

**Q: How long is a typical lease?**
Twelve months is common in many markets, but lease terms vary by location, landlord, property type, and agreement.

**Q: Can I rent month-to-month?**
Sometimes. It depends on the landlord, lease terms, and local rules.

**Q: What is a security deposit?**
Money held by the landlord to cover unpaid rent, damage, or other lease-related costs at end of tenancy. Deposit rules vary widely by jurisdiction and are often regulated.

**Q: Can a landlord reject my rental application?**
Yes, but rejections must follow local housing and anti-discrimination laws. Common lawful reasons include weak income, poor credit, incomplete documents, or timing mismatch.

**Q: Can I bring pets to a rental property?**
That depends on local rules, building rules, the landlord's policy, and the type of pet. Confirm pet policies before applying.

**Q: Can you help me lease out my property?**
Yes. The agent helps price the rental, market it, screen applicants within fair housing rules, schedule showings, and prepare the lease.

**Q: What about service animals or assistance animals?**
Service and assistance animals are legally sensitive and treated differently than pets in many jurisdictions. The agent and legal guidance handle accommodation requests.

**Q: Can I evict a tenant?**
Eviction is heavily regulated and varies widely by jurisdiction. It almost always requires specific notices, grounds, and procedures. Consult a lawyer or paralegal who specializes in landlord-tenant law in your area.
