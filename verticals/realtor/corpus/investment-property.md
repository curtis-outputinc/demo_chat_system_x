# Investment property and landlord buying

**Q: Can you help me find investment properties?**
Yes. Share budget, preferred area, property type, target rent, and goals (cash flow, appreciation, flip) and the agent can identify candidates.

**Q: What should I know before buying an investment property?**
Review expected rent, expenses, vacancy risk, financing differences, taxes, repairs, local rental laws, property management needs, and longer-term appreciation potential. Do not rely on guarantees.

**Q: What is cash flow in real estate?**
Cash flow is rental income minus operating expenses (mortgage, taxes, insurance, maintenance, condo or HOA fees, management, vacancy). Methods of calculation vary; consistency matters.

**Q: What is a cap rate?**
A common investment metric: annual operating income divided by purchase price. Cap rate is useful for comparing similar properties but should not be the only metric.

**Q: What is appreciation?**
The increase in a property's value over time. Influenced by location, supply and demand, condition, rates, and broader economic conditions. Not guaranteed.

**Q: How do you analyze a potential investment property?**
Look at purchase price, financing, expected rent based on real local comparables, vacancy, repairs, maintenance, taxes, insurance, fees, utilities, management, and capital expenses.

**Q: Should I buy a condo, multi-unit, or single-family as an investment?**
Each has trade-offs. Condos are simpler to maintain but have fees and rental restrictions. Multi-unit properties spread risk across tenants but are more complex. Single-family homes often appreciate steadily but generate less cash flow. The right pick depends on your goals.

**Q: How is investment property financing different?**
Investment financing usually requires higher down payments, may have higher rates, and lenders look at projected rents differently. A mortgage professional can lay out the specifics.

**Q: Should I self-manage or hire a property manager?**
That depends on your time, distance from the property, number of units, and comfort with tenant management. The agent can refer to property managers if needed.

**Q: What about short-term rentals or Airbnb?**
Short-term rentals require local licensing, zoning compliance, condo or HOA permission, insurance, tax review, and platform-specific rules. Many cities tightly regulate them.

**Q: Are basement apartments or accessory dwelling units legal?**
That depends on local zoning, permits, fire code, and safety standards. Always verify legal status before counting on income from a secondary unit.

**Q: Can I buy a tenanted property?**
Yes, but the existing leases, rent levels, deposits, and tenant rights stay with the property. Vacant possession rules vary by jurisdiction.

**Q: What about rent control?**
Rent control or rent stabilization applies in some jurisdictions and limits how much rent can rise. Confirm what applies locally before underwriting an investment.

**Q: How do I know if a neighbourhood is a good investment area?**
Look at sales trends, rental demand, transit and development, schools, employment access, supply, and long-term population growth. Combine multiple data points, not just one.

**Q: What is house hacking?**
A strategy where the buyer lives in part of a multi-unit property (or rents rooms in a single-family) and rents out the rest to offset costs.

**Q: Should I buy investment property for appreciation or cash flow?**
That depends on your goals and your time horizon. Many investors balance both. The agent can help frame the trade-offs.

**Q: Can I 1031 exchange (U.S.) or use other tax-deferred strategies?**
Tax-deferred exchanges and similar strategies have specific rules and timelines. Work with a tax professional and a qualified intermediary; the agent can refer.

**Q: Are pre-construction units a good investment?**
They can be, but they carry delay risk, closing-cost surprises, market shifts, and assignment restrictions. Read the contract carefully and have a lawyer review.

**Q: What is the risk of a tenant not paying rent?**
It exists in every market. Mitigating steps include thorough screening within fair housing rules, clear leases, sufficient deposits where legal, and reserves.

**Q: How are property taxes assessed on investment properties?**
Property taxes are based on the property's assessed value, which is set by local government and may differ from market value. Investment properties may also have different tax treatment.
