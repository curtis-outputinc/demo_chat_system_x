# 1. Chatbot Use, Scope, and Safety Boundaries
Purpose: safe, generic chatbot knowledge-base content for mortgage broker websites. These points are educational only and should not tell a visitor what product to choose.
- The chatbot should explain mortgage concepts in plain language, not recommend a specific mortgage, lender, rate, term, amortization, or debt strategy.
- The chatbot can help visitors understand common mortgage words, process steps, document categories, and when to speak with a licensed mortgage professional.
- The chatbot should avoid saying that someone is approved, qualified, guaranteed, eligible, or likely to receive a specific mortgage outcome.
- The chatbot should avoid collecting unnecessary sensitive information such as full government identification numbers, full banking credentials, passwords, or complete account numbers.
- The chatbot can ask general intake questions such as purchase, refinance, renewal, property type, employment type, and preferred contact method.
- The chatbot should clearly state that any mortgage numbers, product fit, payment estimate, or borrowing estimate must be confirmed by a broker or lender.
- The chatbot should encourage direct broker contact when a visitor mentions making an offer, removing a financing condition, refinancing debt, changing lenders, or submitting documents.
- The chatbot should use careful wording such as "may," "typically," "often," "can," and "depends on the lender" rather than absolute promises.
- The chatbot should explain that mortgage rules and lender policies can differ by country, province, state, lender, property, borrower profile, and loan type.
- The chatbot should avoid legal, tax, insurance, investment, debt-repayment, or real estate strategy advice and route those questions to qualified professionals.
- The chatbot should not compare individual lenders by claiming one is best, safest, cheapest, or guaranteed unless a licensed broker verifies the statement.
- The chatbot should be designed to capture questions, explain neutral information, and escalate the conversation when personal guidance is needed.
- The chatbot should remind users that final mortgage decisions should be based on written lender terms, broker explanation, legal review where applicable, and the borrower's full situation.


# 2. What Mortgage Brokers Generally Do
Purpose: safe, generic chatbot knowledge-base content for mortgage broker websites. These points are educational only and should not tell a visitor what product to choose.
- A mortgage broker generally acts as an intermediary between a borrower and one or more mortgage lenders.
- A broker gathers information from the borrower and helps identify possible mortgage options that may fit the borrower's stated situation.
- A broker usually does not lend the money directly unless the business is also licensed or structured as a lender in the relevant jurisdiction.
- A broker may work with banks, credit unions, monoline lenders, mortgage finance companies, alternative lenders, private lenders, or other lending sources.
- A broker may explain differences between lenders, mortgage structures, repayment features, penalties, documentation requirements, and approval steps.
- A broker may help package the application, request lender approval, respond to lender conditions, and coordinate with lawyers, real estate agents, appraisers, and insurers where appropriate.
- A broker should communicate that lender approval is separate from broker guidance and is not controlled solely by the broker.
- A broker may help with purchases, renewals, refinances, equity take-outs, second mortgages, investment properties, and sometimes commercial or construction lending.
- A broker's role often includes education, comparison, documentation support, lender submission, condition tracking, and closing coordination.
- A broker should not be described as the borrower's lawyer, accountant, appraiser, real estate agent, or insurance advisor unless they are separately licensed for that role.
- A broker can explain general mortgage options but should not be portrayed as guaranteeing future rates, future approvals, property values, or investment outcomes.
- A broker should encourage borrowers to read written mortgage documents and ask questions before signing.
- A chatbot should frame the broker as a resource for mortgage navigation, not as a source of guaranteed financial outcomes.

# 7. Core Mortgage Terminology
Purpose: safe, generic chatbot knowledge-base content for mortgage broker websites. These points are educational only and should not tell a visitor what product to choose.
- A mortgage is a loan secured by real estate, where the property is used as security for repayment according to the loan terms.
- Principal is the amount borrowed or still owing on the mortgage, separate from interest and fees.
- Interest is the cost of borrowing money, usually calculated based on the mortgage balance and the interest rate.
- The mortgage term is the length of the current mortgage contract with the lender.
- Amortization is the estimated total repayment period for the mortgage if payments are made as scheduled.
- A fixed rate usually means the interest rate stays the same during the selected term.
- A variable or adjustable rate usually means the interest rate can change based on a lender benchmark or market-linked reference rate.
- A payment frequency describes how often payments are made, such as monthly, semi-monthly, biweekly, or weekly depending on lender options.
- A prepayment privilege allows the borrower to pay extra toward the mortgage within the lender's rules without triggering certain penalties.
- A mortgage penalty is a cost that may apply when a borrower breaks, pays out, refinances, or changes a mortgage before the agreed time.
- A lien, charge, or mortgage registration is the legal security interest registered against the property in favour of the lender.
- Equity is the difference between the property value and the amount owed against the property.
- A commitment letter or mortgage offer sets out lender-approved terms and conditions, but the exact name and format may vary.

- Portability may allow a borrower to transfer an existing mortgage to a new property, subject to lender approval and product rules.
- Assumability may allow another borrower to take over a mortgage, subject to lender approval and legal requirements.
- Blend-and-extend options may combine an existing rate with a new rate when changing mortgage terms, depending on lender policy.
- Collateral charge and standard charge mortgages can differ in registration structure and switching process, depending on jurisdiction.

# 9. Payments, Amortization, and Repayment Concepts
Purpose: safe, generic chatbot knowledge-base content for mortgage broker websites. These points are educational only and should not tell a visitor what product to choose.
- Mortgage payments commonly include principal and interest, and may also include property taxes, insurance, or escrow amounts depending on lender and jurisdiction.
- Early in a mortgage repayment schedule, more of the payment may go toward interest than principal depending on the rate and amortization.
- Over time, more of each payment may go toward principal if the mortgage is paid as scheduled.
- A longer amortization may reduce regular payments but can increase total interest cost over time.
- A shorter amortization may increase regular payments but can reduce total interest cost over time.
- Payment frequency affects how often payments are made and may influence how quickly principal is reduced depending on the structure.
- Extra payments may reduce principal faster if allowed by the mortgage terms.
- Prepayment privileges are lender-specific and may include limits, timing rules, or annual allowances.
- Payment deferrals, skipped payments, or payment holidays are not automatic and depend on lender approval and product rules.
- Missed payments can affect credit, default status, fees, legal action, or lender enforcement rights.
- A borrower facing payment difficulty should contact the lender or broker early rather than waiting until default.
- A chatbot should not advise users to choose a longer or shorter amortization; it can explain the general trade-off and route to the broker.
- A broker can help explain how payment structure fits within lender qualification rules, but the borrower should review affordability carefully.
