# Homeowner FAQ

Everything a homeowner is likely to ask about HVAC. Answers are
plain-language and reflect ABC HVAC's approach. Questions are
grouped by topic. Duplicates from multiple source lists have been
merged, and 50 additional questions have been added at the end.

---

## The basics

**What does HVAC stand for?**
Heating, Ventilation, and Air Conditioning. It refers to the whole
system that moves, filters, heats, and cools the air in your home.

**Do you service both heating and air conditioning?**
Yes. We work on furnaces, boilers, air conditioners, heat pumps,
ductless mini-splits, thermostats, humidifiers, dehumidifiers,
ventilation, and indoor air quality equipment.

**How long does an HVAC system usually last?**
Central air conditioners and heat pumps typically last 10 to 15
years. Gas furnaces last 15 to 25 years with routine maintenance.
Boilers often go 20 to 30 years. Lifespan depends on usage,
maintenance, install quality, and climate.

**How do I know if I need a repair or a replacement?**
General rule: if the system is under 10 years old and the repair
is a small percentage of a new system's cost, repair. If the
system is over 15 years old, breaks down frequently, is
inefficient, or the repair is more than half the cost of a new
system, replacement usually makes more sense. See the "5,000 Rule"
below: multiply the system's age by the repair cost. Over $5,000
generally means replace.

**What does the "5,000 Rule" mean?**
Multiply the age of your system in years by the repair cost in
dollars. If the result exceeds 5,000, it usually makes more sense
to replace. It is a rough rule of thumb, not a strict formula.

**Do you service all HVAC brands?**
We service most major brands. A few specialty or proprietary
systems require manufacturer-trained technicians and specific
parts; if that comes up, we tell the customer upfront.

**Do I need to be home during a service appointment?**
Usually yes. The tech needs access to the thermostat, the furnace
or air handler, the outdoor unit, and often the electrical panel.

**Do you offer emergency service?**
Yes. We have a 24/7 emergency line at (123) 555-5555 for no heat,
no cooling, gas smells, CO alarms, refrigerant leaks, water damage,
and any HVAC failure that is unsafe or urgent.

## Filters

**How often should I change my HVAC filter?**
Standard 1-inch filters: every 1 to 3 months. Homes with pets,
allergies, or heavy dust: check monthly. High-efficiency 4- or
5-inch filters: every 6 to 12 months.

**Can a dirty filter damage my system?**
Yes. Restricted airflow makes the system overheat in heating
mode and freeze in cooling mode. It also raises energy bills and
strains the blower motor.

**What is a MERV rating and what should I use?**
Minimum Efficiency Reporting Value. Higher numbers trap smaller
particles. Most homes do well with MERV 8 to 11. MERV 13 is good
for allergy sufferers or homes near heavy pollen or wildfire
smoke, but only if the blower can push through it (we check
static pressure first).

**Can I put a high-MERV filter in any system?**
Not always. Extremely high-MERV filters (13+) restrict airflow
enough to overheat the blower in older or smaller systems. We
verify compatibility before recommending them.

**Are HVAC filters recyclable?**
Standard pleated and fiberglass filters are generally not
recyclable because of trapped contaminants and blended synthetic
materials.

## Air conditioning

**How do I know what size AC my home needs?**
An HVAC pro runs a Manual J load calculation that factors square
footage, window orientation, insulation, ductwork condition, and
climate. Square footage alone is not enough.

**Is a bigger AC better?**
No. An oversized AC short-cycles: it cools the air fast but does
not run long enough to remove humidity. Result: cold and clammy,
uneven temperatures, and premature compressor wear.

**What is a SEER or SEER2 rating?**
SEER (and its newer version SEER2) measures cooling efficiency
over a season. Higher SEER = better efficiency = lower electric
bill.

**Why is my AC running but not blowing cold air?**
Usually one of: dirty filter, blocked outdoor condenser, tripped
breaker, low refrigerant from a leak, frozen coil, or compressor
issue.

**Why is my AC freezing up?**
Restricted airflow (dirty filter, blocked vents, dirty coil) or
low refrigerant. Turn the system off and let the ice melt fully
before running it again. If it refreezes, call us.

**What should I do if my AC is frozen?**
Turn it off. Let it thaw fully (can take several hours). Change
the filter. Run only the fan for a while to help drying. If it
freezes again, do not keep running it: call for service.

**Why is water leaking from my AC?**
Usually a clogged condensate drain line, a cracked drain pan, a
frozen coil that is melting, or a failed condensate pump. Turn
the unit off to stop damage and call us.

**How often does my AC need to be recharged with refrigerant?**
Under normal operation, never. AC is a sealed system. If it is low
on refrigerant, there is a leak that needs repair.

**Is a refrigerant leak dangerous?**
Refrigerant is not immediately dangerous to be around in small
amounts, but it damages the environment, wrecks the compressor if
the system runs low for long, and needs a licensed technician to
handle. Do not "top it off" without finding the leak.

**Why does my AC turn on and off rapidly (short cycling)?**
Oversized AC, dirty filter, thermostat problem, low refrigerant,
or an overheating compressor.

**What temperature should I set my AC in summer?**
The U.S. Department of Energy recommends 78°F (26°C) when home,
higher when away. Any target you find comfortable is fine; each
degree lower typically costs about 3% more on cooling.

**Should I cover my outdoor AC unit in winter?**
Only put a small cover over the top to keep icicles and debris
out. Do not wrap the whole unit tightly: it traps moisture and
invites rodents.

**How do I clean my outdoor AC unit?**
Turn off power at the disconnect and the breaker. Clear leaves,
grass, and branches from a two-foot radius. Gently rinse the fins
with a garden hose (never a pressure washer). Anything deeper is
a pro job.

**Why does my AC smell like dirty socks?**
"Dirty sock syndrome": mold and bacteria on the wet evaporator
coil. Coil cleaning usually fixes it; a UV light or better
filtration prevents it from returning.

**What is an AC compressor and what happens if it fails?**
The compressor is the pump at the heart of the AC. If it fails on
an older system, it is often more cost-effective to replace the
whole system than the compressor alone.

**Can I replace just the outdoor AC unit without changing the indoor coil?**
No. Mixing an old indoor coil with a new outdoor condenser wrecks
efficiency, voids the warranty, and can shorten the system's life.
We replace both.

**How long does a new central AC install take?**
Straightforward replacement: 4 to 8 hours. If ductwork changes are
needed, it can stretch to multiple days.

**What is a ductless mini-split?**
A cooling / heating system without ductwork. One outdoor unit
feeds one or more small indoor wall or ceiling cassettes. Good
for additions, basements, garages, older homes without ducts, or
rooms that were always the wrong temperature.

**Can a ductless mini-split cool a whole house?**
Yes, with a multi-zone system. One outdoor unit can serve four to
eight indoor heads depending on the model.

## Heating and furnaces

**What is the difference between a furnace and a boiler?**
A furnace heats air and blows it through ducts. A boiler heats
water and distributes it through radiators, baseboards, or
in-floor tubing.

**What does AFUE mean?**
Annual Fuel Utilization Efficiency. It measures how much of the
fuel becomes useful heat. A 95% AFUE furnace turns 95% of the gas
into heat; the remaining 5% goes out the flue.

**How long does a natural gas furnace last?**
With annual maintenance, 15 to 25 years. Neglected systems drop to
10 or less.

**Why is my furnace blowing cold air?**
Common causes: thermostat fan set to "on" instead of "auto,"
dirty flame sensor, failed igniter, tripped safety limit switch,
gas supply interrupted, or the furnace has locked out after too
many failed ignition attempts.

**What should I do if I smell gas near my furnace?**
Leave the building immediately. Do not touch light switches or
electronics. Call 911 or your gas utility from outside. Once you
are out, call us to send a tech.

**How does a pilot light differ from an electronic ignition?**
A pilot burns gas constantly. Electronic ignition (hot surface or
spark) only lights when heat is called. Modern furnaces use
electronic ignition for efficiency and safety.

**Why is my furnace squealing?**
Usually a slipping or worn blower belt, or motor bearings that
need lubrication or replacement.

**What is a heat exchanger and why is a cracked one dangerous?**
The heat exchanger separates combustion gases from the air you
breathe. A crack allows carbon monoxide to leak into the home.
This is a stop-using-the-furnace situation.

**Can a furnace cause carbon monoxide poisoning?**
Yes, from incomplete combustion or a cracked heat exchanger. This
is why annual maintenance and working CO alarms matter.

**How often should I test my carbon monoxide alarms?**
Monthly with the test button. Replace batteries at least once a
year. Replace the whole alarm every 5 to 7 years.

**Why is my furnace short cycling?**
Restricted airflow (dirty filter) causing the safety limit to
trip, thermostat problem, or the furnace is oversized for the
home.

**What temperature should I set my furnace in winter?**
The Department of Energy recommends 68°F (20°C) while awake,
lower while sleeping or away. Every degree lower saves roughly 1
to 3% on heating.

**What is a dual-fuel or hybrid heating system?**
A heat pump for milder weather paired with a gas furnace that
kicks in during extreme cold. Combines the efficiency of a heat
pump with the raw heat of gas.

**Do I need to clean my furnace burners?**
Yes, during annual maintenance. Soot and oxidation reduce
combustion efficiency and can cause short cycling or safety
shutdowns.

**What is the difference between single-stage, two-stage, and variable-speed furnaces?**
Single-stage: full blast or off. Two-stage: high and low. Variable
speed / modulating: adjusts continuously, quietest and most even
temperature.

**Can I switch from oil or propane to natural gas?**
Yes, if gas service is available to your street. Conversion
usually cuts heating costs and eliminates fuel delivery.

**Why is water pooling around my furnace?**
High-efficiency condensing furnaces produce condensate. A clogged
drain line, cracked trap, or failed pump causes leaks. Call us.

**Is a dusty smell normal when I first turn on the furnace for the season?**
Yes, briefly. Dust on the heat exchanger burns off in the first
hour or so. If it lasts more than an hour or turns acrid, shut it
off and call us.

**Can I install a furnace myself?**
No. It involves high-voltage electrical, natural or propane gas
lines, and combustion venting. All of these are life-safety
systems that require a licensed HVAC contractor and usually a
permit.

## Heat pumps

**What is a heat pump?**
Equipment that moves heat instead of generating it. In summer it
acts like an AC (moves heat outside). In winter it reverses and
pulls heat from the outdoor air into the home.

**Can a heat pump both heat and cool?**
Yes. One system for all four seasons.

**Do heat pumps work in cold weather?**
Modern cold-climate heat pumps work efficiently well below
freezing. Standard heat pumps lose efficiency below about 25°F
(-4°C) and usually pair with a backup heat source.

**What is emergency heat (Aux Heat)?**
Backup electric resistance strips or a gas furnace that fires when
the outdoor temperature is too low for the heat pump alone. On
your thermostat, "Emergency Heat" or "Aux Heat" runs only that
backup, not the heat pump.

**Are heat pumps cheaper to run than a gas furnace?**
It depends on local electricity and gas rates. A modern high-SEER
heat pump often beats gas in mild climates and increasingly in
cold climates too. We can walk through the math for your utility
rates.

**How long do heat pumps last?**
About 10 to 15 years, because they run year-round for both heating
and cooling.

**What is a geothermal heat pump?**
A heat pump that exchanges heat with underground loops instead of
outdoor air. Extremely efficient, but higher upfront cost due to
excavation.

**Why is my heat pump blowing lukewarm air?**
Heat pumps deliver air around 85 to 90°F, which feels warm but not
hot the way a gas furnace does. This is normal, not a fault. The
house still warms up, just more gradually.

**Is it normal for a heat pump to frost up outside in winter?**
A light frost is normal. A heavy solid block of ice on the outdoor
coil is not: it means the defrost cycle is not working. Call us.

**What is the defrost cycle?**
The heat pump briefly runs in reverse (cooling mode) to melt frost
off the outdoor coil. Steam rising from the outdoor unit in winter
is normal.

**Can I keep my gas furnace as backup when I install a heat pump?**
Yes: that is a hybrid or dual-fuel setup. Very common and highly
efficient.

**What is HSPF or HSPF2?**
Heating Seasonal Performance Factor. Measures heat pump heating
efficiency over a season. Higher is better.

## Thermostats

**Why is my thermostat blank?**
Dead batteries, tripped breaker, blown fuse in the air handler,
the furnace door safety switch is open, or a wiring issue.

**Why does my thermostat say cooling is on but nothing is happening?**
Possible causes: a control board fault, a delay timer counting
down, a tripped high-pressure switch on the AC, or a failed
communication wire.

**Should my thermostat fan be on "Auto" or "On"?**
Auto for most people. "On" runs the blower constantly, which
improves circulation but uses more electricity and can push
humidity back into the house on humid days.

**Where should my thermostat be located?**
Interior wall, roughly 5 feet off the floor, out of direct sun,
away from drafts, kitchens, exterior doors, supply vents, and
windows.

**Is a smart thermostat worth it?**
For most homes, yes. Automated scheduling and away-detection
typically save 10 to 12% on heating and cooling. Best fit if you
are away from home often or have irregular hours.

**Can any HVAC system use a smart thermostat?**
Most, but not all. Some need a C-wire, and communicating systems
need their own proprietary thermostats. We confirm compatibility
before installing.

**Why is my house temperature different from what the thermostat says?**
Thermostat location matters: sunlight on the thermostat, a nearby
supply vent, or a draft can throw off the reading. Poor airflow to
the room the thermostat is in can also cause it.

## Airflow and comfort

**Why are some rooms warmer or colder than others?**
Ductwork imbalance, closed or blocked vents, poor insulation,
leaky windows, an oversized or undersized system, or a thermostat
that only reads one part of the house.

**Should I close vents in unused rooms?**
No. Closing vents increases duct pressure, causes leaks, and puts
strain on the blower. Better to redesign the airflow or add zoning.

**What is HVAC zoning?**
Separating the home into zones (usually 2 to 4), each with its own
thermostat and motorized dampers in the ductwork. The upstairs
can hold a different temperature from the basement, day/night
schedules can differ per zone.

**Why is my airflow so weak?**
Dirty filter, disconnected or crushed duct, undersized return
grille, or a failing blower motor.

**Should I keep interior doors open or closed?**
For central-air homes, doors closed with the fan running can trap
warm or cool air in rooms without return-air paths. If a bedroom
does not have a return vent, leave the door open when the system
is running, or ask us about adding jumper ducts or transfer
grilles.

**Do I need to clean my ducts?**
Not always. Every 3 to 5 years is a general guideline. Sooner if
you had a major renovation, rodent problem, mold event, or see
visible dust falling out of registers.

**Can I DIY duct cleaning?**
Not effectively. Consumer-grade tools do not reach deep enough.
Professional duct cleaning uses truck-mounted vacuum systems and
agitation brushes.

## Indoor air quality

**How can my HVAC help reduce allergies?**
Higher-MERV filtration, UV coil purifiers, whole-home HEPA where
supported, controlled humidity (30 to 50%), and regular duct
inspections.

**What is the ideal indoor humidity?**
30 to 50%. Below that: dry skin, static, cracked wood. Above that:
mugginess, mold risk.

**Why is my house so dry in winter?**
Heated indoor air holds less moisture than warm outdoor summer air.
Whole-home humidifiers add moisture in a controlled way.

**Why is my house so humid in summer?**
Poor AC performance, oversized AC that does not run long enough to
dehumidify, high indoor humidity sources (unvented bath fans,
crawlspace moisture), or basement leaks.

**Can my HVAC system remove odors?**
Filtration and air purifiers help, but the source of the odor
usually has to be addressed. HVAC also helps by moving air, not
just filtering it.

**Why is there so much dust in my home?**
Leaky ducts pulling attic or crawlspace air into the return,
under-filtering, gaps around the filter frame that let dust
bypass, no return in one wing of the house, pets, or an unfinished
renovation.

**Can mold grow in my ductwork?**
Yes, if the ducts are cool and damp (uninsulated ducts in humid
attics or basements) and dust accumulates. Fix the moisture source
and the mold does not come back.

**What is a whole-home humidifier?**
A humidifier that attaches to the furnace and adds moisture to the
whole home's airstream. Bypass, fan-powered, and steam are the
three main types.

**What is a UV air purifier?**
A UV-C light installed inside the duct or over the coil that kills
mold, bacteria, and virus particles as they pass through.

**What is an ERV or HRV?**
Energy or Heat Recovery Ventilator. Brings fresh outdoor air in
and exhausts stale indoor air while transferring heat (and moisture
in an ERV) between the two streams to avoid wasting energy.

## Efficiency and utility bills

**How much can a new high-efficiency system save?**
Replacing a 15+ year old system with a modern high-efficiency one
often drops heating and cooling costs 20 to 40%. Exact savings
depend on the old system's condition, the new system's rating, and
local utility rates.

**Does closing vents save energy?**
No. It increases duct pressure and causes leaks. Better to zone
the system.

**How does duct sealing improve efficiency?**
Leaky ducts leak 15 to 30% of your conditioned air into
unconditioned spaces. Sealing them makes every dollar of energy
count.

**Should I get an energy audit before replacing my HVAC?**
Ideally yes. It uncovers insulation and air-sealing issues that
would otherwise make you buy an oversized system to compensate.

**Does dirty equipment raise my energy bill?**
Yes. Dirty coils, dirty filters, and worn parts force the system
to run longer for the same result.

**Should I turn my HVAC off when I leave?**
For short trips, no: adjust the setback by 5 to 8 degrees instead
of turning it fully off. For extended vacations (a week or more),
you can raise or lower the setback more aggressively but do not
turn the system off entirely in extreme weather (freezing pipes,
humidity/mold in summer).

## Contractors, quotes, warranties

**How do I choose a reputable HVAC contractor?**
Look for verified reviews, valid state license, general liability
and workers' comp insurance, EPA Section 608 certification, NATE
certification for technicians, transparent quotes, and written
warranties.

**Should an HVAC contractor be licensed, bonded, and insured?**
Yes. This protects you from property damage, poor workmanship, and
worker injury liability.

**What is the standard warranty on a new HVAC system?**
Most brands offer 5 to 10 years on parts, and reputable
contractors offer 1 year of labor by default with extended options.

**What is the difference between a manufacturer warranty and a labor warranty?**
Manufacturer covers replacement parts. Labor warranty covers the
tech's time to install them. Both matter.

**Why is registering a new HVAC system important?**
Unregistered systems usually get half the warranty period. Most
manufacturers require registration within 60 to 90 days.

**Should I repair or replace my HVAC system?**
Over 15 years old and needing a major repair? Usually replace. Under
10 and a moderate repair? Usually repair. See the 5,000 Rule above.

**What should be in an HVAC installation quote?**
Equipment make, model, and size; efficiency ratings; labor,
including ductwork or venting changes; the thermostat; disposal of
old equipment; permits; warranty terms; timeline.

**Can a new HVAC system raise my home's resale value?**
Yes. Buyers strongly prefer newer, higher-efficiency systems.

**Do I need a permit to replace my HVAC?**
Almost always for a full system replacement. Sometimes not for a
same-size like-for-like swap. We pull permits when required.

## 50 additional questions

**Q1. How do I know if my HVAC system is properly sized for my home?**
A quick sign it is not: rooms are consistently uneven, humidity is
too high or too low, the system short-cycles, or the utility bill
is higher than a similar neighbor's. A Manual J load calculation
is the real answer.

**Q2. Can adding attic insulation change what size HVAC I need?**
Yes. Better insulation reduces the heating and cooling load,
which means you can install a smaller (and cheaper) system when
it is time to replace.

**Q3. What is a heat load and how is it calculated?**
Heat load is how much heating or cooling capacity your home needs
in BTUs per hour. It is calculated with a Manual J procedure that
looks at square footage, window orientation, insulation, air
infiltration, and climate.

**Q4. Why does my system run more when it is windy outside?**
Air infiltration through gaps in the building envelope goes up
in wind, so more heated or cooled air escapes and more outdoor
air sneaks in. Air-sealing the envelope helps.

**Q5. Can I run my AC when the outdoor temperature is below 60°F?**
Not usually. Running an AC in cool weather often freezes the
evaporator coil because the refrigerant does not absorb enough
heat. Wait until it warms up or use a heat pump instead.

**Q6. Why is my heat pump running in cooling mode when I set it to heat?**
That is the defrost cycle. The system briefly runs in reverse for a
few minutes to melt frost off the outdoor coil, then returns to
heating.

**Q7. What is a condensate pump and when do I need one?**
A small pump that lifts condensate water out of a low-lying AC air
handler or high-efficiency furnace to a drain that is above it.
Needed when the equipment cannot drain by gravity.

**Q8. How do I unclog my condensate drain line?**
Turn the system off. Locate the drain line (typically 3/4-inch PVC
near the air handler). Use a wet/dry vacuum on the outdoor end to
suck out the clog. Then run a cup of distilled vinegar down the
indoor cleanout to prevent regrowth. If it is a repeat problem,
call us.

**Q9. Why does my thermostat need a C-wire?**
The C ("common") wire provides continuous 24V power to the
thermostat. Smart thermostats need it because their screens and
Wi-Fi radios cannot run on the small charge some old thermostats
draw from the R wire.

**Q10. Can I install a smart thermostat myself?**
If your existing thermostat has a C-wire and you are comfortable
with basic electrical work, yes. If not, a technician can add a
C-wire or an adapter for you.

**Q11. Why does my furnace smell like burning plastic?**
Something is overheating. Turn the furnace off at the switch and
call us. Could be a wire, a motor bearing seizing, or debris on
the heat exchanger.

**Q12. Can HVAC noises damage my hearing?**
No, they are not loud enough. But sudden new noises are a warning
sign that something is starting to fail.

**Q13. What is a variable-speed blower and is it worth it?**
A blower motor that adjusts fan speed continuously instead of only
running at one or two speeds. Quieter, more even temperatures,
better dehumidification, and roughly 30% less electricity to run
the fan. Worth it on most whole-system replacements.

**Q14. What is a two-stage compressor?**
An AC compressor that runs at a low output for mild days and a
high output only when needed. Better humidity control and lower
energy use than a single-stage.

**Q15. Can I recharge my own AC?**
No. Refrigerant handling requires EPA Section 608 certification.
Also, if the system is low, there is a leak that has to be found
and fixed first.

**Q16. Why is my AC line set (the copper lines between indoor and outdoor units) sweating?**
The insulation is missing or damaged. Warm humid air meets the
cold refrigerant line and condensation forms. We reinsulate as
part of a service visit.

**Q17. Does my HVAC have a lifespan warranty?**
Rarely. Some manufacturers offer lifetime heat exchanger warranties
on premium furnaces, but the compressor and other components are
usually 10 years.

**Q18. What is a hard start kit?**
A capacitor accessory that helps an aging AC compressor start
more easily. It can extend the life of a struggling compressor
briefly, but it is a band-aid, not a fix.

**Q19. My AC breaker keeps tripping. What could it be?**
Common causes: a shorted compressor, a failing capacitor, a
grounded compressor motor, a bad breaker, or an undersized
electrical circuit. Do not keep resetting it, that damages the
compressor. Call us.

**Q20. Why do I keep hearing water in my baseboards or radiators?**
Air in the hydronic loop. It has to be bled out at the highest
radiator or through the air separator on the boiler. Once air is
out, quiet returns.

**Q21. What is a hydronic system?**
Heating that uses water as the transfer medium: boiler heats
water, pump moves it through pipes to radiators, baseboards, or
in-floor tubing. Very quiet and comfortable.

**Q22. Can I use my central AC to help dehumidify a basement?**
A bit, if the basement is on the same return circuit and the AC
runs long enough. A dedicated basement dehumidifier does the job
better without cooling the space.

**Q23. Should my humidifier run in summer too?**
No. Summer air is already humid and adding more moisture invites
mold. Turn the humidifier off at the start of cooling season.

**Q24. What is a smart vent and does it work?**
A motorized register that opens or closes remotely to redirect
airflow. They can help with uneven rooms, but closing too many at
once creates the same pressure problem as manually closing vents.
Zoning with dampers in the duct is a more robust solution.

**Q25. Can I use my AC as a dehumidifier without cooling the house?**
Only if it has a hot gas reheat option or a dedicated dehumidify
mode. Otherwise it will overshoot the target temperature.

**Q26. Do heat pumps work with existing ductwork?**
Usually yes, but ducts sized for a furnace sometimes need
adjustments when moving to a heat pump because heat pumps run
longer at lower temperatures. We assess ducts during the quote.

**Q27. What is a cold-climate heat pump?**
A heat pump designed to work efficiently even at very low outdoor
temperatures, typically down to -13°F (-25°C) or lower. Uses
inverter-driven compressor and specialized refrigerant control.

**Q28. Why do my new HVAC quotes vary so much?**
Because they include different equipment, different sizes,
different scope of work, different warranties, and different
labor rates. Cheapest is not always best; the details matter.

**Q29. How can I tell if a contractor is padding their quote?**
Compare line items across quotes: equipment model, ductwork
scope, permits, disposal, warranty, thermostat. If one quote is
30% higher and the extras do not justify it, ask. Any honest
contractor will explain the delta.

**Q30. Does an oversized furnace short-cycle?**
Yes. The oversized furnace reaches the setpoint quickly, shuts
off, cools down, kicks on again a few minutes later. Uncomfortable
and hard on the equipment.

**Q31. What is the ideal furnace runtime per cycle?**
Roughly 15 to 20 minutes per cycle in mild weather, longer in
cold weather. If cycles are shorter than 10 minutes routinely,
the furnace is oversized or something else is wrong.

**Q32. Should I turn my HVAC off during a power outage?**
Yes: flip the breaker or the disconnect. Power can come back with
a surge that damages the equipment. Turn it back on manually once
power is stable.

**Q33. What is a surge protector for HVAC and do I need one?**
A whole-home surge protector at the electrical panel protects
your HVAC compressor, control boards, and other sensitive
electronics from lightning-related surges. Highly recommended in
storm-prone areas.

**Q34. Is it OK to install my outdoor unit in direct sun?**
It works, but efficiency is slightly better if the unit is
shaded. Do not enclose it or crowd it: airflow is more important
than shade.

**Q35. Why is my second floor always hotter than the first?**
Heat rises, plus the roof gets sun exposure the walls do not. Fixes:
zoning, additional return airflow upstairs, more attic insulation,
or a ductless mini-split serving the upstairs.

**Q36. What is a soft start kit for a mini-split?**
A device that reduces the electrical inrush when the compressor
starts. Useful when a mini-split is on a limited electrical
circuit or a solar backup system.

**Q37. Should I bother with an HVAC service plan if my system is new?**
Yes. Most manufacturer warranties actually require documented
annual maintenance. Skipping maintenance can void the warranty.

**Q38. What is delta T on an AC and why does it matter?**
Delta T is the temperature drop across the evaporator coil
(return air temp minus supply air temp). Should be 16 to 22°F on
a properly functioning system. Anything outside that range points
to airflow or refrigerant issues.

**Q39. Why is my new AC louder than my old one?**
Sometimes new equipment has different fan and compressor sounds,
especially variable-speed units that ramp up and down. If it is
noticeably louder or making a specific noise (buzz, rattle,
grinding), call us to check.

**Q40. Can I install a heat pump in a house with radiators?**
Yes, but not by connecting it to the radiator loop. You install a
separate air-source heat pump with new indoor cassettes (ductless)
or with new ductwork. The old boiler can stay as backup or be
retired.

**Q41. What is a package unit vs a split system?**
Package unit: everything in one outdoor cabinet, air ducted into
the house. Common on ground-level in southern climates. Split
system: outdoor condenser + indoor evaporator, connected by
refrigerant lines. Most common configuration.

**Q42. Why does my furnace smell like fuel oil when it runs?**
If it is an oil furnace, mild oil smell during a nozzle change or
startup is normal. Strong or constant oil smell is not: it means
a leak or incomplete combustion. Call us.

**Q43. Does high altitude affect HVAC sizing?**
Yes. Air is thinner at altitude, so combustion equipment needs
derating and cooling capacity changes. Manufacturers publish
altitude adjustment tables and we apply them on quotes above about
2,000 feet.

**Q44. Why is my ductless mini-split leaking water inside?**
Usually a clogged condensate drain line inside the wall or a
kinked drain hose behind the indoor unit. Turn it off and call us.

**Q45. Does an HVAC system need to be sized differently for a home with lots of glass?**
Yes. Large glass areas add significant cooling load in summer and
heating load in winter. The Manual J calculation factors this in.

**Q46. What is a load-matched system?**
When the heating capacity, cooling capacity, blower size, and
ductwork are all sized correctly for the actual heat load of the
house. Comfort is dramatically better and utility bills drop.

**Q47. My furnace tripped and shows an error code. Should I clear it?**
You can try one reset (turn the switch off, wait 60 seconds, turn
it back on). If it trips again, do not keep resetting it. Note the
error code and call us.

**Q48. Do smart vents interfere with my system warranty?**
Generally no, but they can increase duct pressure enough to
trigger high-static-pressure faults. We recommend zoning at the
main trunks instead of at the registers for whole-house control.

**Q49. Can I use my whole-home humidifier if I have a heat pump?**
Yes, but wire it correctly. Heat pumps deliver air at lower
temperatures than furnaces, so a bypass humidifier without its own
fan may not evaporate enough moisture. Steam humidifiers work
well with heat pumps.

**Q50. What is one thing every homeowner should do this weekend for their HVAC?**
Change the filter. If it has been more than three months, do it
today. It is the single highest-impact thing a homeowner can do
for their system.
