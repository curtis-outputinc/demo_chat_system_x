# Service practices and troubleshooting reference

Field-oriented reference material covering diagnostic mindset,
common complaints, and troubleshooting patterns. Complements the
customer-facing FAQ files; used by the bot when a technically
curious visitor drills deeper or when a business owner on the HVAC
side wants to see the tool understands real trade work.

## HVAC purpose and scope

HVAC systems control indoor temperature, indoor humidity, air
circulation, ventilation, indoor air quality, comfort, equipment
safety, building pressure, and energy efficiency. The main systems
found in homes and light commercial buildings include forced-air
furnaces, central air conditioners, heat pumps, boilers, radiant
heating, ductless mini-splits, rooftop units, packaged units, air
handlers, fan coils, make-up air units, exhaust systems,
humidifiers, dehumidifiers, HRVs, ERVs, thermostats, and zoning
controls.

The most common residential setup is a gas furnace, central air
conditioner, ductwork to distribute air, a thermostat to control
operation, and a filter to protect the equipment and clean the
air.

A typical forced-air system has return air ducts pulling air from
the home, a filter cabinet or slot, a blower motor, a heat
exchanger or evaporator coil, supply ducts delivering conditioned
air, and vents and registers in each room.

## The technician's mindset

HVAC is a system, not a collection of parts. Many problems that
look like equipment problems are actually airflow, ductwork,
thermostat, filter, drainage, control-signal, or capacitor
problems. A good technician thinks in systems.

Key diagnostic questions:

- What is the system supposed to be doing right now?
- What is it actually doing?
- When did the problem start?
- Constant or intermittent?
- Has anything changed recently: filter change, renovation,
  painting, drywall or flooring work?
- Are there any error codes?
- Is the thermostat calling?
- Is there power? Fuel? Airflow? Drainage?
- Are safety switches open? Is the system locked out?

Start with safety before diagnosis. Never assume a system is safe
because it was running yesterday. Always check for electrical
hazards, gas leaks, carbon monoxide risk, improper venting,
blocked intake or exhaust, water leaks, refrigerant leaks, hot
surfaces, sharp sheet metal edges, moving blower wheels and fan
blades, combustion issues, and condensate overflow risk.

Never bypass safety switches as a permanent fix. Safety controls
exist because equipment can become dangerous.

A clean diagnosis is better than a fast guess. The most expensive
repair is the one that does not fix the actual problem.

Explain problems in plain language. Customers care about: is it
safe, can it be fixed, how much will it cost, how long will it
take, will it happen again, should I repair or replace, is there
anything I could have done to prevent it, is the equipment under
warranty, is this urgent?

## Common customer complaints and what they usually mean

- "My furnace is not turning on." No thermostat call, no power,
  tripped breaker, furnace switch off, door switch open, blown
  low-voltage fuse, failed transformer, failed control board,
  open safety switch, failed inducer, failed pressure switch, or
  faulty igniter.
- "My furnace turns on but shuts off quickly." Dirty flame sensor,
  grounding issue, gas pressure problem, poor flame carryover,
  control board issue, venting problem, or condensate problem.
- "My furnace is blowing cold air." Thermostat fan set to "on"
  instead of "auto," gas supply interrupted, ignition failure,
  cracked heat exchanger safety trip, dirty flame sensor, or
  system in cooldown after high-limit trip.
- "My thermostat is blank." Dead batteries, tripped breaker, blown
  low-voltage fuse, open furnace door switch, wiring problem.
- "My AC is running but not cooling." Low refrigerant, dirty
  condenser coil, frozen coil, dirty filter, failed compressor,
  bad capacitor, or airflow issue.
- "My AC is leaking water." Clogged condensate drain, cracked drain
  pan, frozen coil melting, failed condensate pump.
- "My outdoor AC unit is not spinning." Tripped breaker, bad
  capacitor, failed contactor, wiring issue, or compressor
  failure.
- "My AC is frozen." Restricted airflow, low refrigerant, dirty
  filter, dirty coil, blower issue, or metering device issue.
- "The system is making a loud noise." Loose parts, blower motor
  bearings, belt slipping, worn compressor, ignition delay bang,
  or debris.
- "Some rooms are hot and some are cold." Duct imbalance, closed
  vents, poor insulation, thermostat placement, leaky windows,
  incorrect system sizing.
- "My energy bills are too high." Dirty equipment, aging system,
  leaking ducts, thermostat setting, insulation, air infiltration,
  or oversized/undersized system.
- "The house is too dry." Winter air is drier by nature; consider
  a whole-home humidifier.
- "The house is too humid." Oversized AC that short-cycles, poor
  AC performance, unvented moisture sources, humidity from
  basement or crawlspace.
- "There is a burning smell." Dust burning off (normal briefly at
  start of heating season), or something is actually overheating
  (electrical, motor, bearing) - turn off and inspect.
- "There is a musty smell." Mold on the evaporator coil, dirty
  filter, clogged drain, or contaminated ducts.
- "The furnace room smells like gas." Evacuate. Call the gas
  utility.
- "The system runs constantly." Extreme weather, undersized
  equipment, leaky ducts, poor insulation, low refrigerant,
  thermostat issue.
- "The system short cycles." Oversized equipment, dirty filter,
  overheating (heat mode), low refrigerant (cool mode), thermostat
  problem, control fault.
- "The filter gets dirty too fast." Heavy dust source, pets,
  construction, high system runtime, or leaky return ducts pulling
  in attic or crawlspace air.
- "The airflow is weak." Dirty filter, dirty coil, weak blower,
  crushed flex duct, closed vents, undersized returns.
- "The vents are noisy." Undersized ductwork, high static
  pressure, loose grille, or velocity-induced whistling.
- "The ductwork bangs." Thermal expansion of metal ducts as they
  heat and cool. Sometimes fixable by adding turning vanes or
  swapping to a slower ramp on the blower.
- "The humidifier is leaking." Solenoid stuck open, clogged drain,
  cracked pad, or water panel not seated.
- "The boiler pressure is low." Auto-fill valve, water leak, or
  drained expansion tank. Not always safe to just refill; find
  the reason first.
- "The water heater is not making hot water." Pilot out, gas
  supply, ignition failure, thermostat, dip tube, sediment
  buildup, or failed element (electric).
- "The pilot light keeps going out." Bad thermocouple, weak
  pilot flame, draft, or gas supply issue.
- "The carbon monoxide alarm went off." Get everyone out, call
  911, then arrange to have all combustion equipment inspected.
- "The thermostat says heat / cool is on but nothing happens."
  Communication fault, tripped safety, delay timer, blown fuse,
  or failed control board.

## Root cause thinking

The easy answer is often not the real answer. Two examples:

- Limit switch opens on a furnace. Easy answer: "bad limit
  switch." Better question: why is the furnace overheating? Real
  causes: dirty filter, weak blower, dirty secondary heat
  exchanger, blocked return air, closed vents, oversized furnace,
  restricted ductwork, dirty evaporator coil.
- AC coil freezes. Easy answer: "low refrigerant." Better
  question: why is the coil dropping below freezing? Real causes:
  low airflow, low refrigerant, dirty filter, dirty coil, blower
  issue, metering device issue, duct restriction.

Good techs verify: voltage, amperage, temperature rise, static
pressure, refrigerant pressures, superheat, subcooling, combustion
performance when applicable, drainage, airflow, control signals,
and actual operation after repair.

## Filter reality

The number-one homeowner maintenance task is changing the filter.
A dirty filter can cause poor airflow, furnace overheating, AC
coil freezing, higher energy bills, poor comfort, blower motor
stress, dust buildup, short cycling, and reduced equipment life.

Check monthly. Replace every 1 to 3 months for standard 1-inch
filters. Homes with pets, renovations, or heavy dust need more
frequent changes. High-MERV filters improve filtration but can
restrict airflow if the system was not designed for them. A filter
that is too restrictive can damage performance even if it is
"better" on paper.

Never run a furnace or air handler without a filter.

## Furnace safety-adjacent notes

- A furnace that starts and shuts off after a few seconds usually
  has a dirty flame sensor.
- A furnace that overheats usually has an airflow problem.
- A cracked heat exchanger is serious. Signs: visible cracks or
  corrosion, flame disturbance when the blower starts, soot,
  unusual odors, CO readings, rollout switch trips, rusted or
  deteriorated sections.
- Heat exchanger inspections should be done carefully by qualified
  technicians.
- Carbon monoxide testing is important on combustion appliances.
- A furnace should not be operated if it is unsafe.

## High-efficiency furnace notes

High-efficiency furnaces extract more heat from combustion gases
than older furnaces. Because they extract more heat, the exhaust
is cooler and produces condensation. They vent through PVC, CPVC,
or approved plastic venting depending on code and manufacturer.

Common HE furnace problems include clogged condensate drain,
blocked condensate trap, blocked intake or exhaust pipe, ice
buildup at vent termination, water in pressure switch tubing,
failed pressure switch, failed inducer, cracked drain tubing,
plugged secondary heat exchanger, poor slope on vent piping,
improper vent termination, or corrosion from condensate leakage.

If a high-efficiency furnace shuts down during snowstorms, check
exhaust termination, intake termination, snow buildup, ice
blockage, wind effects, and recirculation of exhaust into intake.

Pressure switch errors are often NOT the pressure switch itself.
They are usually caused by a blocked vent, blocked intake, weak
inducer, cracked hose, water in hose, clogged condensate trap,
poor vent slope, restricted heat exchanger, or wind or termination
effects. Replacing the pressure switch without checking these does
not fix the real problem.

## AC diagnostics

Central AC has an outdoor condenser, compressor, condenser fan
motor, condenser coil, capacitor, contactor, refrigerant lines,
indoor evaporator coil, metering device, blower motor, drain pan,
condensate drain, and thermostat.

AC does not "make cold." It moves heat. The evaporator absorbs
heat from indoor air; the condenser rejects it outdoors.

Common metering devices: fixed orifice, piston, TXV, electronic
expansion valve.

Low airflow signs: frozen coil, poor cooling, low suction
pressure, compressor stress, water leakage after thawing, poor
humidity control, long run times.

Low refrigerant means a leak. Refrigerant is not consumed. Signs
of low refrigerant: poor cooling, ice on suction line, frozen
coil, long run times, low suction pressure, hissing or bubbling
at leak point, oil stains near refrigerant joints.

Refrigerant handling is regulated. Homeowners should not attempt
it.

Dirty condenser coil signs: high head pressure, poor cooling,
compressor overheating, higher energy use, shortened compressor
life.

Temperature split is a quick check. Typical AC split: 16°F to 22°F
between return and supply. Low split can mean poor cooling or
excessive airflow. Very high split can mean low airflow.

Humidity matters as much as temperature. 74°F and high humidity
feels worse than 76°F and lower humidity. Short cycling reduces
humidity removal. Continuous fan operation can re-evaporate
moisture from the coil and reduce dehumidification.

## Heat pump reality

Heat pumps move heat rather than create it. In cooling mode they
work like an AC. In heating mode they reverse and pull heat from
outside to inside.

Backup heat can be electric resistance, a gas furnace (dual-fuel),
or hydronic support.

Defrost mode is normal. During defrost the outdoor unit may steam
and appear to have a problem. Customers may think the unit is
smoking. It is water vapor from ice melting off the coil.

Common heat pump issues: excessive frosting, failed reversing
valve, defrost board issue, outdoor sensor issue, low refrigerant,
compressor issue, auxiliary heat failure, thermostat setup
problem, poor airflow, incorrect balance point.

Heat pump supply air feels cooler than furnace air. Customers used
to gas furnaces may think the air is "not hot enough." It is. The
delivery temperature is lower but the system runs longer and warms
the house steadily.

Heat pumps work best with steady thermostat settings. Large
setbacks make the backup heat run more often, which negates the
efficiency advantage.

## Boiler reality

A boiler heats water, not air. Water goes to radiators, baseboard
heaters, radiant floor loops, fan coils, air handlers, or indirect
water heaters.

Boiler components: burner, heat exchanger, circulator pump,
expansion tank, pressure relief valve, aquastat, zone valves, air
separator, backflow preventer, pressure reducing valve, low water
cutoff, venting, controls.

Boiler pressure needs to be right. Too low prevents circulation.
Too high pops the relief valve. Common cold pressure is 12 to 15
psi. Rises with heat.

If pressure rises too high: expansion tank is waterlogged or
failed. Signs: relief valve dripping, quick pressure rise on
heating, big pressure swings, tank feels heavy, no air charge on
the bladder.

Air in a hydronic system causes gurgling, no heat in some zones,
poor circulation, pump noise, uneven heating. Purge air properly;
bleeding radiators alone may not do it.

Boiler pumps can fail or seize. Zone valves can fail open or
closed. A thermostat may call but a zone valve may not open. A
zone valve may open but fail to trigger the boiler.

Boiler short-cycling causes: oversized boiler, control settings,
low water flow, small heating zones, dirty heat exchanger,
thermostat, boiler temperature settings, outdoor reset issues.

Condensate from a condensing boiler is acidic. Must drain
properly. Never plug or cap a pressure relief valve discharge -
it is a safety device.

## Water heater complaints

- No hot water: pilot out (older units), gas supply, breaker
  (electric), thermostat, dip tube, sediment buildup, failed
  element.
- Not enough hot water: undersized tank, high demand, dip tube
  failure, sediment buildup, low thermostat setting.
- Water too hot: thermostat set too high, stuck thermostat, mixing
  valve failure.
- Pilot will not stay lit: bad thermocouple, weak pilot, draft,
  gas supply.
- Burner will not ignite: gas supply, thermocouple / thermopile,
  ignition module, sensor.
- Leaking tank: usually terminal, replacement time.
- Leaking fitting: often repairable.
- Rusty water: sacrificial anode is spent or tank is corroding
  internally.
- Rotten egg smell: bacteria reacting with the anode; replace with
  a different alloy anode (aluminum-zinc).
- Popping / rumbling: sediment buildup on the burner floor.
- Relief valve leaking: pressure too high, thermal expansion, or
  failed relief valve.

Sediment causes popping, reduced hot water, lower efficiency,
overheating, and shorter tank life. Flush periodically depending
on water quality.

Tankless units need annual descaling in hard water areas. Isolation
valves and a small pump flush citric or acetic acid through the
heat exchanger.

Tankless is not always a drop-in replacement for a tank unit. Gas
line sizing, venting, and flow rates may need to change.

## Ventilation, HRVs, ERVs

Modern homes are tighter than older homes. Tight homes need
mechanical ventilation. HRVs transfer heat between outgoing stale
and incoming fresh air. ERVs transfer both heat and moisture.

HRVs are common in colder climates. ERVs are useful where humidity
transfer matters (summer humidity control in the south).

HRV / ERV maintenance: clean or replace filters, clean the core,
check intake and exhaust hoods, check drains, check fan operation,
check controls, balance airflow if needed.

Blocked intakes reduce ventilation. Dirty filters restrict airflow.
Poor ventilation can cause stale air, odors, condensation, high
humidity, poor indoor air quality, window moisture, mold risk.

Too much ventilation wastes energy and dries the home in winter.

Exhaust-only ventilation creates negative pressure. Negative
pressure can pull air from garages, crawlspaces, chimneys, or
combustion appliance areas. Combustion appliances and ventilation
must be considered together.

## Indoor air quality drivers

- Filtration
- Ventilation
- Humidity
- Dust
- Pets
- Mold
- Combustion appliances
- Cleaning chemicals
- Building materials
- Outdoor air
- Duct leakage
- Occupant behavior

Common IAQ concerns: dust, allergies, dry air, high humidity,
musty odors, cooking odors, pet odors, smoke, VOCs from paints and
building materials.
