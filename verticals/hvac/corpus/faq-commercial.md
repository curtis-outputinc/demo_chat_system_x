# Commercial FAQ

For property managers, building owners, business operators, and
facility managers looking at HVAC service, maintenance, or
replacement for a commercial building. Duplicated questions from
multiple source lists have been merged.

## Commercial heating

**Why is the building not heating properly?**
Common causes: thermostat or control issue, dirty filters, failed
burners, blower or belt problem, closed damper, low gas pressure,
zoning fault, or equipment that is undersized for the load.

**Why are some areas of the building warm and others cold?**
Zoning imbalance, ductwork leaks or blockage, closed dampers,
poor insulation in one area, thermostat placement, or equipment
that has drifted out of balance since original commissioning. Air
balancing usually restores comfort.

**Do you service commercial boilers?**
Yes. Cast-iron, condensing, hydronic, steam. Common calls: no
heat, low pressure, banging or gurgling, failed pump, stuck zone
valve, cracked expansion tank, ignition fault.

**Why is our commercial heating system short cycling?**
Dirty filter, blocked airflow, failed sensor, thermostat problem,
oversized equipment, or a control board that keeps calling for
heat too aggressively.

**Do you service unit heaters in warehouses?**
Yes. Suspended overhead gas and electric unit heaters,
propeller-fan and tube-exhaust models.

**What if employees smell gas near the heating equipment?**
Evacuate the area. Do not touch light switches. Call the gas
utility and emergency services. Do not try to restart the
equipment yourself. Call us once safe to send a tech.

**How often should commercial heating equipment be inspected?**
At minimum, before heating season. Bi-annual is common. High-use
buildings (restaurants, hospitals, 24-hour operations) inspect
quarterly or monthly.

## Commercial cooling

**Why is the commercial AC not cooling?**
Dirty filters, low refrigerant, dirty coils, failed compressor,
electrical fault, control issue, blocked airflow, or a stuck
economizer damper.

**Why is our rooftop unit running but not cooling?**
Refrigerant issue, compressor lockout, failed capacitor or
contactor, dirty condenser coil, economizer damper problem, or a
control fault.

**Why is our commercial AC freezing up?**
Low refrigerant, restricted airflow, dirty filter, blower issue,
or dirty coils. Turn it off, let it thaw, and call us.

**Why is water leaking from a rooftop unit into the building below?**
Clogged primary condensate drain, cracked drain pan, frozen coil
that is melting, failed pump, blocked drainage line, or negative
pressure sucking water back into the unit.

**Can you service compressors and rooftop electrical?**
Yes. Compressor diagnosis, contactor and capacitor replacement,
control board repair, and rooftop-specific electrical work.

**How often should commercial AC units be serviced?**
Twice a year minimum. Quarterly is common for offices and retail.
Monthly for restaurants, hospitals, and buildings with critical
operations.

**Do you service server room and IT cooling?**
Yes. Precision cooling (CRAC) units, dedicated split systems for
IT rooms, and reliability-focused maintenance for spaces where
downtime is expensive.

**What is a chiller and how is it different from an AC?**
A chiller cools water or a glycol mix instead of air. The chilled
water is pumped through the building to air handlers in each
zone. Chillers make sense in large facilities with many zones or
multiple floors.

**What is a cooling tower?**
The heat-rejection side of a water-cooled chiller. Warm water from
the chiller enters the tower, evaporates a small percentage to the
outdoor air, and returns cooler.

check, electrical inspection (voltage, amp draws, contactor
condition), refrigerant check, drain and pan cleaning, safety
control testing, control board review, and a written service
report.

Depends on the building. Common cadences: monthly for restaurants

## Replacement and installation

**How do we know when to replace commercial HVAC?**
Recommended when equipment is old, unreliable, inefficient,
expensive to repair, no longer meeting building demand, or when
refrigerant is being phased out. The Rule of 4: multiply age by
annual repair cost. If it exceeds 40% of a new system's cost,
replace.

**How long does commercial HVAC equipment last?**
RTUs and split systems: 15 to 20 years. Chillers and commercial
boilers: 20 to 30 years with strict maintenance.

**How long does an RTU replacement take?**
Simple crane-lift replacements: 4 to 8 hours per unit. Full
projects with crane coordination, permits, curb adapters, and
controls integration take several weeks of prep for one day of
installation.

**Do you help size commercial HVAC systems?**
Yes. Every commercial replacement starts with a load calculation
that considers square footage, occupancy, insulation, ventilation
rates, internal heat loads, and hours of operation.

**Can HVAC replacement be done without closing the business?**
Almost always. Work is scheduled after hours, on weekends, in
phases, or during slower periods.

**Do you handle permits for commercial installations?**
Yes. We pull the permits and coordinate inspections.

**Do you work with our general contractor or building engineer?**
Yes. We regularly coordinate with GCs, electricians, plumbers,
engineers, roofers, and property managers on larger projects.

**What is a Tenant Improvement (TI) HVAC job?**
Modifying or re-routing existing ductwork, VAV boxes, and
diffusers to fit a new floor plan requested by an incoming
commercial tenant.

**Who pays for commercial HVAC repairs, landlord or tenant?**
Depends on the lease. Triple Net (NNN) leases usually put
maintenance and repair on the tenant. Modified gross leases
usually keep it with the landlord. Read the lease.

## Ventilation and IAQ

**Do commercial HVAC systems handle ventilation?**
Yes. Many are designed to also introduce fresh outdoor air and
exhaust stale air. Ventilation rates are governed by ASHRAE
Standard 62.1 based on occupancy and space type.

**Why does our building feel stuffy?**
Poor ventilation, dirty filters, blocked ducts, high occupancy,
control issues, or inadequate fresh air intake.

**Can you improve indoor air quality?**
Yes. Options include better filtration (MERV 13+), ventilation
adjustments, UV coil purifiers, humidity control, duct cleaning,
and DOAS (Dedicated Outdoor Air Systems).

**Do you service make-up air units?**
Yes. Common in restaurants, commercial kitchens, warehouses, and
buildings with heavy exhaust.

**Why is our commercial kitchen too hot?**
Exhaust hood pulling too much air out without adequate make-up
air, poor airflow balance, undersized cooling for the kitchen
heat load, or ventilation design that never worked to begin with.

**Can HVAC issues cause odors in a commercial building?**
Yes. Dirty filters, clogged drains, mold, stagnant air, sewer gas
back-flowing through a dry trap, kitchen exhaust issues, or
contaminated ductwork.

**Can you help with humidity in a commercial space?**
Yes. Improved AC operation, ventilation adjustments,
dehumidification equipment, drainage fixes, and building pressure
balancing.

**Why is there condensation on vents or ducts?**
Cold surfaces meeting humid air. Points to insulation issues,
airflow problems, or oversized cooling equipment.

**Do commercial buildings need air balancing?**
Yes, at commissioning and after any major change. Air balancing
ensures the right amount of air reaches each area.

**What is building pressure?**
The relative pressure of the building compared to outside.
Positive pressure keeps outside air out and helps with efficiency.
Negative pressure sucks unconditioned air, moisture, and odors in
through doors and windows.

## Controls and energy

**Do you service commercial thermostats?**
Yes. Install, repair, program, and troubleshoot commercial
thermostats and zone controls.

**Why does the thermostat say cooling is on but the building is warm?**
Equipment fault (compressor, refrigerant, electrical), control
board issue, communication fault, or a stuck damper. Requires
diagnosis.

**Can we control different areas separately?**
Yes. Zoning systems with motorized dampers and separate
thermostats let each area hold its own temperature.

**What is a building automation system (BAS)?**
A centralized computer system that monitors and controls HVAC,
lighting, and other equipment through a single dashboard. Enables
scheduling, remote monitoring, alerts, and demand-response
integration.

**Can you connect HVAC to a building automation system?**
Yes. We work with existing BAS platforms and coordinate with
controls specialists on larger integrations.

**How can we reduce commercial HVAC energy costs?**
Regular maintenance, better scheduling, filter changes, coil
cleaning, duct sealing, upgrading to variable-frequency drives,
right-sizing equipment on replacement, fixing stuck economizers,
and correcting ventilation over-supply.

**Can HVAC maintenance improve energy efficiency?**
Yes. Clean coils, clean filters, correct refrigerant charge,
working economizers, and properly tensioned belts all translate
directly to energy savings.

**Why are our energy bills suddenly higher?**
Dirty equipment, failed control, leaking ducts, stuck damper, low
refrigerant, failing motor, or the system running longer than
scheduled. Diagnosable.

**What is a VFD (Variable Frequency Drive)?**
A drive that modulates the electrical frequency to a motor,
letting fans or pumps run at partial speed instead of always
full-blast. Significant energy savings on retrofit.

**What is an economizer and how does it save money?**
A damper system that opens to pull cool outdoor air into the
building when the outside is cool and dry. Provides "free cooling"
during shoulder seasons without running the compressor.

## Business, safety, scheduling

**Can HVAC problems affect our customers or employees?**
Yes. Poor heating, cooling, ventilation, humidity, or air quality
affects comfort, productivity, customer experience, and sometimes
health and safety.

**Do you provide documentation for landlords or property managers?**
Yes. Invoices, service reports, photos, repair recommendations,
maintenance records, and warranty paperwork.

**Can you prioritize urgent commercial service calls?**
Yes. Business no-heat, no-cooling, safety concerns, and
operational disruption get top priority.

**How do we choose the right commercial HVAC company?**
Look for commercial experience, valid state license, general
liability and workers' comp insurance, EPA Section 608
certification, NATE or MSCA certifications for technicians, 24/7
emergency availability, clear reporting, and references from
similar buildings.

**What certifications should I verify?**
State/local mechanical contractor license, general liability and
workers' comp insurance, EPA Section 608 for refrigerant handling,
and NATE or MSCA certifications for field technicians. Ask.

**How long does a maintenance visit take?**
Typically 45 to 90 minutes per unit, depending on equipment size
and condition.

**What information should we have ready when calling for service?**
The building address, unit type (RTU, split, boiler, etc.),
serial or asset tag if known, symptom, whether tenants are being
affected, and preferred access window.

## Regulations and refrigerants

**What is happening with R-410A refrigerant?**
It is being phased out for new equipment installations because of
its global warming potential. Existing R-410A systems can still be
serviced with reclaimed refrigerant.

**What are A2L refrigerants?**
The new generation of low-GWP refrigerants: R-32 and R-454B are
the most common. They are mildly flammable, so new equipment
using them has specific safety design requirements (spark-
resistant components, leak detection sensors).

**Can we retrofit an old R-410A system to run A2L refrigerant?**
No. A2L refrigerants require redesigned components, leak
detection, and updated electrical. Retrofitting is unsafe and not
legal.

**Can we still buy R-410A for existing systems?**
Yes. Reclaimed R-410A is available for servicing existing
equipment. Supply will contract over time and cost will trend up.

**Do commercial buildings have to meet specific ventilation standards?**
Yes. ASHRAE Standard 62.1 sets minimum ventilation rates by space
type and occupancy. Healthcare has stricter requirements under
ASHRAE 170. Cleanrooms and industrial spaces have additional
requirements.

## Industry-specific

**What HVAC do restaurants need?**
Heavy-duty exhaust hoods, integrated make-up air units, dining
room comfort with a hot kitchen next door, and code-compliant
grease-laden air filtration.

**What HVAC do medical clinics need?**
ASHRAE 170 compliance: high air-change rates, positive or
negative pressure isolation depending on the room, HEPA filtration
in specific areas. We help design and maintain to those standards.

**What HVAC do server rooms and data centers need?**
Precision cooling (CRAC) units that run 24/7, tight temperature
and humidity control, and redundant equipment so a single failure
does not overheat servers.

**What HVAC do warehouses need?**
For heating: overhead radiant tube heaters or unit heaters,
depending on ceiling height and heat load. For cooling: often
none, but some warehouses need targeted evaporative cooling or
spot cooling for staff areas.

**What HVAC do offices need?**
Right-sized RTUs or split systems, VAV boxes for zoning, quality
filtration (MERV 13 is now standard), reliable ventilation, and
smart controls that scale up during occupied hours and back off
during nights and weekends.

**What HVAC do retail spaces need?**
Rapid-response cooling that handles fluctuating occupancy and
open exterior doors. Storefront doors often open frequently in
summer, which loads the system heavily.

**What HVAC do hotels use?**
Packaged Terminal Air Conditioners (PTACs) or Vertical Package
Terminal (VTAC) units in each guest room. Self-contained,
individually controlled, no ducted noise transfer between rooms.
