# Mortgage Refinancing: Frequently Asked Questions

Plain-language Q&A reference for the kinds of questions borrowers ask
about refinancing. Synthesized from two AI-generated sources (Gemini
and ChatGPT), deduplicated semantically, plus 50 additional
borrower-facing questions added. Framework content on conventional /
alt / private buckets already lives in the existing corpus files;
this file is the refi-specific Q&A.

## How the agent should use this file

These Q&As give the agent background context for refinancing
conversations. Typical ranges noted (penalty calculations, closing
cost ranges $1,500-$3,000, discharge fees $200-$400, appraisal fees,
2-4 week processing) are general Ontario market context, not quotes.
Real numbers vary by lender, file, location, and date.

The agent should still defer to a licensed broker for specifics on
any individual file. Borrowers in active urgency (closing in days,
financing condition deadline approaching) should be routed to the
broker for same-day follow-up.

## Category 1: Basic Rules and Core Mechanics

1. What does it mean to refinance a mortgage?
Refinancing means breaking your current mortgage agreement and
replacing it with a brand-new loan. Common reasons: lower the
interest rate, change lenders, change the loan structure, or pull
cash equity out of the home.

2. What is the maximum loan-to-value (LTV) for a mortgage refinance?
Under Canadian regulations, refinances are capped at a maximum of 80%
LTV for uninsured conventional mortgages. You must leave at least 20%
equity untouched in the home.

3. Can I refinance if I have less than 20% equity?
No, not through traditional equity-takeout refinancing. High-ratio
insured mortgages are not eligible for cash-out refinancing. If you
have less than 20% equity, options narrow to renewal, a switch with
no added money, or alternative private financing depending on the
file.

4. What is the difference between a renewal and a refinance?
A renewal is signing a new term on the same outstanding balance with
your current lender at the end of your existing term. A refinance
breaks the current contract, creates a new loan, and is used to add
money, change amortization, switch lenders, or restructure the
mortgage.

5. Do I have to pass the mortgage stress test to refinance?
Yes. A refinance creates a new loan facility, so federally regulated
lenders are required to qualify you using the Minimum Qualifying
Rate (the greater of your contract rate plus 2% or 5.25%).

6. Does the 4.5x loan-to-income (LTI) portfolio cap apply to refinances?
Yes. OSFI's 4.5x LTI portfolio cap counts refinances as new credit
actions, so the file must fit within the lender's portfolio
limits. Straight switches between FRFIs (no added money, no
amortization increase) are exempt from the LTI cap and stress test.

7. Can I refinance a fixed-rate mortgage, or only variable?
You can refinance either, but breaking a fixed-rate mortgage early
typically triggers a much larger penalty (the Interest Rate
Differential) than breaking a variable-rate mortgage (capped at
three months interest).

## Category 2: Prepayment Penalties

8. How is the penalty calculated to break a fixed-rate mortgage?
Lenders charge the GREATER of three months interest or the Interest
Rate Differential (IRD). If current market rates have fallen below
your contract rate, the IRD penalty can be several thousand dollars.

9. How is the penalty calculated to break a variable-rate mortgage?
Variable-rate breakage is typically capped at exactly three months of
interest on the outstanding balance. This is much cheaper than the
IRD that fixed-rate breakage can trigger.

10. Can I avoid the penalty entirely when refinancing?
Sometimes. Two approaches: wait until your current term matures and
refinance at renewal (no penalty), or ask your existing lender for a
"blend and extend" that absorbs the new financing without triggering
the breakage penalty.

11. What is a "blend and extend" refinance?
A strategy where your current lender blends your existing interest
rate with the current market rate into a new, longer term. Lets you
access additional equity or extend the amortization without paying
the upfront breakage penalty that a full refinance to a new lender
would cost.

12. Can the breakage penalty be deducted from my new refinance cash?
Yes. At closing, the lawyer deducts the prepayment penalty, the
discharge fee, and legal costs directly from the new mortgage
proceeds. You receive whatever cash is left after those deductions.

13. What is a mortgage discharge fee?
If you move your mortgage to a different lender, the old lender
charges an administrative fee (typically $200 to $400 in Ontario) to
remove their charge from your land title. The new lender then
registers their own charge.

## Category 3: Debt Consolidation Strategies

14. Can I refinance to pay off high-interest credit card debt?
Yes. This is one of the most common refinance use cases. Using
mortgage equity (lower interest rate, longer amortization) to clear
credit card debt (typically 19-29% APR) drastically reduces monthly
out-of-pocket payment obligations.

15. How does consolidating debt into a mortgage lower my monthly payments?
The strategy stretches short-term high-interest debt across a long
mortgage amortization (up to 30 years for conventional refinance)
at a much lower rate. The single mortgage payment is lower than the
combined payments on the debts being consolidated.

16. Will consolidating debt into a refinance hurt my credit score?
No, the opposite usually applies. Credit utilization on your credit
cards drops to near 0% (which strengthens credit), and scattered debt
payments are replaced by a single installment payment. Over time,
this typically improves the score.

17. Can I refinance to pay off a consumer proposal or bankruptcy?
Yes. Alternative and B-lenders frequently allow refinances up to 80%
LTV specifically to pay out an active consumer proposal at closing,
which discharges the proposal and restores financial standing.

18. What is the risk of consolidating credit card debt into a refinance?
The primary risk is behavioral. If you don't close the credit cards
after consolidating and instead run the balances back up, you end up
with double the total debt (new card balances PLUS the larger
mortgage). The discipline to keep the cards paid off afterwards is
the critical piece.

19. Can I consolidate my spouse's debts into my refinance if they aren't on title?
Usually only if the spouse is added to the mortgage application as a
co-borrower. That makes both parties legally responsible for the
restructured mortgage. A lawyer should explain title and ownership
implications.

## Category 4: Equity Takeout for Renovations and Investment

20. How do I access cash from my home for major renovations?
Two main paths: an equity-takeout refinance (advances a lump sum at
closing) or a Home Equity Line of Credit (HELOC, draw funds as
needed). The right choice depends on whether the renovation cost is
known upfront or staged.

21. What is an equity takeout mortgage?
A refinance structured to increase the total loan balance (up to the
80% LTV cap) and pay the excess cash directly to the borrower at
closing. The borrower walks away with cash plus a larger mortgage.

22. Can I refinance based on what my home will be worth AFTER renovations?
Generally no, not through a standard refinance. Traditional refinances
use current market value. To finance based on after-improvement
value, the file usually moves to a specialized construction-draw
refinance or a private renovation product.

23. Is a HELOC or a standard refinance better for a home renovation?
If the contractor needs the money all upfront, a standard refinance
locks in an interest rate and disburses the cash at closing. If the
renovation happens in unpredictable stages, a HELOC is better
because you only pay interest on what you draw.

24. Can I refinance my home to fund the down payment on an investment property?
Yes. You can extract equity from your primary residence up to the 80%
LTV cap and use the net cash as a conventional down payment on a
second property (rental, cottage, second home).

## Category 5: Costs, Fees, and Timelines

25. What are the total closing costs for a refinance?
Budget approximately $1,500 to $3,000 in standard refinance closing
costs. These cover the new appraisal, real estate lawyer fees, title
insurance registration, and lender administrative charges.

26. Why do I need a new appraisal to refinance with my current bank?
Real estate values change. Lenders require an independent up-to-date
valuation to accurately calculate the 80% LTV ceiling. The
appraisal cost is generally $350-$600 and is paid by the borrower.

27. Do I need a lawyer for a refinance?
Yes. A licensed real estate lawyer is required to discharge the old
mortgage registration from the land registry and register the new
lender's charge on title. The lawyer also handles fund disbursement.

28. Can refinance legal fees be added to the new mortgage balance?
No, not added to the principal. The legal fees are paid out of the
refinance cash proceeds at closing, so you don't need to bring them
to the closing table out of pocket. You just receive less net cash.

29. How long does a refinance take from start to finish?
Typically 2 to 4 weeks. The timeline covers gathering income
documents, lender approval, the appraisal, and legal title
processing. Urgent files can sometimes move faster with attention.

## Category 6: Multi-Property and Investment Refinancing

30. Can I refinance a rental property up to 80% LTV?
Yes, residential rental properties can be refinanced up to 80% LTV,
but they face tighter cash-flow scrutiny than primary residences.
Lenders want lease agreements, deposit history, and a confirmation
that the property cash-flows on its own.

31. How do OSFI rules affect refinancing multiple investment properties?
Under current OSFI guidelines, if more than 50% of your qualifying
income is rental-based, the mortgage is classified as Income-Producing
Residential Real Estate (IPRRE). IPRRE files must pass independent
debt-servicing metrics rather than blending income across your
portfolio.

32. Can I use my regular salary to qualify for an investment property refinance?
Your salary easily backs your primary home and typically your first
rental property refinance. For subsequent investment properties,
each must increasingly demonstrate standalone rental cash flow.

33. What documents are needed for a rental property refinance?
Signed current lease agreements, 3 to 6 months of bank statements
showing consistent rental deposits, current property tax bills, and
your T1 General tax filings.

34. Can I extend my amortization to 30 years when I refinance?
Yes, if the refinance is uninsured (conventional, 80% LTV or less),
amortization up to 30 years is available to keep monthly payments
low. Insured refinances are subject to the standard insurance
amortization rules.

## Category 7: Underwriting, Income, and Credit

35. Can banks refinance a mortgage if I am self-employed?
Yes. Standard banks require 2 years of clean Notices of Assessment
(NOAs). If your tax write-downs are significant, you may need to
pivot to a B-lender stated-income program that uses bank statements
and business documents instead.

36. Can I refinance if I currently owe back taxes to the CRA?
Standard banks typically reject files with CRA tax debt. Alternative
and private lenders will approve the refinance on the strict
condition that the CRA debt is paid in full from the mortgage
proceeds at closing.

37. Can I refinance if my credit score has dropped below 600?
A major bank will say no. The broker can route the file to a
B-lender (minimum credit around 550) or a private equity lender
(no minimum credit). The exit plan back to A-lending becomes
important.

38. How do lenders factor child support or alimony I pay into a refinance?
Any legally mandated child support or spousal alimony you pay out is
either deducted from your gross income or added as a recurring debt
obligation, which lowers your maximum refinance amount.

39. Can I refinance to remove my ex-spouse's name from the mortgage and title?
Yes. This is a matrimonial refinance. You need a legally executed
separation agreement, and your individual income must qualify for
the entire mortgage balance on its own.

40. Can I refinance to add a new partner to the mortgage and title?
Yes. The refinance can add a co-owner and co-borrower. Their income
and credit history factor into the new approval.

## Category 8: Troubleshooting Scenarios

41. What if my current bank rejects my refinance due to high debt ratios?
Don't panic. Major banks are bound by strict OSFI rules. A broker
can pivot the application to a B-lender or credit union that uses
more flexible debt service parameters.

42. If my home value has dropped, can I still refinance?
If a market correction has dropped your home value below the level
needed to keep your existing balance under 80% LTV, you cannot
extract cash. You may need to pay down principal to bring the file
to a workable position.

43. What is a "switch and refinance"?
A combined transaction where you transfer your existing mortgage
balance to a new lender for a better rate (the switch) AND layer an
additional equity top-up at the same time (the refinance). Doing
both at once minimizes total fees.

44. Can I use a private second mortgage instead of refinancing the first?
Yes, and this is often the right move when your first mortgage has a
low locked-in rate and breaking it would trigger a heavy penalty. A
private second mortgage adds new funds without touching the first.

45. Can I refinance a property with a construction lien registered against it?
Yes, but only on the condition that the refinance proceeds are sent
directly to clear and remove that lien at closing.

46. Does refinancing require switching home insurance?
No. You keep your current policy. Your insurance broker updates the
"loss payable" clause to name the new mortgage lender before
funding.

47. Can I refinance while on long-term disability or maternity leave?
Yes. Lenders accept disability and maternity income with a verified
return-to-work date and confirmation from the employer detailing the
base salary you'll return to.

48. What if my appraisal comes in lower than expected?
The lender scales the maximum loan size to 80% of the lower
appraised value, which reduces the net cash payout. You may need to
revise the refinance target or bring more equity.

49. Can I pull out equity to invest in stocks, mutual funds, or crypto?
Yes, the cash is yours once it hits your account. Traditional
lenders will warn against using real estate leverage for volatile
investments, but legally the funds can be used for any purpose.

50. What is the most important metric a broker checks before starting a refinance?
The net equity spread. The broker calculates current home value
against outstanding debts to ensure that hitting the 80% LTV
ceiling yields enough cash, after all closing costs, to actually
achieve the borrower's financial goal.

## Category 9: Additional borrower questions

51. How often can I refinance my mortgage?
There is no legal limit on how often you can refinance, but each
refinance triggers prepayment penalties (if mid-term), closing
costs, and a new qualification. Most borrowers refinance every few
years at most, often coinciding with renewal periods.

52. Is there a minimum waiting period between refinances?
No statutory waiting period exists. Lenders may have their own
policies on how frequently they'll re-do paperwork. Cost-wise,
back-to-back refinances rarely make economic sense.

53. Can I refinance during the first year of my mortgage?
Yes, but the prepayment penalty in the first year can be substantial
on fixed-rate mortgages (IRD can be very large at low contract
rates). Variable-rate mortgages are cheaper to break early.

54. Can I refinance immediately after closing on my home?
Technically yes, but it is rarely worth it. You'd pay the closing
costs of the original purchase, then immediately pay all the
refinance closing costs. Most borrowers wait until a renewal or a
clear strategic reason.

55. Does refinancing reset my amortization?
It can. You choose the new amortization when refinancing. Resetting
to a longer amortization lowers payments but increases total
interest paid over the life of the loan.

56. How does refinancing affect my total interest paid?
It depends on the new rate and amortization. A lower rate alone
reduces total interest. Extending amortization while lowering rate
can leave total interest higher or lower than the original mortgage
depending on the specifics.

57. Will refinancing affect my credit score?
Slightly and temporarily. The hard credit inquiry causes a small
short-term dip. The long-term effect is neutral or positive if the
new mortgage is paid as agreed and debt consolidation reduces other
credit utilization.

58. Can I refinance with the same lender without an appraisal?
Sometimes. The current lender may waive the appraisal if they have a
recent value on file or if the LTV is clearly well below the cap. New
lenders almost always require a fresh appraisal.

59. Can I refinance to get rid of mortgage life insurance?
Yes. You're not obligated to renew mortgage life insurance when
moving to a new mortgage. Many borrowers prefer to replace it with
term life insurance, which is portable and often cheaper.

60. Can I change my payment frequency when I refinance?
Yes. Refinancing is a good opportunity to switch to accelerated
biweekly, weekly, or any other frequency the new lender supports.
This is independent of the rate and amortization choices.

61. How many credit pulls happen during a refinance?
Typically one hard pull per lender that formally reviews the file.
A mortgage broker often uses a single credit report across multiple
lender submissions to minimize the impact.

62. Do I need title insurance when I refinance?
Yes. The new lender requires title insurance on the refinanced
mortgage. Cost is typically $200-$400 in Ontario, paid at closing.

63. Can I do a cash-out refinance like in the United States?
Canadian equity-takeout refinance is the equivalent. The terminology
differs but the concept is the same: increase the mortgage balance,
receive the additional funds as cash. The 80% LTV cap applies.

64. Is mortgage interest tax-deductible in Canada?
Mortgage interest on a primary residence is generally NOT
tax-deductible. Interest on a portion of mortgage borrowing used for
business or investment income may be deductible. A tax professional
should confirm what applies to your situation.

65. Can I refinance to start a business?
Yes. The cash you pull from a refinance can be used for any purpose,
including starting a business. The interest on the portion used for
the business may be tax-deductible (consult a tax professional).

66. Can I refinance to fund a child's education?
Yes. Borrowers often refinance to access cash for tuition or
education expenses. Be aware that the cost of the borrowed funds
extends over the entire amortization period.

67. Will I have to requalify when I refinance?
Yes. A refinance is a brand-new loan and requires full qualification
under the lender's current standards, including income verification,
credit review, debt service ratios, and the stress test (for
federally regulated lenders).

68. Can I refinance if I'm retired?
Yes. Pension income, RRIF withdrawals, and other retirement income
counts. Lenders may apply specific rules for retirement-stage
borrowers, especially regarding amortization length.

69. Will my pension count as income for a refinance?
Yes. CPP, OAS, employer pensions, and other guaranteed retirement
income are generally accepted as qualifying income. The lender may
ask for benefit statements as documentation.

70. Can I refinance if I'm separated but not divorced?
Yes, but the file is more complex. The lender will want to see the
separation agreement and may require both spouses on the application
(or a clean spousal release) depending on whether both names are on
title.

71. Can I refinance multiple properties simultaneously?
Yes, but each property has its own appraisal, legal work, and
underwriting. Coordinating multiple closings adds complexity. A
broker can structure the timeline.

72. Can a real estate lawyer recommend a mortgage broker for my refinance?
Yes, lawyers often have relationships with brokers and can refer.
The borrower is free to choose any licensed broker.

73. Can the bank pay my refinance legal fees?
Some lenders offer "cashback" or "free legal" promotions that
effectively cover part of the refinance closing costs. These offers
come with strings (e.g., must stay with the lender for a number of
years or the cashback is clawed back).

74. What is a "no-fee" refinance offer?
A lender promotion that absorbs the legal, appraisal, and discharge
fees in exchange for a slightly higher rate or a longer mandatory
term. Math depends on the specifics; compare against a regular
refinance net of all costs.

75. Are there cashback offers for refinances?
Yes, some lenders offer cashback at closing as an incentive. The
cashback typically must be repaid pro-rata if the mortgage is broken
early. Read the terms carefully.

76. Can I refinance to access equity for a vacation?
Yes, the funds can be used for any purpose. Most brokers would
counsel against using long-amortizing mortgage debt to fund
short-term consumption, but the choice is the borrower's.

77. Can I refinance to access equity to buy out a business partner?
Yes. Pulling equity from a personal residence to buy out a business
interest is a common use case. The tax treatment of the borrowed
funds may matter for deductibility; consult a tax professional.

78. How does refinancing affect my chance of getting other credit?
A refinance shows up on the credit bureau and adjusts your total
debt and credit utilization profile. The impact on other credit
decisions depends on whether the refinance lowered or raised your
overall debt load.

79. What if my appraisal comes in higher than I expected?
You may be able to refinance for a larger amount than originally
planned, up to the 80% LTV cap on the higher value. The broker can
restructure the refinance amount accordingly.

80. Can I refinance to remove a co-signer?
Yes, but the remaining borrower (or borrowers) must qualify on
their own for the full new mortgage balance. If income or credit
isn't sufficient, the co-signer cannot be removed through a
refinance alone.

81. Can I refinance and add a new co-signer at the same time?
Yes. The new application includes the new co-signer's income and
credit, which may improve qualification or unlock a better rate.

82. How do I shop for the best refinance rate?
A mortgage broker shops multiple lenders for you simultaneously,
which is the fastest way to compare. You can also approach your
existing bank directly, but their offer should be compared against
broker-shopped rates.

83. Is there a difference between refinancing and getting a second mortgage?
Yes. A refinance replaces the existing mortgage entirely with a new
one. A second mortgage adds new debt registered behind the existing
first mortgage, leaving the original intact. Each has different
costs and rate implications.

84. Can I keep my existing rate when I refinance with the same lender?
Generally no. A refinance is a new loan with new market-rate pricing,
unless the lender specifically offers a blend-and-extend that mixes
your existing rate with the current rate.

85. What is a "porting" mortgage?
Porting means transferring your existing mortgage (with its rate and
terms) to a new property when you move. Most A-lender mortgages
have porting features; not all do.

86. Can I port and increase my mortgage at the same time?
Yes, this is called "port and increase." You keep your existing rate
on the original portion and add a new amount at current market rate.
The two are blended into one new payment.

87. Does my mortgage have to be in good standing to refinance?
Yes, generally. Most lenders require your current mortgage to be
current and free of recent late payments. If you're in arrears,
options narrow to specialized alternative or private lenders.

88. Can I refinance while my mortgage is in arrears?
It is much harder but sometimes possible through alternative or
private lenders, especially if there's enough equity to clear the
arrears at closing. Urgent cases like power of sale should reach a
broker immediately.

89. What is a stress-test exempt switch?
A straight switch between federally regulated lenders, with no
increase in loan amount and no increase in amortization, is exempt
from the OSFI stress test. The new lender qualifies you at the
contract rate, not the qualifying rate.

90. What happens if I get pre-approved for a refinance but don't proceed?
No financial obligation. The pre-approval expires (usually 90-120
days) and you can revisit later. The credit pull stays on your
report.

91. Can I refinance just to take advantage of a lower rate?
Yes, this is called a "rate refinance." If the savings from the lower
rate exceed the cost of the breakage penalty plus closing costs over
your remaining term, the refinance makes economic sense. A broker
runs that math for you.

92. How much rate drop justifies a refinance for rate alone?
Rule of thumb: at least 0.5% to 1% improvement over your current
rate, depending on remaining term length and outstanding balance.
The actual break-even is unique to each file.

93. Can I refinance multiple times in a year?
Technically yes, but the costs accumulate. Each refinance triggers
its own closing costs, possible penalties, and appraisals. Most
borrowers refinance no more than once or twice in a 5-year period.

94. Can I roll closing costs into the refinance?
The lender fee and appraisal may sometimes be deducted from the
advance. Legal fees and land transfer charges typically must be paid
separately at closing.

95. Are there refinance options for borrowers with very recent late payments?
Yes, through B-lenders or private. The recency and frequency of late
payments affects lender choice and pricing. A broker can route the
file appropriately.

96. Can I refinance to get a longer term than my current mortgage?
Yes. You can pick any term offered by the new lender (typically 1, 2,
3, 4, 5, 7, or 10 years). Longer terms typically have slightly
higher rates than shorter terms.

97. What is "blanket mortgage" in the refinance context?
A single mortgage secured against multiple properties you own.
Sometimes used in refinances when consolidating financing across a
portfolio. Less common for residential borrowers.

98. Can I refinance to switch from a closed to an open mortgage?
Yes, though open mortgages typically carry higher rates. The trade-
off is flexibility to pay the mortgage off in full anytime without
penalty.

99. What happens to my HELOC when I refinance my first mortgage?
If your HELOC is with the same lender (often part of a readvanceable
plan), the refinance may restructure both together. If it's a
separate product with a different lender, the HELOC stays in place
and the new first mortgage is registered ahead of it.

100. When is the best time to start exploring a refinance?
Three to six months before your current term matures is the most
common timing because there's no breakage penalty at maturity. If
you're refinancing for debt consolidation or to access cash, sooner
may be better if rates and savings justify the early breakage.

## Category 10: Additional borrower questions (merged from second source)

101. What's a simple way to estimate how much I can refinance?
A quick estimate: home value times 80%, then subtract your current
mortgage balance. The remainder is roughly the refinance room
available before fees and penalties. For example, an $800,000 home
with a $500,000 mortgage has about $140,000 of estimated refinance
room before costs.

102. Can I refinance to pay off a car loan?
Possibly. A refinance can roll a car loan into the mortgage if you
have enough equity. Be aware that rolling a short-term debt (e.g., a
3-5 year car loan) into a 25-30 year amortization lowers monthly
payments but typically increases the total interest paid over time.

103. Can I refinance to pay off a line of credit?
Yes, if you have enough equity. Consolidating an unsecured or
secured line of credit into a refinance is one of the most common
debt-consolidation use cases. The new mortgage payment replaces the
LOC payment plus the interest-rate savings.

104. Can I refinance while in active power of sale?
Possibly, but urgency and complexity are extreme. The new lender
must complete underwriting fast enough to pay out the existing
lender before the sale completes. Equity, timing, legal stage,
arrears amount, and lender willingness all matter. Files like this
should reach a broker immediately.

105. Can I refinance with stated income?
Some alternative B-lenders accept stated income or alternative
income documentation for refinances, supported by business bank
statements, contracts, or other verifiable cash flow evidence. The
file still needs to make sense in terms of affordability.

106. Can I refinance without proof of income?
Standard banks and most B-lenders require income documentation.
Some private lenders may review heavily equity-driven refinances
with very limited income documentation. This is usually more
expensive and should be reviewed carefully.

107. Can I refinance with a B-lender?
Yes. If you don't fit bank guidelines (credit, income, debt ratios)
but have at least 20% equity, a B-lender refinance is a common path.
Typical use cases: bruised credit, self-employed write-downs, high
debt ratios, recent credit events.

108. Can I refinance with a private lender?
Yes, when bank and B-lender options aren't available or when speed
matters most. Private refinances are more expensive and usually
shorter term. The exit plan to move back to A or B financing is
important before entering a private refinance.

109. What is an exit plan in a refinance context?
The strategy for moving from the current refinance solution into a
better or cheaper option later. Examples: improve credit during the
B-lender term, build verifiable income, reduce other debts, sell the
property, refinance again into A-lending after the situation
improves. Private refinances especially need a clear exit plan.

110. Can I refinance specifically to lower my monthly payment?
Possibly. Lowering the monthly payment can come from consolidating
debts, extending the amortization, changing the rate type, or
moving to a lower rate. Lower monthly payment isn't always lower
total cost; the broker should model both monthly payment and total
interest over time.

111. Will refinancing save me money?
Sometimes. Whether refinancing saves money depends on the new rate,
the breakage penalty, closing costs, amortization changes, and the
remaining term. A broker runs the break-even math to determine
whether the savings exceed the costs over your expected ownership
horizon.

112. What is the difference between a refinance and a switch?
A switch transfers the mortgage to a new lender at the same balance
without increasing the loan amount or changing the amortization.
Switches between FRFIs are exempt from the stress test if no money
is added. A refinance changes the loan amount, structure,
amortization, or adds money, and requires full requalification.

113. Can I refinance to add a HELOC to my mortgage?
Possibly. Some lender products combine a mortgage with a HELOC
component in a single readvanceable plan. Subject to lender rules
and the LTV caps (overall 80% LTV; non-amortizing HELOC component
capped at 65% LTV per OSFI's B-20).

114. Is a refinance better than a HELOC?
Depends on the borrower's goal. A HELOC is best for flexible,
on-demand access to funds (you only pay interest on what you draw).
A refinance is best for lump-sum borrowing, debt consolidation, or
restructuring the entire mortgage. Many borrowers use both together
in a readvanceable plan.

115. Can I refinance to help a family member buy a home?
Possibly. You can pull equity from your home to gift or lend funds
to a family member. The refinanced amount becomes your mortgage
debt and must be affordable on your income. The gift recipient
treats the funds as a non-repayable gift if structured that way.

116. Can I refinance if I have outstanding collections?
Possibly. Collections affect lender choice and pricing. A bank will
typically reject the file. Alternative or private lenders may
approve the refinance on the condition that collections are paid out
in full from the mortgage proceeds at closing.

117. Can I refinance if my income recently changed?
Possibly. The lender reviews the type of change: a raise or new
job is positive; job loss, reduced hours, probationary employment,
contract work, or maternity leave each require different
documentation. Self-employed transitions usually require new
qualifying logic.

118. Can I refinance to buy out my spouse?
Yes. A spousal buyout refinance is used to remove one spouse from
the mortgage and title, with the remaining spouse refinancing to
cover their share. The remaining spouse must qualify on their own
for the full balance. A separation agreement and legal work are
typically required.

119. What information should I provide to a broker for a refinance review?
Useful information: estimated current property value, current
mortgage balance and lender, current rate and renewal date, desired
new loan amount or cash needed, income, employment type, credit
score range, current debts, and the reason for refinancing. This
helps the broker estimate whether a refinance is likely worth
pursuing.

120. Can a mortgage broker tell me if refinancing actually makes sense?
Yes. A broker compares your current mortgage, the prepayment
penalty, available equity, income, debts, credit, lender options,
and your refinance goal. The broker can model whether refinancing
saves money or whether another option (HELOC, second mortgage,
waiting for renewal) is better.
