# Bad Credit Mortgages: Frequently Asked Questions

Plain-language Q&A reference for the kinds of questions borrowers ask
about getting a mortgage with bruised, damaged, or limited credit
history. Synthesized from two AI-generated sources (Gemini and
ChatGPT), deduplicated semantically, plus 50 additional borrower-
facing questions added. Significant overlap with `alt-lending-faqs.md`
and `private-lending-faqs.md` is intentional; this file pulls the
bad-credit angle together so a borrower asking about credit issues
has a focused reference.

## How the agent should use this file

These Q&As give the agent background context for bad-credit
conversations. Typical thresholds noted (credit score 600+ for CMHC
baseline, 650-680 A-lender preferred, 550+ for B-lender, 6-year
purge of negative items, 30% utilization rule, 90-day clean credit
rule for B-lenders) are general market context, not absolute rules.
Real lender policies vary.

The agent should NOT promise approval based on credit alone. The
appropriate response to a bad-credit visitor is: acknowledge there
are options across the lender tiers, explain what each tier
typically requires, and route to the broker for the specific file
review.

## Category 1: What Bad Credit Means

1. Can I get a mortgage in Ontario with bad credit?
Yes. While a low credit score blocks you from standard major banks,
Ontario has a deep secondary mortgage market: institutional
alternative B-lenders and equity-driven private lenders that
specialize in low-credit profiles.

2. What is considered "bad credit" by Canadian mortgage lenders?
Credit scores range from 300 to 900. Generally: below 600 is poor
or subprime; 600-650 is fair but flawed; 650-680 is acceptable for
some prime products; 680+ unlocks the best rates.

3. What counts as bad credit beyond just the score?
Missed payments, collections, maxed-out credit cards, consumer
proposal, bankruptcy, mortgage arrears, unpaid debts, recent
defaults, multiple recent credit inquiries. Lenders review the
full credit history, not just the score.

4. Do mortgage lenders only look at my credit score?
No. They also review income, employment, down payment, property
value, debt levels, mortgage payment history, and overall risk.
Strong factors in other areas can offset weaker credit.

5. Why do lenders care so much about credit?
Your credit report is the historical track record of how you manage
debt. Lenders use it to calculate the probability of default,
analyzing whether you pay on time, carry maxed limits, or have
skipped past obligations.

6. How are the three lender tiers different for bad credit?
A-lenders (banks): need 650-680+, strict income docs, prime rates.
B-lenders: accept 550-650, more flexible income, slightly higher
rates plus a 1% lender fee. Private lenders: ignore credit largely,
focus on equity and exit, much higher rates (10-15%) plus 3-5%
setup fees.

7. How much higher are bad-credit mortgage rates?
B-lender rates for fair credit run roughly 1-2% above prime bank
rates. Private mortgage rates for severely broken credit run
roughly 4-8% above prime, reflecting the higher risk.

8. Will bad credit always lead to a higher rate?
Almost always yes. Lenders price risk into rates. Better credit
means lower rates; weaker credit means higher rates plus often
upfront fees.

9. Can I get a mortgage with no credit history?
Possibly. No credit history is different from bad credit. Some
lenders may consider alternative credit (rent payments, utilities,
phone bills, bank account activity), a larger down payment, or a
co-signer.

10. Are newcomers to Canada treated like bad-credit borrowers?
No. Newcomers have a "thin credit file," not bad credit. Lenders
handle them through specialized Newcomer to Canada programs that
substitute international credit history, employment letters, and
rental payment history. See `cmhc-newcomers-program.md`.

## Category 2: Lender Tiers and Routing by Credit Score

11. What is the minimum credit score to buy a house with a major bank?
Traditional A-lenders prefer 650-680. For insured (sub-20% down)
mortgages, CMHC's baseline is 600, though individual banks may
impose tougher internal limits.

12. What credit score do I need for a B-lender?
B-lenders typically accept down to 550-600. Below 550, files
usually move toward private lending.

13. What if my credit is under 500?
Under 500, A-lenders and most B-lenders won't qualify you. Private
lenders may approve based on equity (20-25% minimum down) and asset
strength.

14. Can I buy a home with bad credit?
Possibly, but you may need a larger down payment, stronger income,
lower debts, a co-signer, or an alternative lender. The chat agent
should collect credit score range, missed payment history, debt
amounts, income, and down payment.

15. Can I refinance with bad credit?
Possibly. If there's enough equity, a B-lender or private lender
may review the file. Equity is the key factor when credit is weak.

16. Can I renew my mortgage with bad credit?
Possibly. Your current lender may offer a renewal even with weaker
credit, because they're often more lenient at renewal than they
would be on a new application.

17. Can I switch lenders with bad credit?
Possibly, but harder than renewing with your current lender. The
new lender reviews the full file.

18. How does the broker decide which tier to route a bad-credit file to?
The broker reviews credit score, missed payment recency, debts,
income, down payment, equity, property type, and timeline. Goal: the
lowest-cost suitable lender the borrower can reasonably qualify
for.

19. Will utilizing a B-lender hurt my credit?
No. Making consistent on-time payments on a B-lender mortgage is
one of the best ways to rebuild credit history.

20. What is the goal of a bad-credit mortgage?
Almost always a "credit rehabilitation strategy." Use the 1-2 year
B-lender or private term to consolidate debt, pay collections,
discharge proposals, and execute flawless payment habits, then
transition to a prime bank at maturity.

## Category 3: Bankruptcy, Consumer Proposals, and Tax Debt

21. Can I get a mortgage while in an active consumer proposal?
Yes, through alternative or private lenders, on the condition that
enough equity is extracted from the new mortgage to pay off and
fully discharge the consumer proposal at closing.

22. How long after a discharged consumer proposal can I get a bank mortgage?
Most A-lenders require 2 years discharged with two new re-
established lines of credit operating with perfect payment
histories.

23. Can I get a mortgage right after bankruptcy discharge?
Yes through private equity lenders who don't enforce post-bankruptcy
waiting periods, provided you have a 20-25% down payment in cash.

24. How long after a discharged bankruptcy can I get a bank mortgage?
A-lenders typically require 2 years discharged with rebuilt credit
(two new tradelines, perfect payment history). Some lenders require
more.

25. Can I get a mortgage during bankruptcy?
Very difficult. Most lenders require discharge before considering
the application.

26. What is an "Orderly Payment of Debts" (OPD)?
A debt management plan administered through a credit counselling
agency. Lenders treat it identically to a consumer proposal: you can
secure specialized financing, but prime banks reject the file until
the program completes.

27. Can I get a mortgage if I owe CRA back taxes?
Standard banks reject the file instantly. Alternative and private
lenders approve on the condition that CRA tax arrears are paid in
full from the mortgage advance at closing.

28. Can I refinance to pay off CRA debt?
Yes, if there's enough equity. This is one of the most common uses
of B-lender and private refinancing.

29. What if CRA has registered a lien on my property?
The lien must typically be cleared at closing (paid out, postponed,
or otherwise discharged) before the new mortgage can register. More
complex but manageable with the right lender.

30. Will an active cell phone collection or utility default stop my mortgage?
For prime: yes. For alt/private: it won't block approval, but
alternative lenders require collections to be paid in full out of
mortgage funds at closing, with legal receipts compiled by your
lawyer.

## Category 4: Missed Payments and Arrears

31. Can I get a mortgage after missed payments?
Possibly. The lender reviews how many payments were missed, how
recent, whether they're now caught up, and whether the issue is
likely to repeat.

32. Can I get a mortgage after missed mortgage payments specifically?
Missed mortgage payments are more serious than other missed
payments. Lenders look at the number, timing, current status, and
whether the mortgage is in arrears.

33. Can I get a mortgage if I'm in mortgage arrears?
Possibly. Mortgage arrears create urgency and complexity, especially
if the lender has started legal action.

34. Can I refinance to pay mortgage arrears?
Possibly. If there's enough equity, a refinance or private mortgage
may pay arrears and bring the mortgage current. Time is critical
if legal action has started.

35. Can I get a bad-credit mortgage to stop a Power of Sale?
Yes. If you have at least 20-25% remaining equity, a private equity
mortgage can fund quickly to pay off the foreclosing bank, halt
legal action, and preserve remaining equity.

36. How fast can a private mortgage stop a Power of Sale?
With appraisal in hand and clear title, as fast as 3-5 business
days. Urgent files should reach a broker immediately.

37. What if my Power of Sale has already progressed to a Notice of Sale?
Under the Ontario Mortgages Act, a Notice of Sale starts a
statutory notice period. There may still be time for a private
mortgage to fund, but every day matters. Get to a broker now.

38. Can missed payments be removed from my credit report?
Not unless they were errors. Legitimate missed payments stay on the
credit report for 6 years from the date of the original missed
payment.

39. What is the "90-day clean credit" rule for alternative lending?
Many B-lenders will overlook historical credit flaws or an old
bankruptcy on the condition that your credit report shows zero new
late payments or collections for the immediate past 90 days.

40. Will one missed payment kill my mortgage approval?
Often no, especially if it was isolated and addressed quickly.
Multiple missed payments or recent missed payments are much more
damaging than a single old missed payment.

## Category 5: Down Payment and Equity Rules

41. Can I buy a home with 5% down if I have bad credit?
Only if your score is 600+ and you pass CMHC's default insurance
stress test. Below 600, you're ineligible for high-ratio insurance,
meaning a minimum 20% down payment.

42. Why does 20% down make bad credit easier to approve?
20% down means LTV of 80% or less, which eliminates the need for
default insurance. This opens the door to alternative and private
lenders who operate under their own internal rules instead of
insurer rules.

43. Can I use a borrowed down payment with bad credit?
No. Under current guidelines, borrowed down payments are prohibited
for bad-credit files. Funds must come from your own savings,
investments, or a non-repayable family gift.

44. What is the maximum LTV an alternative lender will accept with bad credit?
B-lenders cap around 80% LTV for fair/bruised credit. Private
lenders cap around 75-80% LTV in urban properties, dropping to
50-60% in rural areas where resale is harder.

45. Can I use a co-signer with perfect credit to bypass my bad credit?
A strong co-signer helps with income qualification, but it doesn't
erase your bad credit. Most institutional lenders still base
pricing on the lowest credit score on the application.

46. Will gifted down payment help if my credit is weak?
Yes. Gifted down payment from a family member shows the lender
you're not borrowing the down payment, which strengthens the file.

47. Can I get a mortgage with no down payment and bad credit?
Very difficult. Most lenders require a down payment, and bad credit
makes no-down-payment options nearly impossible.

48. What's the minimum down payment for a private mortgage with bad credit?
Typically 20-25% minimum (depending on property location). Rural
properties need more equity (40-50% sometimes) than urban.

49. Does down payment source matter as much for bad credit?
Yes, more than for prime credit. Lenders trace the down payment
source carefully and require it to come from documented savings or
gift, not borrowed.

50. How much equity do I need to refinance with bad credit?
At least 20% remaining after the new mortgage (so an 80% LTV cap
applies). Some private lenders may go to 75% LTV with high enough
equity cushion.

## Category 6: Rates, Fees, and Total Cost

51. What rates can I expect on a bad-credit mortgage?
B-lender rates: typically 5.5-8% (varies with file strength and
market). Private rates: typically 8-15% depending on first vs
second position and risk profile. All higher than prime rates.

52. Are bad-credit mortgage rates negotiable?
Sometimes, slightly. Stronger files (lower LTV, urban property,
better income) have some negotiation room. Weaker files have less
flexibility because the lender is taking more risk.

53. What setup fees apply to bad-credit mortgages?
B-lender: typically 1% lender fee, sometimes a broker fee 0.5-1%.
Private: typically 2-3% combined lender + broker fees. Both
deducted from the loan advance at closing.

54. Do bad-credit lenders require an appraisal?
Yes, without exception. Because credit history can't act as
security, the physical asset is the lender's only safety net.
Budget $400-$600 for the appraisal.

55. Can I roll bad-credit setup fees into the loan?
You can't increase the loan beyond the approved LTV cap, but the
setup fees are deducted from the net cash advance at closing, so
you don't need to bring extra cash to closing.

56. Why are legal fees higher for bad-credit mortgages?
Bad-credit contracts include more conditions, debt payout
requirements, and complex execution. Lawyers spend more time on
each file. Expect $2,000-$4,000 for combined legal on a bad-credit
file.

## Category 7: Income, Documents, Qualification

57. Do I still pass a stress test with a B-lender?
Yes. B-lenders use standard qualification math but with larger debt
ratio allowances: GDS up to 42% and TDS up to 50% (vs prime 39/44).

58. Can I qualify for a bad-credit mortgage if I'm self-employed?
Yes, very common. Alternative lenders pair the lower credit score
with stated income programs that evaluate cash flow via 6-12 months
of business bank statements rather than tax returns.

59. Can I use non-traditional income like child tax benefits or disability?
Yes, B-lenders accept non-traditional streams (child tax benefits,
permanent disability payments, corporate pensions) provided you
prove continuous, predictable deposits.

60. Does owing on credit cards mean I have bad credit?
Not automatically. If your card balances exceed 30% of total limits,
utilization ratio drops your score. High card payments also inflate
TDS, shrinking your mortgage approval size. Pay them down before
applying.

61. Can I qualify with cash income (tips, side gigs)?
Only if the cash income can be documented through consistent
deposits in bank statements over 6-12 months. Unreported cash
income is very difficult to use.

62. Can I qualify with child support or alimony income?
Yes, B-lenders accept it with a legally signed separation agreement
and consistent recurring deposits over 6+ months.

63. What documents do I need for a bad-credit mortgage?
Common: government ID, recent pay stubs or tax returns, business
bank statements (if self-employed), credit report, debt statements,
property tax bill, mortgage statement (if refinancing), collection
or proposal documents (where applicable), purchase agreement (if
buying).

## Category 8: Renewals, Switches, and Strategy

64. What is the typical term for a bad-credit mortgage?
1 or 2 year fixed terms, almost universally. Short terms provide a
window to fix credit without trapping you in high rates long-term.

65. Can I renew my B-lender mortgage if my credit hasn't improved?
Yes, usually. If you've paid on time, most B-lenders offer renewal
at term end, though with an administrative renewal fee (~1%).

66. Can I break my bad-credit mortgage early if my credit improves?
Yes, with a prepayment penalty (usually 3 months interest). If the
savings from moving to a prime A-lender exceed the penalty plus
costs, it makes sense.

67. How do I graduate from a B-lender back to a major bank?
Maintain perfect mortgage payments throughout the 1-2 year B-lender
term, keep credit utilization below 30%, file clean tax returns
showing strong income, avoid new credit damage, and have your
broker prepare the A-lender application 60-90 days before
maturity.

68. What if my credit hasn't improved by the end of the B-lender term?
You renew with the B-lender (likely with the renewal fee), or
escalate to private if even the B-lender won't continue. The
strategy needs to be re-evaluated with the broker.

69. Can I refinance to consolidate debt with bad credit?
Yes, through B-lenders or private lenders, if there's enough equity.
Consolidating high-interest debt into a mortgage can free monthly
cash flow and accelerate credit recovery.

## Category 9: Credit Repair and Rebuilding

70. What is the fastest way to raise my credit score?
Reduce revolving credit card utilization. Pay balances below 30% of
each card's limit. Never miss a payment deadline. These two actions
move the score fastest.

71. What is a secured credit card and should I get one?
A secured card requires a cash deposit matching your limit and
reports as a regular tradeline to Equifax and TransUnion. After a
consumer proposal or bankruptcy discharge, a secured card is one of
the best tools to rebuild credit.

72. How many active credit lines do I need to prove credit recovery?
A-lenders typically want at least two active tradelines (e.g., one
credit card and one car loan or personal loan) running smoothly for
24 consecutive months.

73. Will checking my own credit score hurt my file?
No. Self-checks via consumer platforms (Borrowell, Credit Karma,
your bank app) are soft inquiries and have zero impact. Hard
inquiries only happen when an external lender pulls your file for an
active application.

74. How long do negative items stay on my credit report?
Most negative items, collections, and judgments are automatically
purged from Equifax and TransUnion 6 years from the date of the
original default or filing.

75. What if my credit report has errors?
Up to 20% of credit files contain errors. Review your Equifax and
TransUnion reports, file disputes for incorrect items, and resolve
them before applying. Corrections can lift the score significantly.

76. Should I close old credit accounts before applying?
Generally no. Closing old accounts can reduce your average account
age and total available credit, both of which can hurt your score.
Keep old accounts open and in good standing.

77. Should I pay off all my debts before applying?
Sometimes yes, sometimes no. The right move depends on whether the
debt is high-interest, what the impact on your debt ratios is, and
whether paying it off depletes the down payment you need.

## Category 10: Co-signers and Spouse

78. Can my spouse with good credit help if I have bad credit?
Yes, your spouse's income and credit can strengthen the joint
application. Both names on the application means both incomes count.

79. Can a co-signer help my bad-credit application?
Yes. A strong co-signer (good credit, stable income, low debts) can
significantly improve approval odds. Both parties are jointly
responsible for the mortgage.

80. Can I remove a bad-credit borrower from an existing mortgage?
Possibly. The remaining borrower(s) must qualify on their own.
Treated as a refinance and requires legal/title changes.

81. Can my parents co-sign my bad-credit mortgage?
Yes. A co-signing parent with strong credit and income often makes
an otherwise marginal file approvable.

82. What happens if my co-signer wants to be removed later?
You refinance the mortgage in your name only (must qualify alone),
typically at renewal when there's no prepayment penalty.

## Category 11: Specific Scenarios

83. Can a bad-credit borrower use a private mortgage to flip a property?
Yes. Real estate investors with credit issues but good deal
recognition often use private capital for short-term flip
financing because banks don't fund flips and B-lenders are
restrictive.

84. Can I buy an investment property with bad credit?
Possibly. Alternative B-lenders offer rental options but typically
require 25-30% down, with the property's rental income covering its
own operational costs.

85. Can I get a bad-credit mortgage on a unique property (hobby farm, log cabin)?
Subprime lenders heavily prefer standard, highly marketable
properties in urban/suburban zones. Unique properties with bad
credit are typically routed to private lending.

86. Can a newcomer to Canada with no Canadian credit get a mortgage?
Newcomers have a thin credit file, not bad credit. Specialized
Newcomer to Canada programs substitute international credit reports,
rental payment history, and employment letters. See
`cmhc-newcomers-program.md`.

87. Can I use a rent-to-own program as a bridge to a future mortgage?
Yes. If you can't produce 20% down and your score is under 600, a
rent-to-own structure lets you lease the property while a portion
of your rent accumulates toward future down payment as your credit
heals.

88. How does a payday loan history affect my mortgage application?
Payday loans on bank statements flag severe cash flow distress.
Alternative underwriters routinely require all active payday/
short-term advance accounts to be closed and paid off as a
condition of approval.

89. Will a past consumer proposal mean I can never access prime bank rates again?
Not at all. A consumer proposal is a temporary credit pause. Once
discharged and paired with 2 years of clean re-established credit,
you can graduate to prime tier rates.

90. Can I get a HELOC with bad credit?
Difficult. Most HELOC lenders require strong credit. Some B-lenders
offer HELOC-like bundled products, but pure revolving HELOCs are
hard to access with bruised credit.

## Category 12: Additional borrower questions

91. Can I get pre-approved with bad credit?
Yes, with B-lenders and some specialty lenders. The pre-approval
process shows you what's available before you start shopping.

92. Will multiple mortgage applications hurt my credit further?
Multiple credit inquiries in a short window (typically 14-45 days)
are treated as a single inquiry by credit scoring models for
mortgage shopping. Outside that window, multiple separate inquiries
can drag the score down.

93. Should I apply with multiple lenders at the same time?
Through a broker, yes. The broker uses one credit pull across
multiple lender submissions. Don't apply directly to multiple banks
yourself; that triggers multiple hard pulls.

94. What if my bank rejected my mortgage because of credit?
Don't apply to other banks immediately. Connect with a licensed
broker who can run a single credit pull and match your file to
specialized subprime underwriters.

95. Will a B-lender or private lender check my credit?
Yes, both check. The difference is what threshold they require and
how they use the report. Private lenders look mostly for active
bankruptcies, judgments, or liens that affect the property; the
score itself matters less to them.

96. Can a bad-credit mortgage be insured?
No. Default insurance requires 600+ credit (CMHC baseline). Below
600, the mortgage is conventional only, requiring 20%+ down.

97. What is a "Credit Repair Clause"?
A condition in a B-lender or private mortgage commitment requiring
the borrower to execute specific credit improvements during the
term (such as paying off collections or maintaining a credit
monitoring service).

98. Are credit repair companies worth using?
Mixed. Legitimate credit counselling agencies can help. Be wary of
companies promising to "remove" legitimate negative items;
legitimate items can't be removed, only disputed if they're
errors.

99. Can rent-to-own programs damage my credit further?
If the program is structured improperly or you default on rent
payments. A well-structured legitimate rent-to-own program can help
build the down payment and history needed for a future mortgage.

100. Will paying my mortgage on time rebuild my credit?
Yes, significantly. On-time mortgage payments are heavily weighted
in credit scoring algorithms. A clean 1-2 year B-lender mortgage
history can lift your score substantially.

101. What information do I need to give a broker for a bad-credit review?
Credit score range, missed payment history, current debts (cards,
loans, lines, etc.), income, employment type, down payment or
equity available, property value, current mortgage balance (if
refinancing), and timeline. Be honest; the broker needs the truth
to help.

102. Can a mortgage broker help me even if I have very bad credit?
Yes. A licensed broker has access to B-lenders, credit unions, and
private lenders that don't deal directly with the public. The
worse the credit, the more value the broker provides by matching
the file to the right lender.

103. What's the single most important factor for a bad-credit mortgage?
Equity (for refinances) or down payment (for purchases). The more
skin the borrower has in the file, the more options are available.
Bad credit + minimal equity is very hard; bad credit + 25%+
equity has real options.

## What this corpus file is not

- It is not a substitute for `alt-lending-faqs.md` or
  `private-lending-faqs.md`; this file pulls the bad-credit angle
  together while those cover the lender mechanics in detail.
- It is not legal, tax, or financial advice for any specific
  borrower's situation.
- It is not a guarantee of approval based on credit score alone.
  Every file is reviewed on its full merits.
- The typical thresholds noted (credit score ranges, B-lender DSR
  ceilings, 6-year purge of negative items, lender fee ranges) are
  market context as of the authoring date, not absolute rules.
- It is not a credit-repair guide; it points to the strategies
  brokers commonly recommend but specific credit-repair work is
  outside the prequalifier's scope.
