# First-Time Home Buying: Frequently Asked Questions

Plain-language Q&A reference for the kinds of questions first-time
homebuyers actually ask. Synthesized from two AI-generated sources
(ChatGPT and Gemini), deduplicated semantically, plus 50 additional
borrower-facing questions added. The framework explanation of CMHC
homeownership programs (Purchase, Home Start, Newcomers, etc.) lives
in `jurisdictions/CA/cmhc-mortgage-loan-insurance.md` and related
files. This file is the borrower-facing Q&A complement.

## How the agent should use this file

These Q&As give the agent background context for first-time-buyer
conversations. Many of the rules referenced (5%/10% down payment
tiers, 30-year amortization rules for first-time buyers, FHSA limits,
HBP withdrawal cap, Ontario land transfer tax rebate) align with the
jurisdictional corpus files; cross-reference where deeper detail is
needed.

The agent should still defer to a licensed broker for specifics on
any individual file. First-time buyers with an accepted offer or
financing condition deadline are time-sensitive and should reach the
broker quickly.

## Category 1: Who Counts as a First-Time Buyer

1. What is a first-time homebuyer?
Generally, a first-time homebuyer is someone who has never owned a
home before. Some programs (like the Home Buyers' Plan, FHSA, and
Ontario LTT refund) have specific definitions that may treat you as
a first-time buyer even if you owned a home long ago, depending on
the time elapsed. A broker can confirm which definition applies to
which program for your situation.

2. Can I be a first-time buyer if my spouse has owned a home before?
For most Canadian programs, if your spouse has owned a qualifying
home during your marriage or common-law relationship, you typically
do not qualify as a first-time buyer. Programs treat the household,
not the individual, in many cases. Read the specific program rules.

3. Can newcomers to Canada be first-time buyers?
Yes. Newcomers are commonly first-time buyers because they're buying
in Canada for the first time. Some lenders have specialized newcomer
programs, and CMHC has a Newcomers program that accommodates
non-permanent residents with work permits.

## Category 2: Down Payment

4. How much down payment do I need for my first home?
In Canada: minimum 5% on homes priced at $500,000 or less. For homes
between $500,001 and $1,499,999: 5% on the first $500,000 plus 10%
on the amount above $500,000. Homes priced at $1.5 million or more
require at least 20% down.

5. Can I buy a home with 5% down in Ontario?
Yes, if the home is $500,000 or less and the file qualifies under
insured mortgage rules. Above $500,000, the tiered down payment
applies (5% on first $500K, 10% above).

6. Can I buy with less than 20% down?
Yes. Buyers with less than 20% down need a high-ratio insured
mortgage, which requires mortgage default insurance (CMHC, Sagen, or
Canada Guaranty). The insurance protects the lender; the borrower
pays the premium.

7. What is mortgage default insurance and why do I need it?
Mortgage default insurance protects the lender if a borrower defaults.
It allows borrowers with less than 20% down to qualify for a
mortgage. The premium is typically added to the mortgage and
amortized over the term.

8. Is CMHC the only mortgage insurer?
No. Canada has three: CMHC (Crown corporation), Sagen, and Canada
Guaranty. They operate on a parallel framework with similar rules.
Your lender chooses which insurer to use based on file fit and
lender relationships.

9. Do I need mortgage insurance with 20% down?
Generally no. With 20%+ down, the mortgage is conventional (low-ratio)
and doesn't require default insurance by law. The lender may still
have its own requirements based on property type, credit, and risk.

10. Can I use a gifted down payment from my parents?
Yes, most lenders accept non-repayable gifts from immediate family
members. The lender requires a signed gift letter confirming the
funds are non-repayable, plus proof that the funds were transferred
into your account.

11. Can I borrow my down payment?
Sometimes. Borrowed down payment is allowed in limited cases (CMHC's
non-traditional down payment program at 90.01-95% LTV on 1-2 unit
homes with strong credit) but generally lenders prefer down payment
from your own savings. Borrowed funds count toward your debt ratios.

12. Can I use my FHSA for my down payment?
Yes. The First Home Savings Account (FHSA) is a registered account
specifically designed for first-time homebuyers to save tax-free
toward a qualifying home. The first-year contribution room is
$8,000. Contributions are tax-deductible, growth is tax-free, and
qualifying withdrawals for a home are tax-free.

13. Can I use the Home Buyers' Plan to withdraw from my RRSP?
Yes. The Home Buyers' Plan (HBP) lets you withdraw up to $60,000 from
your RRSP tax-free to buy or build a qualifying first home. The
withdrawal must be repaid to your RRSP over 15 years, starting in
the second year after withdrawal.

14. Can I use both the FHSA and the Home Buyers' Plan together?
Yes, in most cases. Eligible buyers can combine both, subject to each
program's rules. A broker can confirm the down payment combination;
a tax professional can confirm the tax implications.

15. Can I use my TFSA for my down payment?
Yes. TFSA funds can be withdrawn tax-free at any time for any
purpose, including a home down payment. There's no contribution
repayment requirement like the HBP.

16. Should I use my TFSA or my RRSP for the down payment?
Depends on tax situation, retirement plans, and contribution room.
RRSP via HBP must be repaid; TFSA does not. FHSA is the most
tax-efficient route for first-time buyers if you've contributed
there. A financial planner or accountant can model the best option.

## Category 3: Income and Affordability

17. How much income do I need to buy my first home?
It depends on the purchase price, down payment, debts, property
taxes, condo fees, heating costs, interest rate, and amortization.
There's no universal income number. A mortgage broker calculates an
estimated affordability range after reviewing your full file.

18. What credit score do I need as a first-time buyer?
No single number applies to every lender. CMHC requires at least one
borrower or guarantor to have a 600 minimum credit score. A-lenders
typically prefer 680+. Higher scores unlock better rates and broader
lender options.

19. Can I buy a home with bad credit?
Possibly. Lower credit may require a larger down payment, stronger
income, lower debts, a co-signer, or an alternative lender (B-side).
Below 550, files typically move toward private lending.

20. Can I buy a home with no credit history?
Possibly, but limited credit history makes approval harder. Some
lenders consider alternative credit history (rent payments,
utilities, phone bills). Newcomers can often use international
credit reports or letters of reference from foreign banks.

21. What is the mortgage stress test?
The stress test (Minimum Qualifying Rate) requires borrowers to
qualify at the GREATER of their contract rate plus 2% or the 5.25%
floor set by OSFI. This applies to both insured and uninsured
mortgages.

22. Does the stress test apply to first-time buyers?
Yes. Whether the mortgage is insured (high-ratio, sub-20% down) or
uninsured (conventional, 20%+ down), the stress test applies. The
Minister of Finance applied the same MQR formula to insured
mortgages.

23. What are debt ratios?
Debt ratios measure how much of your gross income goes to housing
and other debts. Lenders use them to confirm the mortgage is
affordable. Two key ratios: Gross Debt Service (GDS) and Total Debt
Service (TDS).

24. What is GDS?
Gross Debt Service ratio. Measures housing costs (mortgage payment,
property taxes, heat, 50% of condo fees) against gross income. CMHC
maximum is 39%.

25. What is TDS?
Total Debt Service ratio. GDS plus all other debt obligations (cars,
credit cards, student loans, lines of credit, child support). CMHC
maximum is 44%.

26. Do student loans affect my mortgage qualification?
Yes. Student loan payments are included in your debt ratios. Even
deferred student loans may have a qualifying payment applied by the
lender based on the outstanding balance.

27. Do car loans affect how much I can borrow?
Yes. Monthly car payments reduce mortgage affordability because
they count in TDS. A $500 car payment can reduce mortgage capacity
by tens of thousands of dollars.

28. Do credit card balances affect approval?
Yes. Lenders include credit card minimum payments (typically 3% of
outstanding balance for unsecured revolving credit) in TDS. High
credit card balances reduce mortgage capacity.

29. Should I pay off debt before buying?
Sometimes. The trade-off is whether using funds for debt repayment
or down payment creates the better mortgage outcome. A broker can
model both scenarios.

## Category 4: Pre-Approval and Documents

30. What is a mortgage pre-approval?
A preliminary lender or broker review of your income, credit, down
payment, and debts. Pre-approval provides an estimated budget and
often includes a rate hold. It is NOT a final approval; the property
and full documentation still need lender review.

31. Is a pre-approval guaranteed?
No. Final approval depends on the specific property, full
documentation review, appraisal, and lender conditions. Pre-approval
is a confidence indicator, not a binding offer.

32. Should I get pre-approved before house shopping?
Yes. Pre-approval gives you a realistic budget, helps your realtor
focus on appropriate properties, and demonstrates to sellers that
you're serious. It usually includes a rate hold for 90-120 days.

33. How long does a pre-approval last?
Most lenders hold the pre-approval rate for 90 to 120 days. After
expiry, you can request a renewed rate hold.

34. What documents do I need for a pre-approval?
Government ID, recent pay stubs, job letter, T4s and Notices of
Assessment for the last 2 years, bank statements showing down
payment, and statements for any existing debts. Self-employed buyers
add T1 Generals, business financials, and business bank statements.

35. What does the lender check before approving me?
Income (employment, stability, sources), credit (score, history,
utilization), debts (other monthly obligations), down payment
(amount and source), property (type, value, condition, location),
and overall affordability.

## Category 5: Closing Costs and Taxes

36. What are closing costs for a first-time buyer?
Beyond the down payment: land transfer tax, legal fees, title
insurance, appraisal fee (if not covered by lender), home inspection,
moving costs, property tax adjustments, utility adjustments, and
sometimes mortgage default insurance PST. CMHC's rough guidance: 1.5%
to 4% of purchase price.

37. What is land transfer tax in Ontario?
A provincial tax payable when title transfers. Calculated as a
percentage of the purchase price, in tiered brackets. Buyers in
Toronto also pay an additional municipal land transfer tax (MLTT).

38. Do first-time buyers get a land transfer tax rebate in Ontario?
Yes. Eligible first-time buyers receive a provincial LTT rebate of
up to $4,000 (effectively no provincial LTT on the first $368,000 of
purchase price). Toronto offers an additional municipal LTT rebate
for qualifying first-time buyers in Toronto.

39. What are the eligibility rules for the Ontario FTHB LTT rebate?
You must be 18 or older, must never have owned a home anywhere in
the world, your spouse must not have owned a home during the
marriage, must occupy the home as principal residence within 9
months, and must be a Canadian citizen or permanent resident
(18-month grace period after closing).

40. What is HST on a new home?
HST applies to new builds at 13% in Ontario (8% provincial + 5%
federal). Resale homes between individuals generally don't have HST.
First-time buyers and others may qualify for the HST New Housing
Rebate. The 2026 Ontario budget expanded the rebate significantly.

41. Can a first-time buyer get HST relief on a new home?
Yes. The 2025 Fall Statement introduced relief that removes the full
provincial portion of HST for first-time buyers on qualifying new
homes up to $1 million. The 2026 budget expanded this further. Talk
to your lawyer about the specifics for your purchase.

## Category 6: Property and Process

42. Can I buy a condo as my first home?
Yes. Condos are common first homes. The lender reviews the purchase
price, condo fees, building condition, status certificate, and your
income/debt profile. High condo fees can reduce mortgage capacity.

43. Do condo fees affect my mortgage qualification?
Yes. Lenders typically include 50% of condo fees in the GDS
calculation, which reduces the mortgage amount you can qualify for.

44. Can I buy a pre-construction condo as a first-time buyer?
Yes. Pre-construction has extra considerations: staged deposit
payments to the builder, mortgage approval timing closer to occupancy,
interim occupancy fees in some cases, and longer overall timelines.

45. Can I buy a new build with 5% down?
Possibly. New builds priced under $1.5M with sub-20% down qualify for
insured mortgages, and first-time buyers of new builds can access
30-year amortization through CMHC Home Start.

46. Can first-time buyers get a 30-year amortization?
Yes, under CMHC Home Start, if BOTH conditions are met: LTV greater
than 80% (insured high-ratio) AND the borrower is either a first-time
homebuyer OR purchasing a newly built home.

47. What is amortization?
The total time to repay the mortgage in full. Common amortizations:
25 years (standard for insured), 30 years (available for first-time
buyers, new builds, and conventional uninsured). Longer amortization
means lower monthly payment but more total interest over the life
of the mortgage.

48. What's the difference between fixed and variable rate?
A fixed rate stays the same for the entire term. A variable rate
moves with the lender's prime rate, so the rate (and sometimes the
payment) changes. Fixed offers predictability; variable historically
has saved money on average but with rate risk.

## Category 7: Offers, Closing, and Beyond

49. What happens after my offer is accepted?
The mortgage file moves to final approval. The lender reviews the
purchase agreement, property details, income docs, down payment
proof, credit, and any other conditions before issuing final
approval. The lawyer prepares closing paperwork.

50. Should I include a financing condition in my offer?
Usually yes for first-time buyers without firm financing. A
financing condition gives you a window (typically 5-10 days) to
confirm mortgage approval before the deal becomes firm. In
competitive markets, waiving the condition is sometimes considered
but is riskier.

## Category 8: Additional borrower questions

51. What is "subject to financing" in an offer?
A condition giving the buyer time to confirm mortgage financing
before the offer becomes binding. If financing falls through during
the condition period, the buyer can walk away with deposit
returned.

52. How long should my financing condition be?
Typical financing conditions run 5 to 10 business days. Shorter
makes your offer more competitive but riskier. Longer is safer but
weaker as an offer.

53. Do I need a real estate lawyer?
Yes. A real estate lawyer is required to handle title transfer,
mortgage registration, and disbursement of funds at closing. Choose
your own lawyer or ask your realtor or mortgage broker for
referrals.

54. When should I hire a real estate lawyer?
Ideally as soon as your offer is accepted, so the lawyer has the
full closing window to complete title searches, review documents,
and prepare paperwork.

55. Do I need a home inspection?
Strongly recommended for any resale home. A home inspector
identifies issues (roof, foundation, plumbing, electrical, HVAC) that
might cost significant money to repair. The cost is typically $400
to $600.

56. Who pays for the home inspection?
The buyer pays. The fee is paid directly to the inspector around the
time of the inspection, usually during the home inspection
condition period.

57. What is title insurance?
A one-time insurance policy protecting against title defects, survey
issues, fraud, and certain other risks. Required by lenders.
Typically $200-$400 in Ontario.

58. Who pays for title insurance?
The buyer, usually paid at closing as part of legal fees. Sometimes
included in the lawyer's quoted fee.

59. What is the difference between deposit and down payment?
The deposit is a smaller amount paid with the offer to demonstrate
commitment, typically held in the realtor's trust account. The down
payment is the total cash you bring at closing. The deposit counts
toward the down payment.

60. How much deposit do I need with my offer?
Typically 1% to 5% of the purchase price, depending on market
norms. In hot markets, larger deposits make offers more competitive.

61. Will I lose my deposit if I back out?
If you back out for a reason not covered by a condition in your
offer (and after conditions are removed), yes, you generally forfeit
the deposit. Back out during a valid condition period (financing,
inspection, etc.) and the deposit is returned.

62. Can I write an offer without a pre-approval?
Yes, but it's risky. Without pre-approval, you have no confidence
your financing will come through, and the seller has less
confidence in your offer. Pre-approval is strongly recommended.

63. What is a "bully offer"?
An offer submitted before the seller's stated offer review date,
typically with strong terms and a tight acceptance window, designed
to pressure the seller into accepting before competing offers
arrive.

64. What is a bidding war?
Multiple buyers competing for the same property with simultaneous
offers, typically on a set offer date. Buyers may offer above
asking, waive conditions, or strengthen terms to win.

65. Should I waive my home inspection condition in a bidding war?
Risky. Waiving the inspection means accepting the property without
the ability to discover unknown issues. Some buyers do a pre-offer
inspection before the offer date to compete; otherwise, waiving is
a financial gamble.

66. Can my pre-approved rate change before I close?
Most lenders hold the pre-approved rate for 90-120 days. If you
close within that window, you get the rate. If rates move down
during the hold, some lenders offer the lower rate; others don't.

67. What is a rate hold?
A commitment from the lender to honor a specific interest rate for a
set period (typically 90-120 days) if the borrower closes within
that window. Protects you from rate increases during house shopping.

68. Can I get a rate hold before I find a property?
Yes, through a pre-approval. The rate hold is part of the
pre-approval package.

69. What if rates drop before my closing?
Some lenders offer a "rate float-down" allowing one rate reduction
during the hold period if rates have dropped. Others lock you to
the original rate. Confirm with your broker.

70. Can I switch lenders after pre-approval?
Yes. Pre-approval doesn't bind you to a specific lender. If you find
better terms elsewhere, you can switch before final approval.

71. What if I find a better rate after I'm approved?
You can ask your lender to match. If they won't and the difference
is meaningful, you may switch lenders, though redoing paperwork
takes time. Discuss with your broker.

72. What is "closing date" vs "possession date"?
The closing date is when title legally transfers and the lawyer
finalizes the transaction. The possession date is when you get the
keys and can move in. They're usually the same day but not always.

73. Do I get the keys on closing day?
Usually yes, after the lawyer confirms the funds have transferred
and title has registered. Sometimes there are delays in the
afternoon if title registration takes longer than expected.

74. What documents do I sign at closing?
The mortgage commitment, mortgage agreement, transfer documents,
title insurance forms, statement of adjustments, and various lender
and lawyer documents. Your lawyer walks you through them.

75. Can I move in on closing day?
Yes, usually after the funds transfer and title registers (often
around mid-afternoon). Some buyers schedule movers for the day
after closing to avoid timing risk.

76. What is the lender's "advance"?
The act of sending the mortgage funds to your lawyer at closing.
"Advance date" or "funding date" is when the lender releases the
money. Usually the day before or the morning of closing.

77. When do my mortgage payments start?
Your first payment is typically due one month after the closing
date, depending on lender practice. Some lenders charge interest
adjustment from closing to the first regular payment date.

78. Can I choose my payment date?
Most lenders let you choose. Common choices: 1st of the month, 15th
of the month, or a date aligned with your paycheck.

79. Can I set up biweekly or weekly payments?
Yes. Most lenders offer monthly, semi-monthly, biweekly, accelerated
biweekly, weekly, and accelerated weekly options.

80. What is "accelerated biweekly"?
Accelerated biweekly payments are equivalent to one extra monthly
payment per year. You pay half of your monthly payment every two
weeks, which results in 26 payments per year (vs 24 with regular
biweekly). Reduces total interest and shortens amortization.

81. Does paying more often save me money?
Yes, slightly. Accelerated payment frequencies (biweekly or weekly)
pay down the principal faster, reducing total interest over the life
of the mortgage and shortening the amortization.

82. Can I make a lump-sum payment in the first year?
Yes, up to your annual prepayment privilege (typically 10-20% of the
original principal, depending on lender). The mortgage commitment
spells out the exact prepayment privileges.

83. What is the difference between principal and interest?
Principal is the amount you owe. Interest is the lender's cost of
lending. Each mortgage payment includes both: early in the
amortization, most of the payment goes to interest; later, more
goes to principal.

84. How does my mortgage payment change over time?
On a fixed-rate mortgage with regular payments, the total payment
stays the same, but the proportion going to principal increases
over time. On variable-rate or adjustable mortgages, the payment
itself may change with prime rate.

85. What's an "interest adjustment"?
A small amount of interest charged for the period between the
closing date and your first regular payment date. Usually paid at
closing or rolled into the first payment.

86. What is property tax adjustment at closing?
The lawyer balances property taxes already paid by the seller (or
owed by them) against what you'll owe going forward. You may
receive a credit or pay an additional amount at closing.

87. What is "porting" my mortgage?
Transferring your existing mortgage to a new property when you move,
keeping the same rate and terms. Most A-lender mortgages allow
porting; not all do. Verify with the broker before signing.

88. What if my closing is delayed?
Closings can be delayed for many reasons (paperwork, title issues,
fund timing). Your lawyer coordinates with the seller's lawyer and
the lender. If the delay is significant, additional interest or
penalty might apply.

89. What is a "status certificate" for a condo purchase?
A document from the condo corporation showing the building's
financial health, reserve fund status, recent meeting minutes, and
any special assessments or pending lawsuits. Your lawyer reviews it
during the conditional period.

90. What is a "reserve fund study" for a condo?
An engineer's analysis projecting the condo's future maintenance
costs and confirming whether the reserve fund is adequate. A weak
reserve fund may lead to special assessments down the road.

91. What if the condo has a special assessment?
A special assessment is an extra one-time fee charged to condo
owners for major repairs or expenses the reserve fund can't cover.
A pending special assessment may affect your buying decision or
mortgage approval.

92. Do lenders require a home inspection?
Lenders typically don't require it, but they may request an appraisal
that includes a basic property condition review. A home inspection
protects YOU as the buyer, not the lender.

93. Can I waive the appraisal condition?
The lender's appraisal is part of their approval, not yours. You
generally can't waive their requirement, though some pre-approval
products may not need a separate appraisal if the lender has good
data on the property.

94. What is "fair market value"?
The price a willing buyer and willing seller would agree to in an
arm's-length transaction. The appraisal determines fair market value
for the lender, which sets the maximum loan amount.

95. What if I pay more than appraised value?
The lender will only lend up to a percentage of the appraised value,
not the purchase price. If you pay above appraised value, you must
bring the difference in additional down payment.

96. Can I negotiate my mortgage rate?
Yes. The first rate offered by a lender is often not their best
rate. A mortgage broker negotiates on your behalf by shopping
multiple lenders. You can also ask your bank to match a broker
quote.

97. Do all banks offer the same rate?
No. Rates vary across lenders, and the same lender may offer
different rates to different brokers, channels, or customers. This
is why shopping pays off.

98. What is the Canadian Mortgage Charter?
A set of expectations the federal government published in 2023
outlining how lenders should treat mortgage borrowers in hardship,
including options for temporary extensions, lump-sum payments
without penalty in certain circumstances, and proactive outreach.

99. What is a "high-ratio" mortgage?
A mortgage with less than 20% down payment (LTV above 80%). These
must be insured. The opposite is a "low-ratio" or conventional
mortgage with 20%+ down.

100. What is "amortization vs term" again?
Amortization is the total time to repay the mortgage (e.g., 25 or
30 years). Term is the length of your current contract with the
lender (e.g., 5 years). At end of term, you renew or refinance.
The amortization clock continues across multiple terms.

## Category 9: Additional borrower questions (merged from second source)

101. What's the precise CRA-style rule for who qualifies as a first-time home buyer?
You qualify as a first-time home buyer if you have never owned a
home anywhere in the world, OR if you have not occupied a principal
residence owned by yourself or your current spouse or common-law
partner in the last 4 calendar years. Different programs may apply
slightly different versions of this rule (HBP and FHSA each have
their own specifics).

102. Can I qualify as a first-time buyer again after a divorce or separation?
Yes. Under CRA rules, a marriage or common-law partnership breakdown
allows you to re-qualify as a first-time buyer for the Home Buyers'
Plan even if you previously owned a home, subject to a 90-day
separation period requirement.

103. If my co-borrower has owned a home before but I haven't, are we eligible for FTHB incentives?
For federal programs like the FHSA and HBP, your individual
eligibility remains intact for your portion. For the Ontario Land
Transfer Tax rebate, the rebate amount is prorated based on your
percentage of ownership in the new home.

104. Is there an age restriction to be considered a first-time buyer?
You must be at least 18 years old to open a First Home Savings
Account (FHSA) or to legally sign a mortgage contract in Ontario.
There is no maximum age ceiling for first-time buyer programs.

105. Can my spouse and I combine our FHSAs?
Yes. If you and your spouse are buying together, each of you can
contribute to your own FHSA up to the $40,000 lifetime maximum. As
a couple, you can pool up to $80,000 in FHSA funds (plus any
investment growth), all withdrawable tax-free for the home purchase.

106. How long do I have to repay the Home Buyers' Plan?
You have a 15-year repayment period to return the funds to your
RRSP. Repayments generally begin in the second year following the
year of your withdrawal. Missed repayments are added back to your
taxable income for that year.

107. Can I use cryptocurrency or cash kept at home for my down payment?
No. Under Canadian Anti-Money Laundering (AML) regulations, all
down payment funds must sit in a regulated Canadian bank account
and be fully traceable for at least 90 days before purchase.
Cryptocurrency must be converted to Canadian dollars and seasoned
in a bank account. Cash kept at home cannot be used.

108. Approximately how much income do I need to buy a $600,000 home with 5% down?
Income required varies based on current interest rates and personal
debts. A typical first-time buyer needs a household gross annual
income of roughly $120,000 to $135,000 to pass the stress test for a
$600,000 home with 5% down. A broker can run the precise number for
your file and current rates.

109. Can I add my parents to my mortgage to help me qualify for more?
Yes. Adding a parent as a co-signer or co-borrower combines their
income with yours, lowering total debt service ratios and helping
you satisfy stress test guidelines. The parent takes on joint
financial responsibility for the mortgage.

110. Does my history of paying rent on time count toward mortgage approval?
Yes. Under the updated Canadian Mortgage Charter framework, lenders
can evaluate verified, consistent rent payment histories to help
establish creditworthiness for buyers with limited credit files.
Documentation usually requires bank statements showing recurring
rent payments and possibly a landlord letter.

111. If I buy a home in Toronto, do I get an extra land transfer tax rebate?
Yes. Toronto has its own municipal land transfer tax in addition to
the provincial LTT. First-time buyers can claim an additional
municipal rebate of up to $4,475 (on top of the $4,000 provincial
FTHB rebate). The municipal rebate is administered through the City
of Toronto.

112. What is the First-Time Home Buyer's Tax Credit (HBTC)?
A non-refundable federal tax credit claimed on the income tax
return for the year you buy your home. It provides up to $1,500 in
federal tax relief to help offset closing costs. It's claimed by
adding the amount on the Schedule 1 of your federal tax return.

113. How does the Ontario Enhanced HST rebate work for a $1.2 million new build?
For new homes priced between $1 million and $1.5 million, the
provincial HST rebate locks in at a flat $80,000 relief. For
qualifying first-time buyers on a purchase agreement during the
2026 budget's enhanced relief window, the rebate is typically
credited by the builder at closing or applied for through CRA after
federal regulations finalize.

114. What happened to the federal First-Time Home Buyer Incentive shared-equity program?
The federal shared-equity program (FTHBI), where the government
contributed to a buyer's down payment in exchange for a share of
home appreciation, was officially discontinued. It is no longer
operational. The replacement focus has shifted to FHSA, the HBP
increase, and HST relief on new builds.

115. What is the benefit of choosing a 30-year amortization over 25 years?
A longer amortization spreads payments over more years, lowering
the required monthly mortgage payment and improving monthly cash
flow. This can help marginal-affordability first-time buyers qualify
or have more breathing room in their budget.

116. What is the downside of a 30-year amortization?
You pay the principal down more slowly, so you pay substantially
more total interest over the life of the mortgage. A 30-year
amortization can add tens of thousands of dollars in total interest
compared to a 25-year on the same loan, depending on rate.

117. What is the typical CMHC insurance premium range?
Published CMHC premium rates range from 0.60% to 4.50% of the
total mortgage amount, depending on the down payment size and LTV.
The smaller the down payment, the higher the premium. A
non-traditional down payment (e.g., borrowed funds) at 90.01-95%
LTV triggers an additional premium surcharge.

118. Is there a tax charged on the CMHC insurance premium in Ontario?
Yes. Ontario charges 8% provincial sales tax (PST) on the CMHC
insurance premium. Crucially, this PST cannot be added to the
mortgage balance. It must be paid out-of-pocket at closing as part
of your legal closing costs.

119. Can I use first-time buyer programs to purchase a duplex or triplex?
Yes, provided you intend to occupy at least one unit as your
principal residence. CMHC allows high-ratio financing up to 95% LTV
on 1-2 unit owner-occupied properties and up to 90% LTV on 3-4 unit
owner-occupied properties.

120. What is "interim occupancy" when buying a pre-construction condo?
The period between the day you move into your new condo unit and
the day the building officially registers with the municipality.
During this phase, you pay the builder a monthly occupancy fee but
do not yet own the unit or have a mortgage. Final closing happens
once the building registers, at which point your mortgage funds and
you take title.

121. Can I claim first-time buyer incentives on a mobile home or modular home?
Yes, provided the home is on a permanent foundation, has year-round
municipal road access, and complies with CMHC's structural and
property standards. Some lenders are stricter than CMHC on these
properties, so the broker should confirm before relying on it.

122. Can I buy a home while still on a workplace probationary period?
Traditional A-lenders typically require you to complete the
employer's standard 3-6 month probationary period before funding,
unless you transitioned directly from an identical role in the same
field with no employment gap. Some B-lenders may have more
flexibility.

123. What is the single biggest mistake first-time buyers make with their credit?
Taking on new credit between pre-approval and closing: financing a
new car, opening new credit cards, or making big-ticket purchases on
credit. Each new debt is picked up by the lender at the
pre-funding employment-and-credit verification and can disqualify
the file just before closing. Avoid all new credit activity once
you have an accepted offer.
