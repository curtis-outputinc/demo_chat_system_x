# Mortgage Renewals and Switches: Frequently Asked Questions

Plain-language Q&A reference for the kinds of questions borrowers ask
about mortgage renewals and lender switches. Synthesized from two
AI-generated sources (ChatGPT and Gemini), deduplicated semantically,
plus 50 additional borrower-facing questions added to complete the
reference. Framework content on conventional / alt / private buckets
already lives in other corpus files; this file is renewal- and
switch-specific.

## How the agent should use this file

These Q&As give the agent background context for renewal and switch
conversations. Typical ranges noted (3-4 week processing, $200-300
discharge fees, 30-60 day initial renewal offer window, 90-120 day
rate hold, 620-650+ minimum credit for prime switches) are general
Ontario market context, not quotes. Real numbers vary by lender,
file, and date.

The single most important policy point for this topic: federally
regulated lenders are no longer required to apply the stress test
when a borrower switches an uninsured mortgage between FRFIs in a
straight transfer (no increase in loan amount, no increase in
amortization). This change took effect November 21, 2023 and is a
material competitive shift for borrowers at renewal.

## Category 1: Renewal vs Switch vs Refinance Fundamentals

1. What is a mortgage renewal?
A mortgage renewal happens at the end of your current mortgage term
(e.g., 5 years) when your existing contract expires. You sign a new
contract for a new term, rate, and conditions, usually with your
current lender, to keep the mortgage active.

2. What is a mortgage switch (also called a transfer)?
A switch moves your existing mortgage balance and remaining
amortization from your current lender to a new lender. The loan
amount stays the same; the new lender's rate and contract terms
apply.

3. What is the difference between renewal, switch, and refinance?
Renewal keeps you with your current lender at the end of term. A
switch moves the same loan balance to a new lender for a better
rate or terms. A refinance breaks or modifies the current contract
to add money, extend amortization, or change the mortgage
structure.

4. What is the difference between a switch and a refinance?
A switch keeps the loan amount the same and matches your existing
amortization. A refinance changes the amount, accesses equity,
consolidates debt, or alters the structure. Switches are simpler
and have lighter qualification rules; refinances require full
qualification.

## Category 2: The Stress Test Exemption (Key Policy)

5. Do I have to pass the mortgage stress test to switch lenders at renewal?
No, not anymore. Federally regulated lenders are exempt from
applying the Minimum Qualifying Rate (the stress test) when a
borrower switches an uninsured mortgage between FRFIs in a straight
transfer. This rule change took effect November 21, 2023.

6. Does the stress test exemption apply to both insured and uninsured mortgages?
Yes, in practice. Insured switches had effectively been
stress-test-exempt at switch already because the original insurance
premium had been paid on the original qualification. The November
2023 OSFI change extended the exemption to uninsured straight
switches between FRFIs.

7. What counts as a "straight switch" for the stress-test exemption?
A straight switch means you transfer your remaining principal
balance and match your exact remaining amortization to a new lender,
without adding ANY new debt and without extending the amortization
timeline. The moment either changes, the deal is reclassified.

8. Can I extend my amortization during a straight switch?
No. Extending your amortization (e.g., from 15 years back out to 25
or 30) is legally reclassified as a refinance and triggers a full
stress test plus full requalification.

9. Can I borrow extra cash during a switch?
No, not without losing the straight-switch treatment. The moment
you pull cash out, the deal becomes an equity refinance subject to
the full stress test and standard refinance rules (80% LTV cap,
appraisal, etc.).

10. Why was the stress-test exemption introduced?
To promote competition. The previous rule trapped borrowers at
their current lender at renewal because requalifying under the
stress test at switch time meant some borrowers couldn't move,
which let lenders offer weaker renewal rates. Removing the test for
straight switches lets borrowers shop freely.

11. Does the 4.5x Loan-to-Income (LTI) portfolio cap apply to straight switches?
No. OSFI's portfolio LTI cap on high-leverage originations is also
exempt for straight switches. This keeps the path clear for
borrowers to move at renewal even if their LTI sits above the
4.5x bank concentration limit.

12. If I change my loan amount or amortization, do I lose the switch rules entirely?
Yes. The moment you increase the principal or extend the
amortization, the deal is reclassified as a refinance. Full stress
test, full income verification, and full debt-service review apply.

## Category 3: Timing and Process

13. When should I start looking at renewal options?
Start 4 to 6 months before your maturity date. This gives time to
lock in a rate hold, compare lenders, and see your current
lender's actual offer before signing.

14. When will my current lender send me their renewal offer?
Most institutional banks send the initial renewal package 30 to 60
days before your maturity date. Their initial offered rates are
rarely their lowest.

15. Should I just sign the renewal paper my bank sends me?
Almost never. The initial offer is typically a generic posted rate
on a standard term. Brokers and competing lenders can almost always
beat it. Sign only after comparing.

16. How long does the mortgage switch process take to close?
A standard switch takes roughly 3 to 4 weeks to fully process. Plan
to have approvals in hand well in advance of your maturity date.

17. Can I renew my mortgage early?
Some lenders allow early renewal before the maturity date. The
offered rate may or may not be competitive. Worth comparing against
outside options before committing.

18. Can I switch lenders mid-term?
Yes, but breaking the mortgage before maturity triggers a prepayment
penalty. The penalty plus closing costs usually outweighs any rate
savings. Almost always better to wait and execute the switch on the
maturity date.

19. What happens if I do nothing when my mortgage expires?
Your current lender will automatically roll you into a 1-year open
fixed or variable rate, which is significantly higher than standard
closed rates. This will instantly spike your monthly payment. Your
broker can still execute a switch out of that open rate at any time
without penalty.

20. What happens if I miss my renewal deadline and my mortgage lapses?
Same as above: the lender rolls you to an expensive open rate
automatically. The mortgage doesn't cancel, but you're paying more
than you need to. A broker can move you to a competitive rate as
soon as the switch processes.

## Category 4: Costs and Fees

21. Does it cost money out-of-pocket to switch my mortgage to a new lender?
For a standard straight switch, often virtually cost-free. Most
incoming lenders pay for the legal transfer and basic processing
fees out of their own pocket to win your business.

22. What is a mortgage discharge fee?
The fee the OLD lender charges to remove their mortgage charge from
your title. Typically $200-$300 in Ontario. Some incoming lenders
will cover it; otherwise it comes out of your account on closing.

23. What is an assignment or transfer fee?
The administrative cost to register the switch with the provincial
land registry. Incoming competitive lenders almost always absorb
this fee entirely to win your business.

24. Will the new lender require an updated property appraisal?
Often, no. Lenders use automated valuation models (AVMs) for
straight switches. When a physical appraisal IS required, the
incoming lender frequently pays for it.

25. What happens to my automated property tax account when I switch lenders?
If your current lender collects property taxes with your mortgage
payment, that account is closed. The new lender either sets up an
identical tax collection program OR you switch to paying the
municipality directly.

26. Will I pay a penalty if I switch at renewal (maturity date)?
No. There's no prepayment penalty for ending a mortgage at its
maturity date. There may still be discharge, legal, or registration
costs depending on the lender, but no prepayment penalty.

27. Will I pay a penalty if I switch before renewal?
Yes, almost always. Breaking a fixed-rate mortgage triggers the
greater of three months interest or the Interest Rate Differential
(IRD). Variable-rate breakage is typically three months interest
only.

28. Can I make a lump-sum payment on my principal at renewal?
Yes. On the exact day your term expires, you can pay down as much
principal as you want with zero penalty before signing the new
contract.

29. Do I need a lawyer to switch my mortgage at renewal?
For most straight switches, no. Lenders use specialized in-house
closing services (like FCT or FNF) for low-cost transfers. You only
need your own lawyer if the transaction is more complex (refinance,
title change, etc.).

30. Can I get cash back when switching lenders?
Some lenders offer cashback or "free legal" promotions as switch
incentives. These typically come with strings (must stay with the
lender for X years or repay pro-rata). Math out the net benefit
before accepting.

## Category 5: Documents and Qualification

31. Do I need to qualify again when switching lenders?
Yes, for credit and income at the contract rate. You don't have to
pass the stress test (for straight switches), but the new lender
still verifies that you have stable income to cover the contract
payment and that your credit is acceptable.

32. Will a new lender check my credit score for a switch?
Yes. The incoming lender conducts a credit check to verify you've
maintained a history of on-time payments during your previous
term.

33. What is the minimum credit score for a prime-lender switch?
Most traditional banks and monoline lenders look for 620 to 650+
to approve a standard low-rate switch. Below that, the file may
need to go to a B-lender.

34. Do I need to verify my income for a straight switch?
Yes. You won't take the stress test, but the new lender still needs
to confirm you have stable, active income to cover the base
contract payment. Documents required are similar to a renewal but
lighter than a full refinance.

35. What documents are needed to switch lenders?
Common: current mortgage renewal statement, annual property tax
bill, home insurance confirmation, recent pay stub, recent T4 or
Notice of Assessment. Self-employed borrowers add T1 Generals,
business financials, or bank statements.

36. What documents are needed to renew with my current lender?
Simple renewals with the current lender often require fewer
documents. If you change the mortgage amount, amortization, title,
or product, more documents are required because the file
re-classifies as a refinance.

37. Can I switch lenders if my income recently changed?
Possibly. If income is lower, irregular, or harder to document,
switching is harder than renewing with your current lender. A
broker can advise whether to switch or stay.

38. Can I renew with my current lender if I don't qualify elsewhere?
Possibly. Existing lenders sometimes offer renewals without the
same level of requalification, but the rate may be less competitive
because they know you can't easily move. Worth using broker leverage
to negotiate.

39. Can I switch lenders if I am self-employed?
Yes, if you can document income. The new lender may require T1
Generals, NOAs, business financials, bank statements, or corporate
documents.

40. Can I switch lenders if I transitioned to self-employment during my term?
Harder. Most A-lenders want 2 years of self-employed income history
through tax filings. If you just transitioned, renewing with your
current lender (who typically doesn't re-verify employment) is
usually the safer path until you have 2 years of self-employed
history.

41. Can I switch lenders with bad credit?
Possibly, but options narrow. The new lender reviews credit score,
missed payments, collections, debt levels, mortgage payment history,
and property equity. Sub-prime files may need a B-lender switch
instead.

42. Can I renew with bad credit?
Possibly. Your current lender may still offer a renewal even with
weaker credit, though the rate may be less attractive. They're often
more lenient at renewal than a new lender would be on a switch.

43. Can I switch lenders if I missed payments during the term?
Possibly, but it makes switching harder. The new lender reviews how
many payments were missed, when, whether the mortgage is current,
and arrears status. Recent missed payments significantly limit
options.

44. Can I renew if I have mortgage arrears?
Possibly, but arrears create serious problems. The lender may
require the arrears to be paid as a condition of renewal or may
offer limited terms.

45. What if my credit score dropped significantly during my current term?
Switching to a prime A-bank may be difficult. The options are to
sign a short renewal with your current lender (who may not
re-verify credit deeply) or have a broker look at B-lender switch
paths.

## Category 6: Rates, Terms, and Strategy

46. Should I lock into a fixed or variable rate at renewal?
Fixed offers payment certainty and protection against rate
increases. Variable can save money if the Bank of Canada lowers
rates but introduces payment volatility. Depends on your risk
tolerance, budget, and rate outlook.

47. Can I switch from variable to fixed at renewal?
Yes. At maturity you can restructure the mortgage however you
prefer, including switching the rate type, without penalty.

48. Can I switch from fixed to variable at renewal?
Yes, same as above. At maturity, the rate type is open to change.

49. What term length should I choose at renewal?
Shorter fixed terms (2 or 3 years) are popular when rates are
shifting because they avoid getting locked in for too long. Longer
terms (5 years) offer long-term peace of mind but higher breakage
penalties if you ever need out.

50. What is a renewal "rate hold"?
A commitment from a lender to honor a specific interest rate for
your upcoming renewal during a set window. Brokers can secure rate
holds for 90 to 120 days to protect you from market spikes while
you wait for maturity.

51. How much can a small rate reduction save me when switching?
Dropping your rate by 0.50% on a $500,000 mortgage saves roughly
$12,000 in interest over a standard 5-year term. The savings
compound on larger balances and longer terms.

52. Why do lenders offer better rates to new switch clients than to renewing clients?
Banks allocate large acquisition budgets to win new market share.
Their retention departments count on consumer inertia to accept
generic renewal rates. Shopping (or having a broker shop) breaks
the inertia and forces competitive pricing.

53. Can I switch lenders just to get a lower rate?
Yes, this is the single most common reason borrowers switch. Even
modest rate improvements compound to meaningful savings over a
5-year term.

54. Can I switch to get better mortgage product features?
Yes. Some borrowers switch for stronger prepayment privileges,
portability, a HELOC sub-account option, different rate types, or
more flexible terms not offered by their current lender.

55. What if my current lender matches the rate I found elsewhere?
You can sign their renewal to save the paperwork and time involved
in a switch. Just check for hidden conditions, restrictive
prepayment privileges, or unfavorable breakage clauses before
signing.

56. What is the single best strategy to secure the lowest renewal rate?
Connect with a licensed broker 90 to 120 days before maturity.
Securing a competitive rate hold creates real outside pressure on
your current lender, forcing them to offer their best rate to keep
your business.

## Category 7: Modifying the Mortgage at Renewal

57. Can I change my amortization at renewal?
Yes, but extending it triggers a refinance, not a switch. Shortening
the amortization (paying off faster) is usually fine within a
renewal or straight switch.

58. Can I increase my mortgage amount at renewal?
Yes, but it becomes a refinance. The lender reviews property value,
equity, income, debts, and credit. Full qualification with stress
test applies.

59. Can I consolidate debt at renewal?
Yes, but adding debt to the mortgage requires a refinance, not a
straight switch. The lender reviews available equity and runs full
qualification.

60. Can I access home equity at renewal?
Yes, but accessing equity is a refinance, not a renewal or switch.
Subject to the 80% LTV cap and full qualification.

61. Can I add a HELOC at renewal?
Possibly. Adding a HELOC sub-account requires lender approval,
property value review, full income qualification, and enough
available equity. Usually treated as a refinance.

62. Can I remove someone from the mortgage at renewal?
No, not as a straight switch. Removing a borrower changes the
underlying legal liability structure. Requires full requalification
and is treated as a refinance.

63. Can I add someone to the mortgage at renewal?
Possibly, treated as a refinance. The new person's income, credit,
debts, and role on title are reviewed.

64. Can I buy out my spouse at renewal?
Possibly, treated as a refinance. The remaining spouse must
qualify on their own for the full mortgage balance, and a
separation agreement plus legal work are typically required.

65. Can I switch lenders after separation or divorce?
Possibly. The new lender may need a separation agreement, income
documents, support payment details, and property and mortgage
balance information. If the file structure changes (e.g., spouse
removed), it becomes a refinance.

## Category 8: Property and Mortgage Types

66. Can I switch my mortgage if my property value has decreased?
Yes, as long as your remaining balance does not exceed the current
property value. Because a straight switch involves no cash-out,
minor market drops do not block the transfer.

67. Can I switch lenders if I have a rental property?
Yes, but the new lender reviews lease agreements, rental income,
property expenses, mortgage balance, and the borrower's full debt
picture. Rental property switches have different rules than
owner-occupied homes.

68. Can I switch lenders on a condo?
Yes. The lender reviews condo fees, unit value, building type,
location, taxes, and marketability.

69. Can I switch lenders on a rural property?
Possibly. Rural properties get more review due to location,
acreage, zoning, water and septic systems, and resale market
liquidity.

70. What is a collateral charge mortgage?
A mortgage charge registered for a higher amount than the actual
loan, allowing the lender to add future borrowing (HELOC, second
loan) without re-registering. More flexible for future credit but
historically harder to switch.

71. What is a standard charge mortgage?
A mortgage charge registered for the specific mortgage amount only.
Simpler to switch between lenders than a collateral charge.

72. Can I switch lenders if I have a collateral mortgage?
Yes. Most modern lenders can transfer collateral mortgages with
minimal friction now. There may be legal or registration costs
depending on the file complexity.

73. Can I switch a mortgage if I have a HELOC attached to it?
Trickier. If the HELOC has an outstanding balance, it must either
be paid off or the entire combined product must be re-qualified and
transferred together under a specialized collateral charge program.

74. My mortgage is with a B-lender. Can I switch to an A-lender at renewal?
Yes, if your credit has improved (typically above 650) and your
income is now verifiable through traditional tax documents.
Renewal is the perfect time to graduate from B to A pricing.

75. My mortgage is with a private lender. Can I switch to a traditional bank?
Moving from a private lender to an institutional bank is processed
as a brand-new mortgage application, not a switch. Full income
verification and the stress test apply.

## Category 9: Life Scenarios

76. Can I switch lenders while on maternity leave or short-term disability?
Yes. Incoming lenders accept maternity and disability income with a
letter from your employer confirming permanent employment status
and your planned return date.

77. What if I lost my job right before my renewal date?
A new lender can't approve a switch without verifiable income.
Safest move: sign the renewal with your current lender (who rarely
asks for new job confirmation on standard renewals) and switch
later when employment stabilizes.

78. Can I switch lenders if I have outstanding property tax or CRA arrears?
No. Incoming lenders require proof that property taxes are fully
current and there are no active tax liens before they accept a
transfer.

79. What is the Canadian Mortgage Charter?
A framework of federal guidelines published in the 2023 Fall
Economic Statement requiring federally regulated lenders to contact
borrowers well before renewal, provide transparent rate options,
and offer relief mechanisms for borrowers facing payment hardship.

## Category 10: Broker Role and Information

80. Can a mortgage broker help with renewal?
Yes. A broker compares your current lender's renewal offer with
other lender options and can secure a competitive rate hold on
your behalf.

81. Can a broker negotiate my renewal rate?
A broker can compare options and present competitive alternatives.
Your current lender often improves its offer once they see you have
real outside options.

82. What information should I provide for a renewal or switch review?
Useful: current lender, mortgage balance, maturity date, current
rate, the renewal offer (if received), property value, income,
debts, credit score range, and whether you want to borrow more
money.

83. Is switching lenders always worth it?
No. Switching is worth it when the rate or product improvement
outweighs the costs, paperwork, and qualification time. A broker
can do the break-even math for your file.

84. Should I accept my lender's first renewal offer?
Not automatically. Initial offers are usually generic and not the
lender's best rate. Compare with at least one outside offer
(through a broker) before signing.

## Category 11: Additional borrower questions

85. How does my mortgage payment change if I renew at a higher rate?
Your monthly payment goes up. The exact increase depends on the
rate difference, remaining balance, and amortization. On a $500,000
balance, every 1% rate increase typically adds roughly $250-$300 to
the monthly payment.

86. How does my mortgage payment change if I renew at a lower rate?
Your monthly payment goes down. Borrowers sometimes use the savings
to make lump-sum prepayments or maintain the higher payment to pay
the mortgage off faster.

87. Can I renew for a shorter term to wait for better rates?
Yes. Short-term renewals (1 or 2 years) are common when borrowers
believe rates will fall and want to renew again sooner at a lower
rate. The trade-off: short terms typically carry slightly higher
rates than longer terms.

88. Can I renew for a longer term to lock in current rates?
Yes. 5-year (or longer) terms are common when borrowers want
payment stability. Long terms carry higher breakage penalties if
you ever need to exit mid-term.

89. What is a 6-month convertible mortgage?
A short-term mortgage product that lets the borrower convert to a
longer-term fixed rate at any time without penalty. Useful for
borrowers who want to wait for better rates before committing.

90. Can I change my payment frequency at renewal?
Yes. Renewal is a natural time to switch between monthly,
semi-monthly, biweekly, accelerated biweekly, weekly, or
accelerated weekly payments.

91. Can I switch to accelerated biweekly at renewal?
Yes. Accelerated biweekly payments add one extra monthly payment
per year and shave years off the amortization while reducing total
interest.

92. Will my pre-authorized debit continue automatically at renewal?
Usually yes if you stay with the same lender. If you switch, you
set up new PAD with the new lender. The old PAD ends with the old
mortgage payout.

93. Can the new lender require I close my chequing account at the old bank?
No. The mortgage and your chequing account are independent
products. You can keep accounts at multiple institutions.

94. How long after switching does my first payment to the new lender happen?
Typically one month after the switch closes, similar to any new
mortgage. There may be an interest adjustment for the days between
close and the first payment date.

95. Do I need to update my home insurance when switching lenders?
Yes. The new lender must be named as loss payee on your home
insurance policy. Your insurance broker updates the policy binder
before funding.

96. Will I need to keep mortgage life insurance through the new lender?
Mortgage life insurance through the old lender ends when the old
mortgage is paid out. You can decline mortgage life insurance with
the new lender (most planners recommend term life insurance
instead, which is portable and often cheaper).

97. Can I get cash back when switching lenders?
Yes, some lenders offer cashback at closing. These offers usually
require you to stay for a minimum term, with pro-rata clawback if
you leave early. Read the terms.

98. Are penalty-buy-out switch offers from competitors legitimate?
Yes, some lenders will absorb part or all of your old lender's
prepayment penalty to win your business. The new rate may be
slightly higher to compensate, so do the full math.

99. Are switch cashback offers worth taking?
Sometimes. Calculate the cashback against the rate spread over the
term. A higher rate plus cashback can be better or worse than a
lower rate without cashback, depending on the math.

100. Can I negotiate the new lender's rate offer?
Yes, especially through a broker. Lenders publish rate sheets to
brokers that aren't the consumer-facing posted rates. Always
negotiate.

101. How much can a broker realistically save me at renewal?
On a typical $400K-$500K mortgage, a broker often saves 0.25% to
0.75% on the rate compared to the initial lender renewal offer.
That can add up to $5,000-$15,000 over a 5-year term.

102. Do brokers have access to better rates than I can see online?
Often yes. Broker rate sheets include discounted rates not posted
to the public, plus the ability to negotiate further on stronger
files.

103. What's the difference between a "posted rate" and a "discounted rate"?
Posted rate is the official benchmark rate the lender publishes
publicly (used as the reference for IRD calculations). The
discounted rate is the actual rate offered to qualified borrowers,
often well below posted. Most consumers get the discounted rate.

104. Can I switch mid-term if a competitor pays my penalty?
Yes, some incoming lenders offer "we'll cover your penalty"
promotions. The math depends on penalty size, new rate, and the
rate spread over the remaining term.

105. Can I move from a closed mortgage to an open mortgage at renewal?
Yes. Open mortgages typically carry higher rates but allow unlimited
prepayment. Some borrowers choose open during periods of expected
sale or major refinance.

106. Can I move from an open mortgage to a closed mortgage at renewal?
Yes, and many borrowers do at renewal to lock in a lower rate once
their short-term need for flexibility has passed.

107. Will my mortgage statement show that I switched lenders?
Your old lender sends a final statement showing the loan was paid
out at maturity (or earlier). The new lender starts a fresh
statement history. Your credit report shows both: the old account
closed in good standing, the new account opened.

108. Will switching lenders hurt my credit score?
Marginally and temporarily. The hard credit pull from the new
lender causes a small short-term dip. Closing the old account in
good standing and opening the new one paid-as-agreed has a neutral-
to-positive long-term effect.

109. Does switching lenders multiple times hurt my credit?
Repeated switches in a short window can compound the small
short-term hits from credit inquiries. Most borrowers switch at
most every 3-5 years, so this isn't usually an issue.

110. Does switching restart my amortization?
No, not on a straight switch. Your remaining amortization carries
over to the new lender. You explicitly chose a longer amortization
to reset, which would re-classify as a refinance.

111. What happens to my prepayment privileges when I switch?
You get the new lender's prepayment privileges going forward. The
old lender's privileges end with the old mortgage. Confirm the
new lender's annual prepayment percentage before switching if
prepayment flexibility matters to you.

112. Do prepayment privileges reset at renewal?
Yes. Annual prepayment privileges typically reset on the anniversary
of the new term. Each new term gives you a fresh annual privilege.

113. Can I take my mortgage with me if I move houses (porting)?
Yes, if the lender offers a portability feature. Porting keeps
your existing rate on a new property purchase, avoiding the
prepayment penalty. Not all lenders offer porting; check before
signing.

114. Can I increase my mortgage when I port?
Yes. "Port and increase" combines your existing mortgage at the
locked-in rate with new money at the current market rate. The two
blend into one new payment.

115. What if I move and don't want to port?
You discharge the old mortgage (paying any applicable penalty if
breaking mid-term) and start fresh with a new mortgage on the new
property. Sometimes cheaper, sometimes more expensive than porting,
depending on rate spreads.

116. What is a discharge statement?
A document from your lender showing the exact payout amount needed
to fully discharge the mortgage on a specific date. Your lawyer
requests it from the lender before closing a refinance, switch, or
sale.

117. How long does it take to discharge a mortgage after I pay it off?
The lender's lawyer prepares and registers the discharge typically
within 30-60 days of payout. Your lawyer can confirm the
registered discharge to ensure clear title.

118. Can my mortgage be renewed automatically without my signature?
Effectively yes, by default — if you do nothing, the lender rolls
you into their automatic open or short-term renewal rate. To
actively renew with terms you want (or to switch), you must engage
before maturity.

119. Will the new lender accept my existing pre-authorized debit setup?
You generally need to set up new pre-authorized debit (PAD) with
the new lender. The old PAD ends with the old mortgage. The new
lender provides forms for you to authorize new PAD.

120. Can a switch include a HELOC sub-account?
Yes, if the new lender's product includes HELOC. The combined
mortgage + HELOC is usually registered under a collateral charge.
Both pieces must qualify together.

121. What is a "blend and extend" renewal offer?
Your current lender blends your existing rate with the current
market rate into a new, longer term. Used when the borrower wants
to avoid the breakage penalty of breaking the current term but also
wants to lock in current rates for longer.

122. Will switching lenders show on my credit report?
Yes. The old mortgage shows as closed in good standing; the new
mortgage shows as a new tradeline. Both visible to future lenders.

## What this corpus file is not

- It is not a substitute for canonical CMHC / OSFI corpus files on
  insurance, LTV ceilings, MQR, or DSR calculations.
- It is not jurisdictional regulatory text (Mortgages Act, MBLAA);
  those live in jurisdiction folders.
- It is not legal, tax, or financial advice for any specific
  borrower's situation.
- It is not a guarantee of approval, rate, or specific outcome on a
  renewal or switch.
- The typical ranges noted (discharge fee ranges, processing
  timelines, rate hold windows, minimum credit thresholds, typical
  rate-savings examples) are Ontario market context as of the
