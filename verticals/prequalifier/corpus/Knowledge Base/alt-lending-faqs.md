# B-Lending and Alternative Lending: Frequently Asked Questions

Plain-language Q&A reference for the kinds of questions borrowers
actually ask about alternative (B-lender) mortgages. Synthesized from
two AI-generated sources (Gemini and ChatGPT), deduplicated
semantically, with 50 additional borrower-facing questions added. The
framework explanation of alternative lending (who fits, what bucket
markers look like) lives in `alternative-lending.md`. The regulatory
background (B-20 stress test treatment, OSFI rules) lives in the
jurisdictional corpus files.

## How the agent should use this file

These Q&As give the agent background context for B-lending
conversations. Many typical ranges noted below (5.5-8% B-lender rates,
1% lender fee, $350-600 appraisal, 1-2 year terms, 10-15% annual
prepayment privileges, etc.) are general Ontario market context as of
the authoring date, not quotes. Real numbers vary by lender, file,
location, and date. The agent should describe ranges in general terms
and route specifics to a licensed broker on the follow-up call.

## Category 1: Alternative Lending Fundamentals

1. What is a B-lender or alternative mortgage?
A B-lender, also called an alternative lender, is a regulated
financial institution (often a trust company, certain credit unions,
or specialty lenders) that uses more flexible guidelines than major
banks. B-lenders work with borrowers who have bruised credit,
self-employed income, recent credit issues, higher debt levels, or
non-traditional income that does not fit strict bank rules.

2. Is a B-lender the same as a private lender?
No. A B-lender is an institutional alternative lender with more
flexible guidelines than a major bank but still operates under formal
underwriting standards. A private lender is an individual, company, or
Mortgage Investment Corporation that lends based primarily on equity
and property value. B-lending sits between major bank lending and
private lending in cost and flexibility.

3. Are B-lenders safe to use?
Yes. B-lenders are regulated financial institutions subject to
federal or provincial oversight (such as FSRA in Ontario) and
consumer protection laws. They are not predatory or unregulated.

4. What is the main difference between an A-lender and a B-lender?
A-lenders (the major banks) require strong credit, traditional T4 or
salaried income, and pass the strict federal stress test. B-lenders
accept lower credit scores, offer flexible income documentation,
allow higher debt ratios, and charge somewhat higher rates plus a
lender fee in exchange.

5. Why would my broker suggest a B-lender over a traditional bank?
Common reasons: self-employed income that does not show fully on tax
returns, minor credit blemishes, unfiled tax returns, recent credit
events, high but manageable debt ratios, or income types the bank
will not fully consider. A B-lender offers a workable path while the
borrower works toward returning to a bank.

6. Are credit unions considered B-lenders?
Sometimes. Many credit unions are A-lenders, but some operate with
more flexible alternative-style guidelines, especially when they are
provincially regulated. Ontario credit unions in particular have more
flexibility because they are not federally regulated under OSFI's
B-20 stress test.

7. Are trust companies considered B-lenders?
Often yes. Trust companies frequently operate in the alternative
lending space with more flexible underwriting than the big six banks,
while still being formal institutional lenders.

8. What is a Mortgage Investment Entity (MIE) or MIC?
A Mortgage Investment Corporation (MIC) pools capital from investors
to fund a portfolio of mortgages. Some MICs operate in the private
lending space, while others operate as more institutional B-lenders
depending on their structure and capital source.

9. Who usually uses a B-lender?
Borrowers with lower credit, self-employed income, recent missed
payments, high debt, prior consumer proposals, recent bankruptcies, or
income that a major bank will not fully accept. Many use B-lending as
a temporary step before returning to a bank.

## Category 2: Credit Qualification

10. What is the minimum credit score for a B-lender?
The absolute minimum is typically around 550, though scores above 600
secure more competitive alternative rates and broader lender choice.
Below 550, the file usually moves toward private lending instead.

11. Can I get a mortgage with bad credit through a B-lender?
Possibly. B-lenders accept lower credit scores than major banks, but
they still review the full file: credit score, payment history,
income, down payment, property value, debts, and the reason the
credit was damaged.

12. Can I get a B-lender mortgage if I am currently in a consumer proposal?
Yes. Many alternative lenders approve files where the consumer
proposal is paid off and discharged at closing using the new mortgage
proceeds. Discharged proposals (paid out before application) also
qualify, often at better terms.

13. How long after bankruptcy can I apply for alternative lending?
Often immediately upon receiving the official discharge, provided
there is a 20% down payment or 20% equity in an existing home. The
lender will want to see the discharge documents, rebuilt credit, and
reasons for the original bankruptcy.

14. Can I qualify for a B-mortgage if I have active collections or tax liens?
Yes, but the lender typically requires those collection accounts and
any CRA tax balances to be paid off in full from the mortgage proceeds
at closing.

15. Does a B-lender look at my personal debt like car loans and credit cards?
Yes. The lender reviews monthly debt obligations, but with more
leniency than a major bank on how those balances impact final
borrowing capacity.

16. Will utilizing a B-lender hurt my credit score?
No. Making consistent on-time payments on an alternative mortgage is
one of the best ways to rebuild your credit history.

17. What if my credit score drops further while I am with a B-lender?
If credit degrades significantly during the term, the lender may
decline to renew, which could force a transition to private financing
at renewal. Maintaining payments and avoiding new credit damage is
critical during a B-lender term.

18. Can I use a B-lender if I have no credit history?
Possibly. The broker may rely on alternative credit evidence: rent
payment history, utility payment history, phone bills, bank deposit
history, or international credit reports. A larger down payment and
stronger income help offset the lack of established Canadian credit.

19. Do B-lenders accept newcomers to Canada?
Yes. Some B-lenders specialize in newcomer files and accept
international credit reports, larger down payments, employment
strength, or alternative documentation when Canadian credit history
is limited.

## Category 3: Income and Employment Verification

20. Will a B-lender require proof of income?
Yes, almost always. The proof can be more flexible than what a bank
accepts (bank statements, business records, contracts) but the lender
still needs to understand how the borrower will make payments.

21. Do B-lenders use the federal mortgage stress test?
Federally regulated B-lenders still apply the stress test (the
Minimum Qualifying Rate). Provincially regulated lenders, including
some Ontario credit unions, can use more flexible benchmarks because
they are not bound by OSFI's stress test rule.

22. What is the maximum Total Debt Service ratio for a B-lender?
Most alternative lenders cap TDS between 44% and 50%, which is
meaningfully more flexible than the 44% rule typically enforced at
major banks. Some lenders may stretch higher on exceptional files.

23. Do B-lenders care more about income or property equity?
They care about both. B-lenders are more flexible than banks but
still want to see that the mortgage payments are sustainable. If
income is weaker, stronger equity or a larger down payment helps
offset the file.

24. Can I qualify with cash income?
Only if cash income can be documented through consistent, verifiable
deposits into bank statements over 6 to 12 months. Unreported or
unverifiable cash income is very difficult to use for qualification.

25. How does a B-lender view child support or alimony income?
They will count it as qualifying income if backed by a legally signed
separation agreement and shown through consistent recurring deposits
over at least 6 months.

26. Can I qualify with a B-lender if I have been at my new job less than 3 months?
It is challenging if you are still on workplace probation. The file
is stronger if you transitioned directly from an identical role in
the same industry without a gap.

27. Can contract workers use B-lenders?
Yes. B-lenders may consider contract workers when the broker can
present contracts, bank deposits, tax documents, length of time in
the field, and reasonable expectation of continued income.

28. Can I qualify if I am paid only in tips?
Tips count if they appear as verifiable deposits over a sustained
period. Lenders generally need to see 6 to 12 months of consistent
deposits matching the stated income.

29. Can I qualify if I recently changed jobs?
Possibly. The lender will want to know if the job is permanent,
full-time, contract, or probationary. A job letter, paystubs,
employment history, and stability all factor in.

30. Do B-lenders accept rental income to help me qualify?
Yes, under strict guidelines. The lender reviews net lease
agreements, recent rent deposits, and property profitability.
Investment properties often must show standalone cash flow viability
under newer regulations.

31. Can I qualify with a B-lender if my income is entirely from investments?
Yes, with accountant-prepared notes and multiple years of stable
investment income shown through personal tax filings.

32. Do B-lenders look at my bank statements?
Frequently yes, especially for self-employed or alternative-income
borrowers. Bank statements help confirm cash flow, income deposits,
debt payments, and any NSF (non-sufficient funds) activity.

## Category 4: Self-Employed Specifics

33. I am self-employed and write down my income for taxes. Can a B-lender help?
Yes. B-lenders specialize in stated income programs that allow 6 to
12 months of business bank statements to demonstrate true cash flow,
rather than relying only on personal tax returns that reflect
write-downs.

34. What is a "stated income" program?
A program where the lender accepts that the borrower's tax return
does not reflect their real earnings, allowing them to state a
reasonable income supported by business bank statements, financial
statements, or other alternative documentation.

35. What documents do I need as a self-employed borrower for a B-deal?
Typical package: business license or articles of incorporation, 6
months of corporate bank statements, the two most recent personal
Notices of Assessment, T1 Generals, T2125 statement of business
activities if applicable, and additional financial statements where
the file is more complex.

36. Can the broker use my business deposits to prove income?
Yes, this is standard practice for self-employed B-lender files. The
broker reviews business deposits over 6 to 12 months to build a
defensible income picture.

## Category 5: Down Payment, Equity, and LTV

37. What is the maximum loan-to-value (LTV) for a B-lender?
B-lenders cap financing at a maximum of 80% LTV. This means a minimum
20% down payment is required for purchases, or a minimum 20% equity
position is required for refinances.

38. Can I get a B-lender mortgage with 10% down?
Less likely. With less than 20% down, the mortgage is high-ratio and
must be insured, which usually means qualifying through the insurer's
rules (which mirror A-lender standards). B-lender financing is
typically reserved for conventional (20%+ down) transactions.

39. Can I get a B-lender mortgage with 20% down?
Yes. With 20% down, the mortgage is conventional and B-lenders have
their standard product line available, subject to credit, income,
property, and debt review.

40. Can I borrow up to 95% with a B-lender?
Generally no. The 80% LTV cap is the standard alternative-lending
ceiling. High-ratio (above 80% LTV) lending typically requires
insured A-lender financing.

41. Can I use a gifted down payment for a B-lender mortgage?
Yes. Gifted down payments from immediate family members are accepted
with a signed gift letter and proof of the funds transfer.

42. Can I borrow my down payment from a line of credit for a B-mortgage?
Generally no. Most B-lenders require the 20% down to come from your
own savings, investments, or a non-repayable family gift, not from
borrowed funds.

43. Can I refinance my home up to 85% LTV with an alternative lender?
No. The regulatory ceiling for standard refinances in Ontario is 80%
LTV, regardless of whether the lender is A or B. To access more
equity above that ceiling, the file moves to private lending.

44. Can I get a HELOC through a B-lender?
Some B-lenders offer bundled HELOC products, but standalone
revolving HELOC products are uncommon in the alternative space and
heavily restricted compared to fixed-term alternative mortgages.

## Category 6: Rates, Fees, and Total Cost

45. What are typical B-lending interest rates?
Alternative mortgage rates generally range between 5.5% and 8%,
depending on credit score, down payment size, property type, and
file complexity.

46. Are B-lender rates higher than bank rates?
Yes, typically. The premium reflects the more flexible underwriting
and the additional risk the lender takes on with non-prime files.

47. Do B-lenders charge processing or setup fees?
Yes, virtually all alternative lenders charge a one-time lender fee,
typically around 1% of the total mortgage amount, deducted directly
from the loan advance at closing.

48. Do I have to pay my mortgage broker a fee for a B-lender deal?
Sometimes. In more complex restructuring files, a broker fee of 0.5%
to 1% may apply, disclosed in advance. Many standard B-lender deals
do not include a separate broker fee.

49. Are the legal fees higher when closing an alternative mortgage?
Slightly higher than standard bank deals, because real estate
lawyers complete additional compliance documentation and process
unique lender conditions. The difference is usually modest.

50. Is there an appraisal fee for B-lending applications?
Yes. An independent appraiser typically charges between $350 and
$600 to confirm current market value. The borrower pays this cost.

51. Is CMHC insurance required for alternative mortgages?
No. B-lender mortgages require a minimum 20% down payment, which
makes them conventional (low-ratio) loans that do not require
default insurance.

## Category 7: Term Structure, Payments, and Prepayment

52. What mortgage term lengths do alternative lenders offer?
B-lenders predominantly offer short-term fixed options: 1-year or
2-year terms are the most common. This gives the borrower time to
repair credit or improve income before moving back to an A-lender.

53. How long do people usually stay with a B-lender?
Most borrowers aim for 1 to 3 years, depending on how long it takes
to rebuild credit, document income properly, reduce debt, or build
more equity to qualify with a bank.

54. Can I get a variable rate mortgage with a B-lender?
Variable rate options exist but are less common in the alternative
space. Most borrowers prefer a short-term fixed rate to keep payments
stable while they work toward returning to traditional lending.

55. Are there prepayment penalties if I break a B-lender mortgage early?
Yes, usually three months of interest or an Interest Rate Differential
(IRD), whichever is greater, depending on the specific lender's
contract terms.

56. Can I make extra lump-sum payments on an alternative mortgage?
Yes. Most B-lenders allow lump-sum prepayments of 10% to 15% of the
original principal annually without penalty, in line with standard
prepayment privileges.

57. Can I get an open mortgage with a B-lender?
Open mortgages exist but are less common in the alternative space.
Most B-lender mortgages are closed for the term length with
prepayment privileges, since the shorter term itself provides
flexibility.

## Category 8: Properties and Locations

58. Do alternative lenders fund high-rise condominiums in Ontario?
Yes, in major urban areas, provided the building has a healthy
reserve fund and no active structural litigation. Lenders may
request the condo's reserve fund study and recent meeting minutes.

59. Can I get a B-mortgage on a rural property with a well and septic system?
Yes, though lenders may cap LTV at 70% or 75% (lower than the
standard 80%) and require passing water potability and septic
functionality inspections.

60. Do B-lenders finance commercial or mixed-use properties?
Most residential B-lenders focus exclusively on residential
properties (1 to 4 units). True commercial files must go through
dedicated commercial alternative divisions.

61. Does property location inside Ontario affect my B-lender approval?
Yes. Major urban centres (GTA, Ottawa, London, Hamilton) get the
best rates and maximum leverage. Remote or northern areas face
stricter caps because of slower resale markets.

62. Do alternative lenders finance flipped properties or major fixers?
No. B-lenders require properties in move-in-ready condition. Fixer-
uppers and construction-in-progress files typically go to private
lenders that offer specialized construction or renovation products.

63. Can I buy an investment property using a B-lender?
Yes, but newer regulations require rental properties to show
standalone cash flow viability under tighter independent reviews to
prevent over-leveraging.

64. Can I get a B-lender mortgage for a rental property?
Yes. The lender reviews rental income, property location, type,
condition, equity position, and borrower strength. Rental income is
often only partially counted toward qualifying income.

65. Can a non-resident of Canada apply for alternative lending in Ontario?
It is highly restrictive. Alternative lenders prioritize borrowers
with established Canadian credit histories and stable local tax
residency. Most files require a Canadian co-borrower or significantly
larger down payment.

## Category 9: Use Cases and Common Scenarios

66. Can I buy a home with a B-lender?
Yes. Many borrowers buy homes through B-lenders when they cannot
qualify through a major bank. Down payment of 20% or more is
required, and the lender still reviews income, credit, property,
debt, and affordability.

67. Can I refinance with a B-lender?
Yes. A B-lender refinance is used when the borrower has enough equity
and the file fits B-lender guidelines. Common reasons: consolidate
debt, access equity, pay arrears, pay CRA debt, or restructure
finances.

68. Can I switch from a bank to a B-lender at renewal?
Yes, if the bank cannot or will not renew you and a B-lender can.
The broker should check penalties, maturity date, rate comparison,
fees, and whether the switch creates enough net benefit.

69. My bank refused my renewal due to higher rates and stress test issues.
Can a B-lender take it over?
Yes, this is a common B-lender scenario. If the borrower's file
fails the bank's stress test at current rates, a B-lender can use
more flexible criteria to absorb the mortgage at renewal.

70. Can B-lenders help with CRA tax debt?
Yes, on the condition that the CRA debt is paid in full from the
mortgage proceeds at closing before any other cash is released to
the borrower.

71. Can I use a B-lender to consolidate debt?
Yes, if there is enough equity and the file qualifies. Consolidating
high-interest debt into a B-lender mortgage can lower monthly cash
flow pressure, but it increases the total mortgage balance and
secures previously unsecured debt against the home.

72. Can I use a B-lender to pay off credit cards?
Possibly. A refinance may roll credit card balances into the
mortgage if there is sufficient equity, reducing monthly payments
considerably. The trade-off is converting unsecured debt to secured
debt and extending the repayment period.

73. Can I use a B-lender to pay off a line of credit?
Yes, if there is enough equity. A refinance can consolidate unsecured
lines of credit into the mortgage, simplifying payments.

74. Can B-lenders help if I missed mortgage payments?
Possibly. Missed payments are a red flag, but many alternative
lenders will review the file. The lender needs to know the number of
missed payments, current arrears status, whether legal action has
started, and the equity available.

75. Can I get a B-lender mortgage if I am in power of sale?
Possibly, but this is urgent and complex. There must be enough
equity, enough time before the sale date, and the file may shift
toward private lending depending on urgency. Files like this should
reach a broker immediately.

## Category 10: Exit Strategy and Renewal

76. What is an exit plan from a B-lender mortgage?
An exit plan is the borrower's strategy to leave B-lender financing
later, typically by qualifying for a cheaper A-lender mortgage at
renewal. Common steps: improve credit score, build clean tax-filed
income, pay down debts, increase equity.

77. Can I move from a B-lender back to a bank later?
That is the typical goal. By keeping perfect payment history,
reducing other debts, and resolving the issues that pushed you to
B-lending originally, you can usually graduate back to an A-lender
at renewal or refinance.

78. How do I eventually graduate from a B-lender back to a major bank?
By maintaining perfect mortgage payments throughout the B-lender
term, keeping credit utilization below 30%, filing clean tax returns
with strong reported income, and avoiding new credit damage during
the 1 to 2 year B-lender term.

79. What happens when my alternative mortgage term ends?
If you have paid on time, the B-lender typically offers a renewal.
Most borrowers, however, aim to transition back to an A-lender at
renewal time to access lower rates. The broker should plan the exit
60 to 90 days before maturity.

80. Can I renew with a B-lender?
Yes. Renewal is usually offered when payments have been on time,
though terms and rates may change. Many borrowers prefer to move
back to a bank at renewal if they qualify.

## Category 11: Process, Documents, and Closing

81. How long does it take to get an approval from an alternative lender?
Typically 48 to 72 hours for an initial commitment, and 2 to 3 weeks
to complete full document verification and funding.

82. Will a B-lender approve me faster than a bank?
Sometimes, especially for straightforward files with complete
documents. Complex files (self-employed, multiple income sources,
problem credit) can still take time.

83. What documents should I prepare for a B-lender?
Standard package: government ID, mortgage statement (if refinancing),
property tax bill, income documents, bank statements, credit details,
debt statements, purchase agreement if buying, appraisal if requested.
Self-employed borrowers add business bank statements, T1 Generals,
NOAs, articles of incorporation, or financial statements.

84. Do B-lenders require appraisals?
Almost always yes. The lender wants to confirm market value,
condition, location, and saleability of the property, since the
property is their collateral.

85. Can I add a co-signer to my B-lender mortgage?
Yes. A co-signer with strong credit and stable income can
significantly improve approval odds and may lower the interest rate
offered.

86. Can I break my current mortgage and move to a B-lender?
Possibly, but the prepayment penalty can be expensive. The broker
compares the penalty against new rate, fees, payment change,
consolidation benefit, and whether waiting until renewal would make
more sense.

87. What is loan-to-value (LTV)?
LTV is the mortgage amount divided by the property value, expressed
as a percentage. For example, a $600,000 mortgage on an $800,000
home is 75% LTV.

88. Why does LTV matter for B-lending?
B-lenders use LTV to measure risk and structure pricing. Lower LTV
(more equity) generally opens more lender options and better rates.
Higher LTV means fewer lenders, stricter conditions, or higher
pricing. The 80% LTV cap is firm for B-lenders.

## Category 12: Risks and Comparisons

89. Is B-lending risky?
B-lending costs more than A-lending and involves shorter terms, lender
fees, and stricter exit planning. Used correctly as a temporary
bridge, it is a legitimate tool. Used without an exit plan, the cost
can compound.

90. Is a B-lender better than a private lender?
For most borrowers who can qualify, yes. B-lenders are typically
cheaper, more structured, and run longer terms than private lenders.
Private lending is reserved for files that even B-lenders cannot
approve.

91. How does the broker decide between A-lender, B-lender, and private lender?
The broker reviews credit, income, equity, property, timeline, debt
level, purpose of funds, and exit strategy. The goal is to place the
borrower with the lowest-cost suitable lender they can reasonably
qualify for.

92. What happens if I miss a mortgage payment with a B-lender?
NSF fees apply, and repeated missed payments jeopardize the chance of
renewal at term end. Persistent default can lead to formal
enforcement just like with any other lender.

## Category 13: Additional borrower questions

93. Will a B-lender consider me if I am on parental leave?
Possibly. The lender may use return-to-work income (with a letter
confirming return date) plus current parental benefits. Some lenders
are stricter with parental leave files than others.

94. Can a B-lender approve me while I am on disability?
Possibly. Disability income from credible sources (long-term
disability insurance, WSIB, CPP-D) can count as qualifying income if
documented properly and shown as recurring.

95. Can I use a B-lender if I just immigrated to Canada?
Yes. Many B-lenders work with newcomers, often using international
credit reports, employment letters, larger down payments, or
alternative documentation when Canadian credit is limited.

96. What if I am paid in cryptocurrency or US dollars?
Foreign-currency or crypto income is difficult. Lenders generally want
income converted to and consistently deposited as Canadian dollars,
verifiable through Canadian bank statements. Discuss the specifics
with the broker.

97. Can I get a B-mortgage if I am separated but not divorced?
Yes. The lender will want to see the separation agreement (if
finalized) and the financial split. Spousal income may or may not be
considered depending on the file structure.

98. Will being in a common-law relationship affect my B-lender application?
Both partners may need to be on the application depending on the
property structure and lender. Common-law status itself does not
disqualify; the lender treats it like any spousal arrangement.

99. Can my parents co-sign a B-lender mortgage with me?
Yes. A co-signing parent with strong credit and income can boost
approval odds and possibly lower the rate. The co-signer is jointly
liable for the mortgage.

100. Do B-lenders accept gifted equity from a parent's home?
Some do, structured as a gift from family. The gift letter must
confirm the funds are non-repayable and the source of the gift must
be documented.

101. What is the difference between a Schedule I bank and a B-lender?
Schedule I banks are the major Canadian banks (the Big Six). B-lenders
include trust companies, certain credit unions, and specialty
alternative lenders that operate with more flexible criteria.

102. How is interest calculated on a B-lender mortgage?
Most Canadian mortgages, including B-lender ones, are calculated
semi-annually, not in advance. The agreement specifies the
calculation method.

103. Do B-lenders use compound or simple interest?
Canadian residential mortgages typically compound interest
semi-annually under standard market practice. The lender's commitment
spells out the precise calculation.

104. Can my B-lender mortgage payments be deducted automatically?
Yes. Most B-lenders require automatic pre-authorized debit (PAD)
from a borrower's bank account to ensure on-time payment.

105. Can the B-lender include closing costs in the mortgage?
Generally no. Closing costs (legal, appraisal, lender fee, land
transfer tax) must be paid from separate funds at closing. Some
lenders allow part of the lender fee to be deducted from the loan
advance.

106. Can the B-lender help with closing costs?
Indirectly. By rolling the lender fee into the mortgage deduction,
the borrower's out-of-pocket closing burden is reduced, but they
still must pay legal, appraisal, and land transfer tax separately.

107. What if I lose my job during a B-lender term?
Job loss does not automatically default the mortgage as long as
payments continue. If income loss makes payments impossible, contact
the broker immediately to discuss options, including potential
restructuring or earlier transition.

108. Will the B-lender want me to use a specific lawyer?
The lender requires you to use a real estate lawyer in good standing,
but the borrower generally chooses their own lawyer. The lender's
solicitor handles the lender's side separately.

109. Will I need home insurance for my B-lender mortgage?
Yes. All lenders require home insurance maintained throughout the
mortgage term, with the lender named as loss payee. Lapsed insurance
is a default condition.

110. Can I get a B-lender mortgage on a property with a basement apartment?
Yes. The lender treats it as a 1-unit or 2-unit property depending on
how the basement apartment is registered. Rental income from the
basement may be partially counted toward qualifying income.

111. Will the B-lender count basement apartment rent as income?
Often yes, partially. Many lenders count 50% of the gross rental
income or 100% of net rental income depending on policy.

112. Can a B-lender mortgage be assumed by a new buyer?
Some B-lender mortgages are assumable, meaning a buyer can take over
the existing mortgage. The lender must approve the assumption based
on the new buyer's credit and income.

113. What is a portability feature in a mortgage?
Portability lets the borrower transfer their existing mortgage to a
new property, keeping the same rate and terms. Some B-lenders offer
this; many do not. Confirm with the broker before purchasing.

114. Can I port my B-lender mortgage to a new property?
Possibly. Portability depends on the specific lender's product. If
not available, the borrower may face a penalty to break the mortgage
when buying a new home.

115. What happens to my B-lender mortgage if I die during the term?
The mortgage becomes a debt of the estate. The executor pays it out
from estate proceeds, sells the property, or refinances. Life
insurance, if separately arranged, may cover the balance.

116. Will I need mortgage life insurance for a B-lender mortgage?
Mortgage life insurance is offered but not required. It pays out the
mortgage balance if the borrower dies. Many financial advisors
recommend term life insurance instead, which is portable and often
cheaper.

117. Can I have two B-lender mortgages at the same time?
On different properties, yes. On the same property, only one
first-position mortgage exists; a second mortgage would be a separate
charge behind the first.

118. Can I apply with multiple B-lenders at once to compare offers?
Yes, and this is standard practice when working with a mortgage
broker. The broker manages the applications to minimize duplicate
credit pulls.

119. How many credit pulls happen during a B-lender application?
Typically one hard credit pull per lender, but a mortgage broker often
uses a single shared credit report across multiple lender
submissions when possible to reduce credit impact.

120. Will multiple credit inquiries hurt my credit score?
Multiple mortgage-related inquiries within a short window (typically
14 to 45 days) are usually treated as a single inquiry by credit
scoring models, so the impact is minimized. Outside that window,
multiple inquiries can drag the score down.

121. Can I cancel my B-lender mortgage application after approval?
Usually yes, before closing. After signing the mortgage commitment
and funding, you cannot cancel without breaking the mortgage and
paying the prepayment penalty.

122. Can I cancel my B-lender mortgage after I sign?
Once funds advance, you cannot cancel. You can pay it out early, but
that triggers the prepayment penalty (typically three months interest
or IRD). Before funding, conditions and the lender's commitment
specify what cancellation rights exist.

123. Is there a cooling-off period for B-lender mortgages?
Mortgage agreements in Canada generally do not have a statutory
cooling-off period after signing. Conditions in the commitment must
be satisfied to fund, but once funded, the mortgage is binding.

124. Can I dispute my appraisal value with a B-lender?
You can ask the lender to consider a second appraisal at your
expense, especially if you believe the first appraisal missed
comparable sales or has errors. The lender decides whether to
reconsider.

125. How does a B-lender determine if my property is sellable?
The appraisal includes a marketability assessment: comparable
recent sales, time on market in the area, property condition,
location desirability, and any unique features (good or bad) that
affect resale. Less marketable properties may face lower LTV caps.

126. Is my B-lender file kept confidential?
Yes, subject to privacy laws. Lenders share information only with
parties involved in the transaction (your broker, lawyer, appraiser)
and with credit bureaus where required.

127. Will my bank know I went to a B-lender?
Generally only if your B-lender reports to the credit bureau (which
most do), in which case the new mortgage appears on your credit
report and could be visible to other lenders.

128. What happens if my B-lender goes out of business during my term?
The mortgage agreement survives. Your loan would be assigned to
another lender or administered by a trustee, with no change to your
payment terms. Your obligations continue under the existing contract.
