# Self-Employed Mortgages (Business-For-Self): Frequently Asked Questions

Plain-language Q&A reference for the kinds of questions self-employed
borrowers, freelancers, contractors, and incorporated business owners
ask about getting a mortgage. Synthesized from two AI-generated
sources (Gemini and ChatGPT), deduplicated semantically, plus 50
additional borrower-facing questions added. Significant overlap with
`cmhc-self-employed-program.md` (CMHC overlay file) and
`alt-lending-faqs.md` is intentional; this file pulls the
self-employed angle together for borrower-facing questions.

## How the agent should use this file

These Q&As give the agent background context for self-employed
mortgage conversations. Typical thresholds noted (24-month operating
history standard, 15% gross-up for sole proprietors, 0.5-1.25% rate
premium on alt programs, 1% B-lender fee, 12-24 month bank statement
programs) are general market context, not absolute rules. Real
lender policies vary.

The CMHC base file (`jurisdictions/CA/cmhc-mortgage-loan-insurance.md`)
and CMHC self-employed overlay (`jurisdictions/CA/cmhc-self-employed-program.md`)
have the canonical CMHC program detail. This file is the borrower-
facing Q&A complement.

## Category 1: Self-Employed Mortgage Fundamentals

1. Can self-employed individuals qualify for a mortgage in Ontario?
Yes. Self-employed Canadians, freelancers, independent contractors,
and business owners fully qualify for mortgages. Lenders use
specific underwriting tracks designed around entrepreneurial income.

2. What is a Business-For-Self (BFS) mortgage program?
A specialized mortgage framework that looks beyond traditional T4
paystubs. BFS evaluates an entrepreneur's financial health by
examining gross revenue, cash flow, business bank statements, and
industry reasonability rather than just net taxable income.

3. Why is it harder to get a mortgage when self-employed?
Income can fluctuate, and business owners typically reduce taxable
income through deductions. Lenders need to understand income
clearly enough to decide whether the mortgage is affordable, and
heavy deductions can leave reported income too low for standard
qualification.

4. Why do traditional banks reject so many self-employed applicants who make great money?
Accountants design business tax structures to minimize personal
income tax by maximizing write-offs. While this reduces tax bills,
it also reduces the reported net income on paper that traditional
banks use for qualification. Strong cash flow gets buried behind
paper deductions.

5. What counts as self-employed income?
Income from sole proprietorships, partnerships, corporations,
contract work, freelance work, commission-based business, or
professional practice. The lender reviews how the income is earned
and how consistently received.

6. How long do I need to be self-employed to qualify for a bank mortgage?
Traditional A-lenders strictly require a minimum of 2 full years of
documented self-employment history. With less than 24 months,
you'll typically need to pivot to alternative B-lenders or private
lenders.

7. Are exceptions made for businesses operating less than 2 years?
Yes. Lenders may consider 12-24 months if you transitioned into an
identical role in the same industry, have proven prior employment
in the field, and carry strong personal credit.

8. Does it matter if I operate as a sole proprietor or am incorporated?
Yes. Sole proprietors qualify based on personal tax filings and net
business income. Incorporated owners can qualify using T4 salary,
corporate dividends, or retained earnings depending on the lender
and program.

9. What credit score do self-employed borrowers need?
For prime A-lenders: 680+ preferred. For Alt-A programs: 650+.
For B-lenders: down to 600 or even 580 with strong compensating
factors.

10. What are the maximum debt service ratios for self-employed at a major bank?
Traditional A-lenders enforce GDS at 39% and TDS at 44%, same as
salaried borrowers.

11. Do alternative B-lenders allow more flexible debt ratios?
Yes. B-lenders (like Equitable Bank) routinely allow GDS/TDS up to
50%/50%, and up to 60%/60% under specialized extended ratio
programs in select urban markets.

## Category 2: Documentation

12. What documents do self-employed borrowers need?
Common: T1 Generals, Notices of Assessment (NOAs), business
financial statements, business bank statements, articles of
incorporation, GST/HST returns, invoices, contracts, deposit
history. Exact list depends on the lender and program.

13. What is a Notice of Assessment (NOA) and why is it vital?
A Notice of Assessment is the CRA document confirming details of
your filed tax return. Lenders use NOAs to confirm income, check
that taxes are filed and current, and verify Line 15000 (Total
Income) for qualification math.

14. What is Line 15000 on my NOA?
Line 15000 (formerly Line 150) shows your Total Income declared to
CRA. Traditional bank underwriters use this specific box as the base
starting point for primary mortgage qualification.

15. What is a T1 General?
The personal income tax return. Self-employed borrowers provide T1
Generals so the lender reviews total income, business income,
deductions, and history.

16. What is a T2125?
The Statement of Business or Professional Activities used by sole
proprietors to report business income and expenses. CMHC's
Self-Employed program lists T2125 + NOA as one acceptable
documentation path.

17. What is a T2 Corporate Tax Return?
The corporate tax return filed by incorporated businesses. Lenders
reviewing retained earnings or corporate cash flow may request the
T2.

18. Will lenders check if I owe CRA?
Yes. Lenders routinely review NOAs to see if taxes are filed and
whether amounts are owing. CRA debt complicates the file
significantly.

19. Do I need to be up to date with my tax filings?
For traditional A-lender mortgages, yes. Banks require the last 2
years of NOAs fully processed by CRA before they'll review the
file.

20. Will lenders look at my business bank statements?
For self-employed bank-statement programs (Alt-A), yes, very
closely. They review 12-24 months of statements for deposit
consistency, expense reasonableness, NSF flags, and overall cash
flow patterns.

21. What if I haven't separated personal and business bank accounts?
Co-mingling complicates underwriting. Alternative underwriters need
clear deposit trails. Having dedicated business accounts allows
faster, cleaner qualification. If you're planning to apply within
1-2 years, separate accounts now.

## Category 3: Traditional vs Alternative vs Stated Income Tracks

22. What is the traditional self-employed qualification track?
Full documentation verifying taxed net income. Lender reviews the
last 2 years of personal tax filings and bases borrowing power on
the net profit declared to CRA.

23. What is the "Bank Statement" or "Alt-A" program?
Focuses on operational cash flow instead of tax returns.
Alternative lenders analyze 12-24 months of business bank
statements, calculating qualifying income from total deposits using
a standard percentage.

24. What is the minimum down payment for a bank statement program?
Because bank statement programs are non-prime alternative
solutions, they don't qualify for default insurance. Minimum 20%
down (80% LTV or less).

25. Can I get an alternative self-employed mortgage on a rental property using bank statements?
Yes. Many alternative lenders finance residential investment
properties for business owners, evaluating standalone rental cash
flow alongside business deposits.

26. Are alternative B-lender rates higher for self-employed?
Yes. Non-traditional verification carries a rate premium of about
0.50% to 1.25% above prime bank rates.

27. Is there an upfront fee for a bank statement mortgage?
Yes, virtually all alternative self-employed products carry a one-
time 1% lender fee, deducted from the loan advance at closing.

28. What is a "stated income" mortgage?
The borrower states or declares their reasonable annual income
based on industry averages, rather than proving it through tax
assessments. The stated amount must match the size and revenue of
the business operation.

29. Can self-employed borrowers buy with less than 20% down using stated income?
Yes. Private mortgage insurers (Sagen and Canada Guaranty) offer
insured Stated Income programs allowing business owners to purchase
owner-occupied homes with as little as 10% down (up to 90% LTV).

30. What is CMHC's Self-Employed program?
A CMHC mortgage insurance program for self-employed borrowers that
allows high-ratio insurance with as little as 5% down for homes
under $500,000. Requires full 2-year traditional T1 General and NOA
documentation. See `cmhc-self-employed-program.md`.

31. What is Sagen's Business for Self (Alt-A) program?
A high-ratio insurance program from Sagen for entrepreneurs to
purchase a primary residence with up to 90% LTV, using alternative
documentation (client contracts, business financials) to validate
income reasonability.

32. What is Canada Guaranty's Low Doc Advantage program?
Built for self-employed buyers with strong credit but lower
reported tax income. Uses the gross revenue of the business and
your Line 15000 history to confirm declared income reasonability.

33. Is there a maximum purchase price for high-ratio insured self-employed mortgages?
Yes. Any mortgage with less than 20% down (high-ratio insured) is
capped at less than $1.5 million purchase price.

34. Are default insurance premiums higher for stated income mortgages?
For standard proof of income: standard premiums. For alternative
"without traditional proof" stated programs: a surcharge applies
reflecting the higher risk.

35. What is "Reasonability Confirmation" in stated income?
The underwriter reviews your business type and checks if claimed
income matches economic reality. Stating $150K on a $180K gross
solo design business would be flagged as unreasonable due to
necessary operating overhead.

36. What is a Declaration of Income (DOI) form?
A legally binding document from the lender where you formally state
your business earnings under signature, acknowledging that
misrepresenting the numbers constitutes mortgage fraud.

37. Can I use stated income for an equity refinance?
Most institutional stated income programs are restricted to new
purchases only. Equity-takeout refinances with alternative
documentation typically run through B-lenders at 80% LTV.

## Category 4: Add-Backs and Gross-Up

38. What is the 15% gross-up rule for sole proprietors?
Recognizing that sole proprietors deduct extensive business
expenses, CMHC and traditional A-lenders allow brokers to gross up
the Net Income on tax returns by 15% to expand qualifying room.

39. What are "add-backs"?
Specific non-cash business deductions that can legally be added
back to net tax income to raise qualifying income. Eligible add-
backs are limited to: business-use-of-home, motor-vehicle expenses,
and capital cost allowance (CCA) under CMHC rules. Other deductions
cannot be added back.

40. How does Capital Cost Allowance help my application?
CCA is a depreciation write-off. Because it's a paper deduction
that didn't actually drain cash from your bank account, lenders
allow you to add the full CCA amount back to your qualifying
income.

41. Can I use motor vehicle expenses as an add-back?
Yes, for sole proprietors using the add-back path under CMHC's
Self-Employed program. The vehicle deduction is recognized as a
business expense that doesn't reduce true personal earning power.

42. Can I use business-use-of-home as an add-back?
Yes. Like motor vehicle expenses, the business-use-of-home
deduction is recognized as eligible for add-back to qualifying
income.

43. Can I add back any business deduction?
No. Only the three eligible categories: business-use-of-home, motor
vehicle, and capital cost allowance. Other deductions (advertising,
travel, supplies, etc.) cannot be added back.

44. Can I use gross business revenue to qualify?
Usually no, at least not directly. Lenders want net income or
documented cash flow. Bank statement programs come closest to
"gross deposit" qualification but apply a standard percentage to
arrive at qualifying income.

## Category 5: Business Structures

45. Can I qualify if I'm incorporated?
Yes. The lender reviews corporate financial statements, articles of
incorporation, T2 corporate returns, business bank statements, and
how you draw income (salary, dividends, retained earnings).

46. Can I use retained earnings to qualify?
Yes, with the right lender. Progressive alternative lenders analyze
T2 returns and allow your percentage ownership in net corporate
retained earnings to factor into personal debt-servicing ratios.

47. What documents do I need to use retained earnings?
The last 2 years of complete Corporate Financial Statements
(compiled by a CPA via Review Engagement Report) along with T2
Corporate Tax Returns.

48. Can I use my corporate business account to hold my down payment?
Yes. When extracting down payment funds from a corporate account,
you must present an accountant's note proving the withdrawal won't
disrupt business operational health.

49. Can I qualify if I pay myself dividends only?
Yes. Lenders review dividend income through your T1, NOAs, and
corporate financials. Some lenders accept dividends fully; others
prefer salary-based payment.

50. Can I qualify if I pay myself a salary from my corporation?
Yes. T4 salary from your corporation is treated like employment
income, simpler for many lenders than dividend-based qualification.

51. What is the "Income Smoothing" technique?
An incorporated owner pays themselves a consistent, identical
monthly T4 salary for 24 months before mortgage shopping, preventing
erratic revenue spikes from disrupting bank algorithms.

52. Can I qualify as a sole proprietor?
Yes. Sole proprietors use T1 Generals, T2125 forms, NOAs, business
bank statements, invoices, and contracts.

53. How does being in a partnership work?
Lenders review the partnership agreement to verify your ownership
percentage, applying that pro-rated share of partnership revenue or
cash flow to your file.

## Category 6: Income Calculation

54. How do lenders calculate qualifying income when revenue fluctuates?
If income is stable or increasing: 2-year mathematical average. If
income dropped significantly in the most recent year: lenders use
only the lower year to calculate risk.

55. What if my income went down last year?
The decline makes qualification harder. The lender may use only
the lower year, want explanation of the drop, and may want
evidence that current income is stable.

56. What if my income went up this year?
Higher current income helps but lenders often rely on filed tax
income, so a single high year doesn't fully count. Bank statement
programs that look at recent deposits can capture the increase
better.

57. Can I use projected income?
Usually no alone. Lenders may review projections as context but
prefer proven income history. Bank statement programs can show
recent improving trends through deposits.

58. Can I use cash income that isn't reported?
No. Unreported cash income cannot be used. Lenders want
verifiable income. Cash earnings need to flow through bank
accounts to be documented.

59. Can I qualify with commission income?
Yes. Commission income requires a history (typically 2 years),
supported by tax documents, T4As, employment contracts, or bank
deposits.

60. Can I qualify with contract work?
Yes. Contract workers may qualify if income is stable and
documented through contracts, bank deposits, tax filings, and
length of time in the field.

61. Can I qualify if I work in the gig economy (Uber, freelance platforms)?
Yes. Gig economy workers are classified as self-employed BFS
earners. With 12-24 months of consistent, recurring platform
deposits, you can qualify under alternative programs.

62. How do lenders treat seasonal self-employment (landscaping, roofing)?
Lenders welcome seasonal income provided you display a 3+ year
history of consistent recurring revenue during those specific
months, backed by stable off-season cash reserves.

63. Can I qualify if I just started my business?
Possibly, but harder. The lender reviews prior industry experience,
contracts, deposits, down payment, credit, and business stability.
Sub-12 months almost always means alternative or private lenders.

64. Can I qualify if I bought an existing business?
Possibly. The lender reviews the business purchase agreement,
historical financials, your experience, current income, and
business bank statements.

65. Can I qualify if I recently incorporated?
Yes. If you transitioned the same business from sole proprietorship
to corporation, lenders treat the history as continuous, combining
tax histories across both structures.

## Category 7: Down Payment and LTV

66. Can self-employed borrowers buy with 5% down?
Possibly, through CMHC's Self-Employed program with full
traditional documentation (2-year NOAs etc.). Tiered down payment
rules apply: 5% on first $500K, 10% on amount above.

67. Can self-employed borrowers buy with 10% down?
Yes, through Sagen's BFS or Canada Guaranty's Low Doc programs
that allow alternative documentation up to 90% LTV (10% down).
Possibly with a stated income surcharge.

68. Can self-employed borrowers buy with 20% down?
Yes. With 20% down the mortgage is conventional, opening up B-
lender and bank statement programs that don't require insurer-
acceptable documentation.

69. What if I have 35% down and complex self-employed income?
Excellent position. The lower LTV opens conventional alternative
programs that don't need insurer approval, with much more
flexibility on income documentation.

## Category 8: Specific Scenarios

70. Can a self-employed borrower add a T4 co-signer to boost approval?
Yes. Blending self-employed cash-flow with a T4 salaried co-signer
(spouse or parent) creates a hybrid profile that lowers
underwriting risk and improves approval odds.

71. Can rental income help if I'm self-employed?
Yes. Rental income (from another property or a suite in the
subject property) can support qualification, though lenders may
only use part of the rent and will review property expenses.

72. Can investment income help?
Yes if stable and recurring. Dividend income from non-business
investments, RRIF withdrawals, or other documented investment
streams can support qualification.

73. Can my spouse's employment income help?
Yes. Joint application includes both incomes. Their salaried
employment can offset some of the documentation challenges of your
self-employed file.

74. Will a recent equipment or vehicle purchase hurt my application?
Yes. New commercial truck or machinery on credit adds monthly loan
obligations to your corporate or personal credit files, inflating
TDS and shrinking mortgage space. Avoid major credit purchases
before applying.

75. Can I get a mortgage if I owe CRA back taxes?
Standard banks reject the file. Alternative B-lenders and private
sources approve on the condition that CRA tax arrears are paid in
full from the mortgage advance at closing.

76. What if I have unpaid GST/HST?
Treated like CRA debt. Banks reject; alternative/private require
payment at closing. Self-employed borrowers often have GST/HST
exposure, so this is a common scenario.

77. Can I refinance my home to fund my business?
Yes. Pulling equity from your home for business capital is a
common use of self-employed refinance. Interest on the portion used
for business may be tax-deductible (consult an accountant).

78. Can I get a HELOC if I'm self-employed?
Yes if you qualify. The lender reviews income documentation,
credit, property value, mortgage balance, debts, and equity.
Self-employed HELOC applications often require stronger
documentation than salaried.

79. Can I refinance with stated income?
Some alternative lenders accept stated income for refinances at
80% LTV. Bank statement and B-lender refinances are more common
than insured stated income refinances.

80. Can I get construction financing as a self-employed borrower?
Yes, but the lender requires income documentation. Construction
loans are riskier, so lenders are stricter about income proof for
self-employed than for salaried construction borrowers.

## Category 9: Troubleshooting

81. What if my bank rejected my self-employed application because of low net income?
Don't panic and don't alter your tax returns. Have your broker
redirect the file to an Alternative Bank Statement program or a
Private Equity Loan, which assess gross cash flow rather than net
tax boxes.

82. Why are business bank statements inspected so closely?
Underwriters check for operational reasonability. They verify
deposits match revenue claims, scan for NSF flags or overlapping
payday advances, confirm expenses align with the industry, and
look for cash flow gaps that would suggest the business is
struggling.

83. What is an NOA verification delay?
If your broker submits the file right around tax season, lenders
demand the newest year's NOA. If CRA hasn't processed your return,
it creates an approval bottleneck. Solution: file early or wait
for processing.

84. Can newly self-employed borrowers (under 12 months) get a bank mortgage?
Usually no. Sub-12 month self-employment almost always goes to
alternative or private. Bank statement programs typically need 6-12
months of operational history; full A-lender programs need 24
months.

85. What if I transitioned from employment to self-employed mid-term?
Hard to switch lenders during the term while transitioning. Most
A-lenders want 2 years of self-employed income. Best strategy:
renew with your current lender (who doesn't re-verify income
deeply) until you have 2 years of self-employed history.

86. Do private mortgage lenders care about business financials?
No, largely. Private lenders are asset-based and bypass income
documents. They base risk on the property's equity and the
borrower's exit strategy back to bank or B-lender financing.

87. How does a business plan help my mortgage application?
For newer businesses (12-18 months) or expanding into a new
revenue stream, a concise executive business plan with locked-in
client contracts and projected revenues helps underwriters justify
exceptions to standard policy.

88. Can I use my business assets as collateral for a personal mortgage?
Generally no in the mortgage context. Mortgages are secured against
the property only. Business assets are a separate collateral
category for other types of financing.

89. What if my CRA debt is too large to clear at closing?
Then the file likely doesn't work, even at private. The CRA debt
must be cleared. Alternative: partial payment plan with CRA, time
to pay it down, then re-apply.

## Category 10: Additional borrower questions

90. Should I file my taxes a certain way to make mortgage qualifying easier?
Plan with your accountant 24+ months ahead of buying. Many self-
employed borrowers reduce their write-offs in the 1-2 years before
applying so their reported income better supports the mortgage.
This means more tax paid but easier mortgage approval.

91. Can I qualify based on my future income from a signed contract?
Sometimes, especially for B-lenders or alternative programs. A
signed multi-year contract with predictable revenue can support
qualification, especially when combined with past business history.

92. How do mortgage brokers help self-employed borrowers?
Brokers know which lenders accept which income documentation, which
B-lender programs match which file types, and how to structure the
application to show maximum qualifying income legally. For
self-employed files, broker expertise is high-value.

93. Can I claim my mortgage interest as a business expense if I work from home?
The home office portion of the interest may be deductible if you
have a dedicated business-use-of-home space. Consult an accountant.

94. Will incorporating help me get a mortgage faster?
Not necessarily. Incorporation has tax benefits but doesn't speed
mortgage qualification. The new corporation needs 2 years of
history for traditional bank programs.

95. Does my industry affect mortgage qualification?
Sometimes. Stable, traditional industries (medical, accounting,
legal) qualify easily. Volatile or seasonal industries (real
estate, hospitality, certain trades) may face more scrutiny.

96. Can I use my Notice of Assessment to skip providing tax returns?
Usually no. Most lenders want both the T1 General AND the NOA. The
NOA confirms what CRA processed; the T1 shows what you filed
including the business details.

97. What if my accountant hasn't finished my taxes for last year?
You usually need to wait until taxes are filed and assessed (NOA
received) before applying. A few alternative lenders may use
draft financials, but most want CRA-processed numbers.

98. Can I get a mortgage if I'm in the process of changing my business structure?
Possibly. The lender wants stability and clear history. Changes
mid-application can pause the file. Best to complete the structure
change, build 6-12 months of history under the new structure, then
apply.

99. What information should I prepare for a self-employed mortgage review?
Last 2 years of T1 Generals and NOAs, last 2 years of business
financials (if incorporated), 6-12 months of business bank
statements, articles of incorporation (if applicable), business
license, current contracts, recent invoices, credit information,
down payment source documentation, property details if buying.

100. Can a mortgage broker help me even if I'm complex self-employed?
Yes, especially. Brokers specialize in matching complex files to
the right lender. The harder your file (multiple income streams,
incorporated, recent transitions, tax debt), the more value the
broker provides.

101. Should I consult my accountant before applying?
Yes. Your accountant can clarify what income shows on your filed
returns, what add-backs are possible, and whether to adjust filing
strategy 12-24 months before applying.

102. Should I consult a financial planner?
Helpful but not required. A planner can model whether the mortgage
fits your broader financial plan, considering business income
stability, retirement goals, and tax efficiency.

## What this corpus file is not

- It is not a substitute for `cmhc-self-employed-program.md` (CMHC
  Self-Employed program overlay) or `cmhc-mortgage-loan-insurance.md`
  (base CMHC file) which describe the program rules in detail.
- It is not a substitute for `alt-lending-faqs.md` which has
  significant overlap on self-employed B-lender questions.
- It is not legal, tax, or accounting advice for any specific
  borrower's situation.
- It is not a guarantee of approval; every file is reviewed on its
  own merits.
- The typical thresholds noted (rate premiums, lender fees, history
  requirements, gross-up percentages, add-back eligibility) are
  market context as of the authoring date, not absolute rules.
- It is not a guide to specific accounting strategies; consult an
  accountant for tax filing decisions before applying for a
  mortgage.
