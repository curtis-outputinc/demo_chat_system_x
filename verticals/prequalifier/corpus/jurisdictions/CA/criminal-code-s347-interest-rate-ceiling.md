# Criminal Code Section 347: Criminal interest rate ceiling (Federal Canada)

Section 347 of the Criminal Code of Canada sets the upper legal limit
on interest rates for any loan in Canada, including private mortgages.
It is the legal ceiling above which charging interest becomes a
criminal offence.

This is the practical upper bound on what any lender — A-side, alt,
private, or otherwise — can lawfully charge once all fees are
accounted for. It matters most for private and alternative lending,
where lender fees, broker fees, and rates stack quickly enough to
approach the ceiling.

## Source URL

- https://laws-lois.justice.gc.ca/eng/acts/c-46/section-347.html

## The current criminal interest rate

- 35% effective annual rate.
- Defined in the statute as "an annual percentage rate of interest
  calculated in accordance with generally accepted actuarial
  practices and principles that exceeds 35 per cent on the credit
  advanced."
- This is a CALENDAR-YEAR EFFECTIVE rate, not a simple stated APR.
- Was previously a higher ceiling (60% effective annual rate); the
  current 35% threshold reflects the lowered rate after recent
  amendments. The statute was last amended March 26, 2026.

## What counts toward the 35% ceiling

The statute's definition of "interest" is intentionally broad. It
includes the aggregate of all charges and expenses paid or payable
for the advancing of credit, whether in the form of:

- Interest itself.
- Fees.
- Fines.
- Penalties.
- Commissions.
- Other similar charges or expenses, by any name.

Practical implication for private and alt lending: the effective rate
must include the lender fee, the broker fee, and any setup or other
charges, all rolled together with the contract interest rate.

## What is excluded from the calculation

The following are NOT counted toward the 35% ceiling:

- Repayment of the credit advanced itself.
- Insurance charges, provided the face amount of the insurance does
  not exceed the credit advanced.
- Official fees required by law to perfect security (e.g. registration
  fees a borrower would pay regardless of the lender).
- Overdraft charges up to a maximum of $5 (for credit unions and
  deposit-taking institutions).
- Required deposit balances.
- Property taxes (in mortgage and hypothec transactions specifically).

## Mortgage-specific treatment

Mortgages get two carve-outs:

- Property taxes are excluded from the interest calculation.
- Required deposit balances are excluded.

Otherwise, mortgages fall under the same 35% ceiling as any other
credit transaction.

## Penalties for violation

Charging an effective rate above 35% is a criminal offence,
prosecutable as either:

- Indictable offence: up to 5 years' imprisonment.
- Summary conviction: maximum $25,000 fine or up to 2 years less one
  day imprisonment, or both.

## Why this matters for the prequalifier

For the conventional and alt buckets, the s.347 ceiling is almost
never relevant. Rates plus fees rarely approach 35% effective.

For the private bucket, it can become relevant. Private mortgage
files commonly carry a combination of:

- A higher contract rate than A or alt.
- A lender fee (often 1-3% of the loan).
- A broker fee.
- Sometimes additional setup or holdback fees.

When those are aggregated and amortized into the effective annual
rate, a private file can approach the ceiling on shorter-term, lower
LTV files where the fees are large relative to the principal. The
broker has a responsibility to keep the effective rate below 35%; the
prequalifier does not need to do this calculation, but the agent
should be aware that the ceiling exists.

The agent should not quote the s.347 ceiling as a rate to expect —
real private rates are typically well below it. The bot's only use
for the rule is to acknowledge, if a borrower asks "how high can the
rate go," that there is a legal ceiling and the broker handles the
specifics on the call.

## What this corpus file is not

- It is not a guide to current private mortgage rates or fees.
- It is not legal advice on whether a specific transaction breaches
  s.347.
- It is not provincial usury or consumer-protection law (some
  provinces layer additional consumer protection on top of s.347;
  those live in province-specific corpus files).
- It is not a description of payday-loan law (payday loans have their
  own separate regulatory regime under provincial law).
