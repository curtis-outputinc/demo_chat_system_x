# OSFI Guideline B-20: Residential Mortgage Underwriting (Federal Canada)

This is the foundational federal Canadian regulation governing residential
mortgage underwriting. It is issued by OSFI (Office of the Superintendent
of Financial Institutions). The assistant should treat B-20 as the
authoritative reference for how A-lenders in Canada underwrite, and as the
practical reference most alternative lenders mirror.

## What B-20 is

- Federal Canadian guideline setting out OSFI's expectations for prudent
  residential mortgage underwriting.
- Covers all federally-regulated financial institutions (FRFIs) engaged in
  residential mortgage underwriting or in acquiring residential mortgage
  loan assets in Canada.
- A "residential mortgage" under B-20 means any loan to an individual
  secured by residential property of one to four units.
- Home equity lines of credit (HELOCs), equity loans, and other products
  that use residential property as security are also covered.
- Built around five fundamental principles: governance, borrower
  identity / willingness to pay, borrower capacity to pay, property
  collateral, and credit / counterparty risk management.

## Who B-20 binds, and who it does NOT bind

- B-20 binds FRFIs: the major banks, trust and loan companies, and
  federally-incorporated insurance companies.
- B-20 does NOT directly bind provincially-regulated lenders, most MICs,
  or private lenders. Those lenders set their own underwriting standards,
  though many mirror B-20 as a practical reference.
- This scope distinction matters for the prequalifier's bucket read:
  conventional A-lender files are evaluated against B-20-level standards;
  alt-lender and private files often relax one or more B-20 expectations
  in exchange for higher rates, lender fees, and shorter terms.

## Credit decision and underwriting framework

- The borrower's demonstrated willingness and capacity to service the
  debt should be the primary basis of the credit decision.
- Undue reliance on collateral is discouraged. Realizing on collateral is
  difficult for the borrower and costly to the lender.
- The three borrower-and-property principles (identity, capacity,
  collateral) are evaluated holistically and on a risk-based basis.

## Principle 2: borrower identity, background, and willingness to pay

### Background and credit history

- Lenders should make a reasonable enquiry into the prospective
  borrower's background, credit history, and borrowing behaviour.
- A credit bureau score is an indicator commonly used to support credit
  granting, but should not be the sole basis of qualification.
- A credit score measures past behaviour and does not immediately
  incorporate changes in financial condition or current willingness to
  pay.
- Borrower consent for the assessment must be obtained, and the lender
  must comply with provincial and federal privacy legislation (e.g.
  PIPEDA).

### Loan documentation expected

- Description of the purpose of the loan (purchase, refinance, type of
  property and occupancy).
- Employment status and income verification (Principle 3).
- Debt service ratio calculations with verification documentation for
  key inputs (heating, taxes, other debt obligations).
- LTV ratio, property valuation, and appraisal documentation (Principle
  4).
- Credit bureau reports and other credit enquiries.
- Documentation verifying the source of the down payment.
- Purchase and sale agreements and other collateral supporting
  documents.
- Explanation of any mitigating criteria for higher credit risk factors.
- Property insurance agreements.
- Clearly stated rationale for the credit decision, including
  exceptions.
- Where applicable, a record from the mortgage insurer validating
  commitment to insure.
- An independent third party should be able to replicate the credit
  decision from the documented underwriting file.

### Purpose of the mortgage loan

The lender should document:

- Intended use of the loan: purchase, refinancing, renewal, equity
  takeout.
- Type of purchase: owner-occupied primary residence, second or
  recreational property, investment property, or a property that relies
  on rental income to service the loan.
- Type of refinance: debt consolidation, change of existing loan
  characteristics, access to home equity, renovation, and so on.

### Anti-money-laundering and anti-terrorist-financing

- Residential mortgages are subject to the Proceeds of Crime (Money
  Laundering) and Terrorist Financing Act and Regulations.
- Lenders must comply with customer identification and record-keeping
  requirements and assess whether a customer is a higher-risk customer.
- If a lender suspects illicit purposes, it should decline the loan and
  consider filing a suspicious transaction report to FINTRAC.

### Misrepresentation

- Lenders should detect, prevent, and report fraud and misrepresentation
  in the underwriting process (e.g. falsified income documents).
- Suspected or confirmed fraud on insured mortgages should be reported
  to the relevant mortgage insurer.

## Principle 3: borrower capacity to pay

### Income verification

- Income verification should be rigorous because income is the key
  capacity factor and verification deters fraud.
- Lenders should substantiate the borrower's employment status and
  income history.
- Documentation should: be verified by an independent source, be
  difficult to falsify, directly address the declared income amount, and
  not contradict other underwriting information.
- Income assessments should reflect the stability of the income,
  including possible negative outcomes (e.g. salary variability).
- Temporarily high income (overtime, irregular commissions, bonuses)
  should be normalized or discounted.
- Self-employed borrowers should provide proof of income such as the
  Notice of Assessment and T1 General, plus relevant business
  documentation.
- Loans materially dependent on rental income from the property require
  rigorous due diligence on that income.
- Income from sources outside Canada is harder to verify; lenders should
  conduct thorough due diligence, and unverifiable income should be
  treated cautiously when assessing capacity.

### Guarantors and co-signors

- A guarantor or co-signor must be assessed with the same rigour as a
  primary borrower, commensurate with how much support the file relies
  on from them.
- The guarantor / co-signor must fully understand their legal
  obligations.

### Debt service coverage: GDS and TDS

Two standard ratios assess affordability:

- GDS (Gross Debt Service): the share of gross income required for
  housing costs.
- TDS (Total Debt Service): the share of gross income required for
  housing costs plus all other debt commitments.

Factors that go into the calculation include:

- Principal and interest payments on the mortgage loan.
- Primary and other sources of income.
- Heating costs.
- Property taxes.
- Condominium or strata fees.
- Payments for all other credit facilities (unsecured personal loans,
  second mortgage loans, credit cards, lines of credit, and so on).

Notes:

- For insured mortgages, CMHC (and the private insurers) define GDS / TDS
  and set maximum limits. Lenders meet the insurer's limits to keep the
  insurance.
- GDS / TDS must be calculated conservatively and stressed for varied
  financial and economic conditions or higher interest rates.
- For uninsured mortgages, the lender contemplates current and future
  conditions and makes appropriate judgments.

### Qualifying rate (the stress test)

- For all uninsured residential mortgages, the qualifying rate is the
  greater of:
  - the contract mortgage rate plus a buffer set by the Superintendent,
    or
  - the minimum (floor) rate set by the Superintendent.
- The buffer and the floor are set by OSFI and can change over time. The
  assistant should never quote a specific stress-test rate from memory;
  the current Superintendent's floor and buffer live in the
  jurisdiction's rules config.
- The qualifying rate is what DSR / affordability is tested against, not
  the contract rate the borrower would actually pay.

### Amortization

- The lender must set a maximum amortization period in its underwriting
  policy.
- The average amortization in the portfolio should be less than that
  stated maximum.
- The exact maximum amortization for any given loan depends on whether
  the loan is insured or uninsured, and on jurisdiction-specific rules
  that have changed over time. Live values belong in the rules config,
  not in the corpus.

### Other capacity factors

The lender may also consider:

- The borrower's assets and liabilities (net worth).
- Other living expenses.
- Recurring payment obligations.
- Alternate sources of loan repayment.

## Principle 4: property collateral and LTV

### Valuation

- Mortgage loans rely on collateral as recourse if the borrower
  defaults, so the property assessment must be sound.
- The lender takes a risk-based approach combining valuation tools:
  on-site inspection, third-party appraisal, automated valuation tools.
- On-site inspection by a qualified employee or appraiser is generally
  expected; it also helps validate occupancy and condition.
- Third-party appraisers must be licensed and independent of the
  acquisition, processing, and decision process.
- Automated valuation tools require monitoring of effectiveness and
  controls on appropriate use.
- A single valuation method should not be relied upon. The lender
  should be able to critically review and challenge valuation
  assumptions.
- Higher-risk transactions (high LTV, illiquid properties, markets with
  rapid price increases) require more comprehensive and prudent
  collateral valuation.
- Realistic, substantiated, supportable valuations are expected; in
  rapidly-rising markets the lender should not assume prices will keep
  rising.

### LTV framework

- LTV (loan-to-value) is the ratio of the loan amount to the property
  value. It is highly correlated with credit risk: higher LTV files
  generally perform worse than lower LTV files.
- The lender sets maximum LTV limits for different loan types: insured
  loans, conventional mortgages, non-conforming mortgages, HELOCs.
- LTV must be recalculated on any refinancing, and whenever a borrower's
  risk profile or delinquency status changes.
- Lenders must not arrange (or appear to arrange) with another lender a
  structure that circumvents the LTV limit (e.g. bundling a mortgage
  with various priority interests so the combined LTV exceeds the
  lender's own limit).

### Down payment

- The lender should rigorously confirm whether the down payment comes
  from the borrower's own resources or savings.
- Gifted down payments must be accompanied by a letter from the gifter
  confirming no recourse.
- Non-traditional sources (e.g. borrowed funds) require additional risk
  mitigation.
- Cash-back and incentive / rebate payments do NOT count as down
  payment.

### Property-value adjustments for LTV

When computing LTV and setting lending thresholds, the lender should
adjust the property value as appropriate by considering:

- Location, type, and expected use of the property.
- Current market price, recent price trends, and housing market
  conditions.
- Any other risk that affects the sustainability of the property's
  value.

In markets with rapid house price increases, the lender should use more
conservative valuations and not assume prices will stay or keep rising.

### LTV thresholds by loan type

Insured / non-conventional ("high ratio"):

- High-ratio loans have LTV above 80%.
- By law, a residential mortgage underwritten for purchasing,
  renovating, or improving a property must be insured if LTV is above
  80%.

Conventional / low ratio:

- Conventional loans have LTV of 80% or less.
- Do not require mortgage insurance by law.

Non-conforming (a subset of conventional):

- Defined broadly as conventional loans with higher-risk attributes or
  deficiencies relative to other conventional mortgages.
- A lender's non-conforming definition should include any of:
  - Loans with insufficient income verification (do not meet Principle
    3).
  - Loans to borrowers with low credit scores.
  - Loans to borrowers with high debt serviceability ratios.
  - Loans with underlying property attributes that elevate credit risk
    (e.g. illiquid properties).
  - Loans that otherwise have clear deficiencies relative to other
    conforming mortgages.
- Maximum LTV for non-conforming residential mortgages is 65%.
- The 65% ceiling is not a "below this, due diligence stops" line; sound
  underwriting still applies.
- The maximum lending threshold for a non-conforming loan should
  decrease as transaction risk increases (multiple risk attributes,
  property valuation risk, and so on).

HELOCs:

- A HELOC is a non-amortizing (revolving) credit product secured by a
  residential property.
- HELOCs can build to greater outstanding debt over time and can mask
  borrower distress (a borrower can draw on the HELOC to make mortgage
  payments).
- Maximum authorized LTV for the non-amortizing HELOC component of a
  residential mortgage is 65%.
- The lender should review the HELOC's authorized amount if the
  property's value materially declines or the borrower's financial
  condition changes materially.

## Principle 5: credit and counterparty risk management

### Mortgage insurance

- Mortgage default insurance is a risk mitigation strategy but does not
  substitute for sound underwriting.
- Insurance can be obtained from CMHC or from private mortgage insurers.
- The lender must conduct due diligence on the mortgage insurer (claims
  payment record, expected future claims, balance sheet strength,
  funding, governance, reinsurance arrangements).
- For insured mortgages, the lender must meet the insurer's underwriting,
  valuation, and information requirements to keep the insurance valid.

### Purchase of mortgages from third parties

- A lender acquiring residential mortgages originated by a third party
  must ensure those underwriting standards are consistent with B-20 and
  its own underwriting policy.
- It is not enough to rely solely on the third party's attestation.

### Higher-risk asset portfolios

For portfolios with greater credit risk (e.g. non-conforming mortgages),
the lender should exercise heightened prudence: greater oversight,
increased monitoring, stronger internal controls, stronger default
management, and increased capital backing the portfolio risk.

## What this corpus file is not

- It is not a list of current rates, current OSFI floor, current
  qualifying buffer, or current amortization caps. Those values change
  and live in the prequalifier's rules config, not here.
- It does not list specific lenders or specific insurer requirements.
- It is not legal advice for any individual borrower.
- It is the framework B-20 sets, expressed so the assistant can refer to
  it accurately when a borrower asks why a lender wants what it wants,
  what a debt service ratio is, why LTV matters, what insured vs
  uninsured means, what non-conforming means, and so on.
