## Page 1: Insurance Broker Fundamentals
• An insurance broker helps clients explore insurance options from one or more insurance companies.
• Insurance brokers generally act as intermediaries between insurance consumers and insurance providers.
• Insurance brokers may assist individuals, families, and businesses.
• Insurance brokers typically gather information about a client’s situation and insurance needs.
• Insurance brokers may help explain policy options, coverage features, limitations, exclusions, and pricing.
• Insurance brokers do not generally create insurance policies themselves.
• Insurance policies are typically issued by insurance carriers or insurance companies.
• Insurance brokers may help clients compare multiple options.
• Insurance brokers may assist clients before, during, and after policy purchase.
• Insurance brokers often help clients review insurance needs over time.
• Insurance brokers may specialize in personal insurance, commercial insurance, life insurance, health insurance, or specialty insurance.
• Broker responsibilities can vary depending on jurisdiction, licensing requirements, and business structure.
• Insurance brokers generally seek to match client needs with available insurance products.
• Insurance brokers may assist with policy changes and updates.
• Insurance brokers may help clients understand renewal options.
• Insurance brokers often provide educational information but do not guarantee coverage outcomes.
• Coverage decisions are ultimately governed by policy wording and insurer approval.
• Insurance brokers may work independently or as part of a brokerage.
• Insurance brokers may represent access to multiple insurance carriers.
• Insurance brokers are typically required to maintain licensing requirements within their jurisdiction.


## Page 2: How Insurance Brokers Work
• Clients typically contact a broker seeking insurance information or quotes.
• Brokers gather relevant information from the client.
• Information may include personal, property, vehicle, business, or health-related details depending on the insurance type.
• Brokers submit information to one or more insurers.
• Insurers evaluate risk using underwriting guidelines.
• Underwriting criteria vary between insurers.
• Quotes may differ between insurers for similar coverage requests.
• Coverage options may vary between insurance companies.
• Brokers review available options with clients.
• Policy features and exclusions may differ between insurers.
• Premiums may vary based on underwriting results.
• Brokers may answer general questions regarding policy features.
• Brokers may assist with application completion.
• Insurance companies make final underwriting decisions.
• Brokers may help coordinate communication between client and insurer.
• Brokers may assist with renewals.
• Brokers may help identify coverage gaps.
• Brokers may assist with policy amendments.
• Brokers may assist during claims processes but typically do not make claims decisions.
• Claims decisions are generally made by the insurer.


## Page 3: Insurance Industry Terminology
• Premium: The amount paid for insurance coverage.
• Deductible: The amount paid by the policyholder before certain coverage applies.
• Policy: The insurance contract.
• Coverage Limit: The maximum amount payable under a covered claim.
• Claim: A request for benefits under an insurance policy.
• Beneficiary: A person designated to receive benefits.
• Liability: Legal responsibility for damages or injuries.
• Risk: The possibility of loss.
• Underwriting: The process insurers use to evaluate risk.
• Exclusion: A circumstance not covered by a policy.
• Endorsement: A modification to a policy.
• Renewal: Continuation of coverage after a policy period ends.
• Cancellation: Termination of a policy.
• Replacement Cost: Cost to replace covered property.
• Actual Cash Value: Value after depreciation is considered.
• Insured: Person or entity covered by the policy.
• Insurer: Company providing insurance coverage.
• Broker: Licensed intermediary assisting clients.
• Rider: Additional coverage attached to a policy.
• Waiting Period: Time before certain benefits become available.
